## When Silla Brought the Peninsula Together

In 668, Silla defeated Goguryeo and Baekje and brought the Korean peninsula under one rule. From then on, the kingdom is called **Unified Silla**!

In this age, many beautiful temples and pagodas were built. **Bulguksa Temple** and **Seokguram Grotto** in Gyeongju are world-famous cultural treasures still visited today. Inside Seokguram, finely carved stones form a great image of the Buddha.

A general named **Jang Bogo** built a sea base called Cheonghaejin and ran trade linking Silla, China, and Japan — a man who sailed to far lands to buy and sell.

The monk **Hyecho** traveled all the way to India on foot, visited Buddhist holy sites, and left a travelogue called *Wang Ocheonchukguk Jeon*. It was a remarkable journey for its time. 🛕

## Culture and Society of Unified Silla

Unified Silla (668–935) was the unified state Silla achieved by allying with Tang to topple Baekje (660) and Goguryeo (668), and then expelling Tang from the peninsula in the Silla–Tang War (670–676).

**Flowering Buddhist culture:** Bulguksa (completed under King Gyeongdeok in 751) and Seokguram represent the height of Silla Buddhist art. The Divine Bell of King Seongdeok (the "Emille Bell," 771) ranks as the largest temple bell in East Asia. Wonhyo (617–686), with his "Hwajaeng" (harmonization) thought, and Uisang (625–702), with his Hwaeom (Avatamsaka) thought, formed the twin pillars of Silla Buddhist philosophy.

**The Hwarang:** The Hwarang were a training corps of aristocratic youth, bound by the "Five Secular Injunctions" formulated by Wongwang. Heroes of unification such as Kim Yushin and Kim Chunchu were Hwarang.

**Maritime trade and Jang Bogo:** Jang Bogo (?–846) established Cheonghaejin on Wando Island, dominated the Yellow and South Seas, and built a trade network linking Silla, Tang, and Japan. Hyecho's *Wang Ocheonchukguk Jeon* (c. 727) is the oldest surviving Korean travelogue, recording India and Central Asia.

**Late Silla turmoil:** From the 9th century, royal succession struggles intensified and regional strongmen rose. Gyeon Hwon's Later Baekje (900) and Gungye's Later Goguryeo (901) appeared, ushering in the Later Three Kingdoms.

## Assessments and Disputes

**Legitimacy of unification and the question of territory:** Silla's unification rested on alliance with Tang, and after unification its territory was confined to the lands south of the Daedong River–Wonsan Bay line, losing Goguryeo's vast Manchurian domains. Choe Nam-seon read this as the "achievement of national unification," while Sin Chae-ho judged it a "subservient half-unification." Today scholarship holds these views in productive tension and aims at a balanced assessment.

**Structural contradictions of the Bone-Rank system:** Unified Silla society rested on the *golpum* system — Sacred Bone, True Bone, and the Head Ranks (6 down to 1). Sixth-Head-Rank intellectuals such as Choe Chiwon were blocked by status barriers despite their talents and sought careers in Tang. This rigidity became a structural cause of late-period social contradictions.

**Jang Bogo in historical perspective:** Jang Bogo, who appears even in the Japanese monk Ennin's *Nittō guhō junrei kōki*, was an international figure — not a mere maritime merchant but a political and economic power who held a key node of the East Asian trade network. His downfall is read as a case study in the struggle between the central aristocracy and regional powers.

**Architectural principles of Seokguram:** Seokguram is not a natural cave but an artificial dome built up of stone — a reflection of advanced Silla mathematical and architectural knowledge. Analyses showing that the position of the principal Buddha and the direction of incoming light precisely align with the spring and autumn equinox sunrises are read as evidence of astronomical learning.

> "Unified Silla was the completion of an ancient integration and, at the same time, a transitional age whose internal contradictions prepared the way to the medieval era." — Mainstream Korean historiography
