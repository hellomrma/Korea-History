## Liberation Came, but the Country Split in Two

On August 15, 1945, Japan finally lost the war. Korea was freed from 35 years of colonial rule and celebrated **Liberation (Gwangbok)**! The whole country filled with joy.

Right after liberation, however, the United States and the Soviet Union agreed to divide and occupy the peninsula. Soviet forces took the north (above the 38th parallel); American forces took the south (below it). This dividing line is the **38th parallel**.

In the north, **Kim Il-sung**'s faction took shape under Soviet sponsorship. In the south, **Syngman Rhee** and others built their political base under American influence. Rifts between left and right made it impossible to form a single unified government.

In 1948, the **Republic of Korea** was established in the south and the **Democratic People's Republic of Korea** in the north. The single country split in two — and the era of division began.

## Liberation and the Path to Division

The Liberation–Division period (1945–1950) was the process from liberation from Japanese rule to the founding of separate governments in the south and the north.

**Liberation, August 15:** With the U.S. atomic bombings (Hiroshima on Aug. 6 and Nagasaki on Aug. 9) and the Soviet declaration of war on Japan (Aug. 8), Japan announced unconditional surrender on Aug. 15, ending colonial rule.

**The 38th parallel and Allied military governments:** To disarm Japanese forces, the U.S. and the USSR set the 38th parallel as a temporary line. The U.S. military government ruled the south (1945–1948); a Soviet military government ruled the north. The U.S. military government's partial retention of colonial-era structures itself caused confusion.

**Left–right confrontation:** Immediately after liberation, Yeo Un-hyeong organized the Committee for the Preparation of Korean Independence, but the right wing (around Syngman Rhee) and the left (around Pak Hon-yong) clashed sharply. The trusteeship plan adopted at the December 1945 Moscow Conference of Foreign Ministers pushed left–right conflict to a peak.

**Founding of separate governments:** When the U.S.–Soviet Joint Commission collapsed in 1947, the Korean issue passed to the United Nations. After the USSR refused UN observers entry to the north, the UN resolved to hold elections in regions where they were possible. The South-only election of May 10, 1948 led to the founding of the Republic of Korea on August 15 and the DPRK on September 9.

## Interpreting the Liberation Years

**Where lies the responsibility for division?** Interpretations diverge sharply by the answer. The traditionalist view emphasizes the Cold War structure of Soviet/North Korean expansionism and U.S. containment. Revisionists like Bruce Cumings argue that U.S. support for the right and the rehabilitation of Japanese collaborators deepened the division. The post-revisionist view integrates both, while also weighing the autonomy and choices of internal Korean actors.

**The Committee and the People's Republic:** Yeo Un-hyeong's Committee for the Preparation of Korean Independence and the Korean People's Republic (Sept. 1945) were spontaneous political experiments in the liberation space. But the U.S. military government refused to recognize them, and clashes with right-wing efforts to install the Provisional Government planted the seeds of left–right conflict.

**The Jeju 4·3 incident (1948):** What began as an uprising against South-only elections became, under heavy military and police suppression, a tragedy in which tens of thousands of Jeju residents perished. Since the 2000s, official state investigation and apology have followed, but the political sensitivity of historical assessment remains.

**Distortion in the trusteeship debate:** Around the trusteeship clause of the Moscow Agreement, the right wing rallied as "anti-trusteeship" and the left as "pro-trusteeship." Recent research has shown that at the time the Soviet Union was reluctant about trusteeship while the United States was more enthusiastic — calling into question the conventional formula that the USSR led the trusteeship.

**Structuring division:** The five years of the liberation space were a process in which external intervention and internal ideological conflict combined to harden division from a "temporary" condition into a "structured" reality — and the precondition for the Korean War.
