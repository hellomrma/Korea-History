## The Korean Empire — A Land of an Emperor

In 1897, **King Gojong** renamed the country the **Korean Empire (Daehan Jeguk)** and proclaimed himself **emperor**. Becoming an emperor — a rank higher than a king — was a way of saying, "Our country is an independent state, no lower than China."

Emperor Gojong introduced many new things to make the country stronger. He brought in streetcars, telephones, and electricity, and built new schools and hospitals. These efforts are called the **Gwangmu Reform**.

But neighboring Japan kept pressing on Korea. In 1905, Japan forced the **Eulsa Treaty**, which stripped Korea of its diplomatic rights. Gojong protested, and in 1907 he secretly sent envoys to an international conference in **The Hague**, in the Netherlands.

In the end, in 1910, Japan annexed the Korean Empire entirely. This sad event is called **Gyeongsul Gukchi** (the National Disgrace of 1910). Yet the name "Daehan" lived on — today's **Republic of Korea (Daehan Minguk)** continues that legacy.

## The Rise and Fall of the Korean Empire

The Korean Empire (1897–1910) was an absolute monarchy proclaimed by Joseon to assert its independence amid the rivalry of Japan, Qing China, and Russia.

**Founding context:** Shaken by the Eulmi Incident of 1895 (the assassination of Empress Myeongseong), Gojong took refuge in the Russian legation in 1896 (the *Agwan Pacheon*). A year later he returned to the palace and, in 1897, ascended the throne as emperor at the Hwangudan altar, declaring the national title "Daehan Jeguk" and the era name "Gwangmu."

**The Gwangmu Reform:** A modernization program based on the principle of "old as foundation, new as reference."
- Land surveys and the issuance of *jigye* (land-ownership certificates)
- Industrial promotion: textiles, paper, glass
- Military reform: establishment of the Marshal's Headquarters and a military academy

**The Eulsa Treaty (1905):** After winning the Russo-Japanese War, Japan coerced Korea into a treaty that stripped it of its diplomatic rights and set up a Resident-General. Gojong refused to sign; the "Five Eulsa Traitors," including Yi Wan-yong, signed it instead.

**The Hague mission and forced abdication:** In 1907, Gojong dispatched Yi Jun, Yi Sang-seol, and Yi Wi-jong to the Hague Peace Conference; Japan blocked their participation. Using this as a pretext, Japan forced Gojong to abdicate.

**National Disgrace (29 August 1910):** Under Emperor Sunjong, the Japan–Korea Annexation Treaty ended the Korean Empire.

## Historical Assessments and Debates

**The Gwangmu Reform — debate over its character:** Assessments split in two. Positive views read the land surveys, *jigye* certificates, and palace-led industrial promotion as a genuine attempt at autonomous modernization. Critical views frame it as a top-down, conservative modernization centered on imperial power — at odds with the constitutional-monarchy aspirations of the Independence Club, prioritizing autocratic authority over civil rights.

**Legal validity of the Eulsa Treaty:** International-law scholars have argued the treaty is null from inception, signed under coercion and without the emperor's ratification. Professor Yi Tae-jin and others have argued that the lack of the emperor's signature on the treaty document renders the treaty itself void — a debate tied to the broader question of the legality of colonial rule.

**The Hague mission in international-law terms:** The dispatch of Yi Jun and his colleagues, by appealing to global opinion, can be read as a forerunner of modern international human-rights advocacy. Yet the international order of the time rested on great-power balance, and the United States, through the Taft–Katsura agreement of 1905, in effect acquiesced to Japan's domination of Korea.

**The "Daehan" inheritance:** After the March 1st Movement of 1919, the Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea inherited the empire's title and adopted "Daehan Minguk." This was an act of legal-line succession; the preamble of today's Korean constitution likewise affirms succession from the Provisional Government.
