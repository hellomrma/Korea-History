## A Tragic War Between One People

In the early hours of June 25, 1950, North Korean forces suddenly crossed the 38th parallel and attacked the South. This was the **Korean War**.

Within days Seoul fell. Many families were separated as people fled south.

The United States and other UN countries sent troops to help. In September 1950, General **MacArthur** carried out a landing at Incheon and dealt a major blow to the North Korean army — the **Incheon Landing**.

Then China entered the war on the side of the North. The fighting surged back and forth. In January 1951, Seoul was retaken once more (the **January 4 Retreat**).

After three long years, on July 27, 1953, an **armistice agreement** halted the fighting. There was no winner, no loser — just a war that stopped. The division of the peninsula has continued to this day. 😔

## The Course and Aftermath of the Korean War

The Korean War (June 25, 1950 – July 27, 1953) began with North Korea's invasion and lasted three years of total war. It cemented the division of the peninsula and remains the nation's greatest tragedy.

**Outbreak:** At 4 a.m. on June 25, 1950, North Korean forces launched a surprise attack across the 38th parallel. Seoul fell within three days, and by August UN/ROK forces had been pushed back to the Pusan Perimeter along the Nakdong River, anchored on Daegu and Busan.

**Reversal:** On September 15, the Incheon Landing under General MacArthur turned the tide. Seoul was retaken on September 28, and in October UN forces captured Pyongyang.

**Chinese intervention and the January 4 Retreat:** From late October, the Chinese People's Volunteer Army intervened in force, pushing UN troops back. Seoul fell again on January 4, 1951, with forces withdrawing south of the Han River. The front then stabilized near the 38th parallel.

**Casualties and armistice:** Truce talks began in July 1951, but the prisoner-repatriation issue and others drew them out for more than two years. The armistice was signed on July 27, 1953. Total casualties on both sides exceeded five million; the issue of separated families persists to this day.

## Historical Interpretation and International Context

**Debate over origins and responsibility:** Three perspectives coexist on the war's origins: the traditionalist view (North Korea/Soviet-led aggression), the revisionist view (U.S. provocation or inducement), and the post-revisionist view (combining civil-war elements with international Cold War dynamics). Soviet documents declassified after 1991 clearly show Kim Il-sung planning the invasion with Stalin's approval — strong evidence supporting the traditionalist view.

**The "Forgotten War" paradox:** Compared with Vietnam, the Korean War received little attention in the U.S. — sometimes dubbed "The Forgotten War." Yet it was the turning point that extended American Cold War strategy from a Europe focus to Asia and triggered the large military buildup envisioned in NSC-68.

**Meaning of Chinese intervention:** China's entry was the new People's Republic's debut as an international actor confronting the United States, contributing to its later status as a regional power. It also strengthened the U.S. commitment to defend Taiwan and entrenched the East Asian Cold War order.

**Civilian casualties and intra-national violence:** Massacres of civilians by both sides — including the Bodo League killings (1950) and the No Gun Ri massacre — show that the war was not a simple interstate conflict but a complex eruption of ideological and national fissures.

**Legal status of the armistice:** The armistice is not a peace treaty but a halt to hostilities, so legally the state of war has not ended. This continues to shape military tensions on the peninsula, the legal basis of U.S. troop deployments, and the framework for North Korea's nuclear issue 70 years on.
