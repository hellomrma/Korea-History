## When Three Kingdoms Contested the Peninsula

About 1,700 years ago, three powerful kingdoms ruled the Korean peninsula: **Goguryeo**, **Baekje**, and **Silla**! The long age of their rivalry is called the **Three Kingdoms period**.

**Goguryeo** held the broad lands of the north. Under King Gwanggaeto its territory expanded greatly.

**Baekje** stood near the western sea. Its culture and arts flourished, and it transmitted Buddhism and culture to Japan.

**Silla** had the smallest territory at first but grew steadily and finally unified the three kingdoms.

Across all three lands, people came to embrace **Buddhism** and built many temples and pagodas. The Three Kingdoms period was when Korea's ancient culture flowered. 🏯

## The Course of the Three Kingdoms and Unification

The Three Kingdoms period (4th century – 668 CE) was the age in which Goguryeo, Baekje, and Silla contested across the Korean peninsula and Manchuria.

**Goguryeo at its peak:** King Gwanggaeto (r. 391–412) expanded north into Manchuria and south to the Han River basin. His son, King Jangsu, moved the capital to Pyongyang (427) and pushed south to seize the Han River region. In the 6th century Goguryeo repulsed successive massive invasions by Sui and Tang and held its place as an East Asian great power.

**Baekje's culture and diplomacy:** Baekje exchanged actively with the Southern Dynasties of China and served as a cultural bridge to Japan, transmitting Buddhism (in 384), Chinese characters, and Confucianism. Under King Seong, the capital moved to Sabi (Buyeo) in a bid for revival, but Seong fell at the Battle of Gwansanseong (554) against Silla.

**Silla's rise and unification:** Silla officially adopted Buddhism under King Beopheung (527). Under King Jinheung it secured the Han River basin and gained a foothold for direct contact with Tang. The Silla–Tang alliance destroyed Baekje in 660 and Goguryeo in 668, completing unification.

**Gaya:** The Gaya confederacy along the Nakdong River — first centered on Geumgwan Gaya, then Daegaya — was incorporated into Silla in 562.

## Interpretation and Disputes

**When did the Three Kingdoms become "states"?** The *Samguk Sagi* dates the founding of Goguryeo, Baekje, and Silla to 37 BCE, 18 BCE, and 57 BCE respectively, but archaeological and source-critical research places their completion as centralized ancient states after the 4th century CE. The retrojection of founding dates is also a question of dynastic historiography aimed at securing legitimacy.

**The Silla–Tang War and the character of "unification":** Silla's unification rested on Tang military support, and the resulting territory shrank to lands south of the Daedong River — leading to criticism that this was only a "half unification." Conversely, Silla's repulse of Tang's ambitions for direct rule over the peninsula in the Silla–Tang War (670–676) is praised in the register of national independence.

**International context of Goguryeo's wars with Sui and Tang:** Sui Yangdi's failed expedition against Goguryeo in 612 was a key cause of Sui's collapse, and Tang Taizong likewise failed at the Battle of Ansiseong in 645. These episodes reveal Goguryeo's strategic standing in 7th-century East Asia.

**Political role of Buddhism:** Adopting Buddhism was not merely religious; it was an ideological tool to strengthen royal authority and integrate aristocratic factions. Wongwang's "Five Secular Injunctions" and Wonhyo's "Hwajaeng" (harmonization) thought were intellectual searches for unifying a society fractured by war.

**Re-evaluating Gaya:** Long treated as peripheral, Gaya is now reassessed through archaeology as a sophisticated polity with autonomous iron production and maritime trade networks. The 2023 inscription of the Gaya Tumuli on the UNESCO World Heritage list reflects this revaluation.
