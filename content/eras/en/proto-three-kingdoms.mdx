## What Came Before the Three Kingdoms?

About 2,000 years ago, many small countries lived across the Korean peninsula and its surroundings.

To the north was a kingdom called **Buyeo**. Its people were skilled farmers, and in December they held a great festival called **Yeonggo**. Together they sang, danced, and gave thanks to heaven!

In the south of the peninsula were three "Han" countries. The **Mahan** in the west, the **Jinhan** in the east, and the **Byeonhan** in the south were collectively called the **Samhan**.

People of this era began using tools and weapons made of **iron**. Iron farming tools made farming much easier. 🌾

Later, the Samhan grew into Baekje, Silla, and Gaya. Small villages joined together to become big kingdoms!

## Polities and Society of the Proto-Three Kingdoms Period

The Proto-Three Kingdoms period (1st century BCE – 3rd century CE) lies between the fall of Gojoseon and the formal emergence of the Three Kingdoms — a time when many polities coexisted across the peninsula.

**Buyeo:** Centered in Manchuria's Songhua River basin, Buyeo took the form of a five-tribe confederation. It held the *Yeonggo* sky-worship rite in December, raised cattle, horses, pigs, and dogs, and combined agriculture with herding. The ruling lines of both Goguryeo and Baekje are said to have descended from Buyeo.

**The Three Han (Samhan):** In central and southern Korea, 78 small statelets aligned as Mahan (54), Jinhan (12), and Byeonhan (12). Each was led by a chief; sacred precincts called *sodo* lay outside the chief's authority.

**Okjeo and Dongye:** Located in the northeast, both Okjeo and Dongye were subordinate to Goguryeo and paid tribute, yet preserved distinct customs. Okjeo's *minmyeoneuri* (child-bride) practice and Dongye's *chaekhwa* (penalties for transgression) are well known.

**Spread of iron culture:** Iron farming tools and weapons raised agricultural productivity and intensified competition among polities. The Byeonhan region was famed for iron production and exported iron to Lelang and to Wa (Japan).

The statelets gradually consolidated: Mahan → Baekje, Jinhan → Silla, Byeonhan → Gaya.

## Reconstruction and Scholarly Debates

**Origin of the term "Proto-Three Kingdoms":** The term was proposed by archaeologist Kim Won-yong in the 1970s; in textual history the period is still often called "early Three Kingdoms" or the "Samhan period." This shows that history and archaeology do not periodize identically.

**Limits of the sources:** The principal textual source is the "Account of the Eastern Barbarians" in the *Wei Shu* of the *Sanguozhi* (Records of the Three Kingdoms). It is, however, an external record written from a Chinese viewpoint and may carry political intent and misunderstanding.

**Sodo and dual power:** In Samhan society the *sodo*, ruled by a priestly figure (*cheongun*), was a sacred precinct outside the chief's secular authority. This indicates a society in the process of separating religious and secular power; some scholars analyze it through Max Weber's typology of charismatic authority.

**Buyeo as ancestral source:** Goguryeo's foundation myth depicts Jumong as Buyeo-born, and Baekje's tradition stresses that Onjo and Biryu were of Buyeo lineage. Later dynasties drew on Buyeo as a source of legitimacy — a politically constructed memory of succession.

**Iron culture and social differentiation:** Large-scale iron production in Byeonhan was not merely economic; it was a driver of social change — building trade networks, deepening class differentiation, and strengthening military capacity. Iron artifacts from the Haman and Gimhae tombs support this archaeologically.

> "The Proto-Three Kingdoms period is a transition toward a new political order after the fall of Gojoseon. Without understanding this period, the dynamics behind the formation of the Three Kingdoms cannot be grasped." — Mainstream view in Korean ancient-history scholarship
