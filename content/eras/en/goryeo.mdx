## Goryeo — The Root of "Korea"

In 918 CE, a general named **Wang Geon** founded a new dynasty. Its name was **Goryeo**. The English word "Korea" comes from this very name!

Goryeo produced many remarkable cultural treasures. To restore Buddhist scriptures destroyed in war, artisans carved more than 80,000 wooden printing blocks over sixteen years. This is the **Tripitaka Koreana**. It is still preserved at Haeinsa Temple today!

The people of Goryeo were the first in the world to invent **metal movable type**. Cast in iron, the type made it possible to print books — 200 years before Gutenberg in Europe!

Goryeo was also famed for **Goryeo celadon** — clear, beautifully green-glazed porcelain that the whole world admired. 🏺

## The Founding and Culture of Goryeo

Goryeo (918–1392) was founded by Wang Geon, who overthrew Gungye of Later Goguryeo. By absorbing Silla (935) and Later Baekje (936), he unified the Later Three Kingdoms.

**Wang Geon's founding vision:** To bind together the powerful regional clans, Wang Geon used marriage diplomacy and left the *Ten Injunctions*, emphasizing succession from Goguryeo, devotion to Buddhism, and respect for the Western Capital (Pyongyang).

**Cultural achievements:**
- **Tripitaka Koreana (Jaejo Daejanggyeong):** Some 80,000 wooden blocks carved between 1236 and 1251 to invoke the power of the Buddha for the country during the Mongol invasions. Preserved today at Haeinsa, a UNESCO Memory of the World heritage.
- **Metal movable type:** Records of *Sangjeong Gogeum Yemun* printed by metal type around 1234 place Goryeo's invention some 200 years before Gutenberg's (c. 1450).
- **Goryeo celadon:** The "kingfisher" celadon was praised even in Song China as "first under heaven."

**Military Coup of 1170:** Reacting to civilian officials' discrimination against the military, Jeong Jung-bu and others staged a coup, ushering in roughly a century of military rule.

**Mongol invasions and resistance:** Seven Mongol invasions from 1231 devastated the land, yet the resistance of the Sambyeolcho (1270–1273) and the carving of the Tripitaka Koreana expressed the people's defiance.

## Goryeo's Historical Character and Scholarly Debates

**Was Goryeo "medieval"?** Korean historiography has long debated whether Goryeo was a feudal medieval society or a transitional phase from antiquity to the medieval era. The hereditary appointment system (*eumseo*) and the *gongeumjeon* (merit-prebend land) institutionalized aristocratic privilege — read as feudal elements — while the civil-service exam and the *Jeonsigwa* land system are taken as bureaucratic, centralizing features.

**The metal-type primacy debate:** Goryeo's priority in metal movable type is broadly accepted, but the oldest extant printed example is the *Jikji Simche Yojeol* (1377). Evidence for the 1234 printing rests on a literary record by Yi Gyu-bo; physical confirmation remains an open task.

**Duality of the Yuan-Mongol period:** The era of Mongol overlordship (1270–1356) was politically subordinating yet also a window onto Eurasian exchange. Cotton's introduction by Mun Ikjeom (1363), the adoption of gunpowder, and the cultural fusion of *Goryeo-yang* and Mongol fashions are products of this period.

**Meaning of the Tripitaka Koreana:** The Tripitaka was a religious and cultural achievement and also a wartime mobilization project. Some analyses note that the Choe military regime, after relocating to Ganghwa Island, used the project to bolster its authority and unite popular sentiment.

**The new gentry-scholars and Goryeo's end:** The rise of new Neo-Confucian scholars like Jeong Dojeon and Jeong Mongju in the late 14th century was a movement toward an ideal Confucian state. Their alliance with Yi Seong-gye (the Wihwa-do Retreat of 1388) — a fusion of military force and reformist ideology — became the engine of Joseon's founding.
