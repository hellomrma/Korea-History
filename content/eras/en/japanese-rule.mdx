## People Who Fought to Reclaim Their Country

In 1910, Japan annexed Korea. The 35 years from 1910 to 1945 are known as the **Japanese Colonial Period**.

Japan banned the use of Korean, forced Koreans to take Japanese-style names, and stopped teaching Korean history. It was a very hard, painful time.

But our ancestors never gave up. On March 1, 1919, people across the country poured into the streets shouting "Long live Korean independence!" This was the **March 1st Movement**.

**Yu Gwan-sun** led an independence rally at the Aunae market in her hometown of Cheonan, was arrested by Japanese police, and died in prison.

**Ahn Jung-geun** struck down Itō Hirobumi, the leading architect of Japan's takeover of Korea.

In Shanghai, independence activists came together to form the **Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea**. Thanks to those who fought to the very end, liberation finally came in 1945. 🇰🇷

## Colonial Rule and the Independence Movement

The Japanese colonial period (1910–1945) lasted 35 years, from the loss of sovereignty under the Annexation Treaty until liberation.

**Structure of colonial rule:** The Japanese Government-General of Korea consolidated legislative, executive, judicial, and military authority in the Governor-General. The 1910s "military rule" period imposed gendarme-police governance. After the March 1st Movement, the regime adopted so-called "cultural rule," but the underlying exploitation continued.

**The March 1st Movement (1919):** Triggered by the death of Emperor Gojong, this was a nationwide nonviolent independence movement. Beginning with the reading of the Declaration of Independence by 33 national representatives on March 1st, more than two million people took part across the country. It was the immediate cause of the founding of the Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea in Shanghai (April 1919).

**Diverse forms of resistance:**
- Armed struggle: Hong Beom-do at the Battle of Bongo-dong (1920); Kim Jwa-jin at the Battle of Cheongsanri (1920)
- Targeted action: Ahn Jung-geun's act (1909); Yun Bong-gil's act (1932)
- Diplomatic and cultural movements: the Provisional Government's diplomacy, the Korean Production Movement, and the founding of the Singanhoe

**Erasure policies:** From the 1930s, Japan attempted to erase Korean identity through forced name-changes, the abolition of Korean-language education, and compulsory shrine worship. Young people were conscripted as labor and soldiers, and women were forced into the Japanese military "comfort women" system.

**Liberation (15 August 1945):** With the U.S. atomic bombings and the Soviet declaration of war on Japan, Japan surrendered, and Korea's 35 years of colonization ended.

## Interpretations and Disputes

**The "colonial modernization" debate:** Some scholars (An Byeong-jik, Yi Yeong-hun and others) approach the period through the lens of "colonial modernization," pointing to the construction of railways, roads, and modern financial institutions. Mainstream nationalist historiography responds that this modernization was a tool of exploitation and aggression, and that Koreans' economic position in fact worsened. The debate is both methodological and politically charged in the present.

**Significance of the March 1st Movement:** The March 1st Movement is read not as a mere petition for independence but as the starting point of a modern national movement in which "the people" emerged as a subject of history. Its nonviolent character invites comparison with Gandhi's civil disobedience, and its founding of the Provisional Government anchored a republican vision of the state.

**Legitimacy of the Provisional Government:** Founded in 1919, the Provisional Government faced internal conflicts (the Syngman Rhee–Yi Tong-hwi rivalry) and chronic financial shortages, limiting its actual state functions. Yet its 1944 preparations for the Korean Liberation Army's domestic offensive and its role in the Allied war against Japan carry symbolic weight as a marker of independent sovereignty.

**The "comfort women" issue:** The exploitation of "comfort women" by the Japanese military is a wartime sexual-violence crime under international law; international human-rights bodies and the UN have affirmed Japan's legal responsibility. The 2015 Korea–Japan agreement on the issue has been criticized at home and abroad for excluding the victims and for using "final and irreversible resolution" language.

**The politics of memory:** Differences in historical perception between Korea and Japan persist over textbooks, the Dokdo islets, and the Yasukuni shrine — not as past-history issues alone but as a "politics of memory" tied to contemporary diplomacy, politics, and identity.
