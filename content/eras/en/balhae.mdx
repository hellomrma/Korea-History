## Balhae — Heir to Goguryeo

About 30 years after the fall of Goguryeo, in 698 CE, a general named **Dae Joyeong** founded a new kingdom. Its name was **Balhae**!

Balhae rose on the lands of former Goguryeo — across Manchuria and the Maritime Province. Dae Joyeong led a coalition of Goguryeo refugees and Mohe people, defeated the Tang army, and founded the new state.

Balhae lasted more than 200 years and became a powerful kingdom. At its peak, people of Tang China called Balhae **Haedong Seongguk** — "the Prosperous Land East of the Sea."

Balhae governed its territory through five major cities (the Five Capitals) and fifteen administrative regions (the Fifteen Provinces).

In 926 CE, Balhae fell to the powerful Khitan and disappeared from history. Many of its people later migrated south and joined the new kingdom of Goryeo.

## The Founding and Growth of Balhae

Balhae (698–926) was founded by Dae Joyeong, a former Goguryeo general, who united Goguryeo refugees and Mohe forces around Mount Dongmo in Jilin.

**Background:** After Goguryeo fell in 668, the Tang dynasty forcibly relocated many Goguryeo refugees to the Chinese heartland. When the Khitan rose in revolt against Tang in 696, Dae Joyeong seized the chance, moved east, defeated Tang troops at the Battle of Tianmen Pass, and founded Balhae.

**Growth and zenith:** The third king, Mun, moved the capital to Sanggyeong Yongcheonbu and modeled the central government on Tang's Three Departments and Six Ministries. Under King Seon (r. 818–830) the realm reached its largest extent — Five Capitals, fifteen prefectures, sixty-two counties — and Tang itself praised Balhae as the "Prosperous Land East of the Sea."

**Culture and diplomacy:** Balhae exchanged envoys with Tang, Silla, and Japan, absorbing advanced Tang institutions. Records mention Balhae missions to Japan more than thirty times. Buddhism flourished — Sanggyeong has yielded many temple sites and Buddha statues.

**Fall:** In 926, a sudden assault by Yelü Abaoji of the Khitan toppled Balhae in just a few weeks. Many royals and aristocrats fled to Goryeo, where King Taejo welcomed them as "kin" of his own people.

## Historical Identity and Scholarly Debate

**Whose history is Balhae?** Korea, China, and Russia all claim Balhae as part of their national past. China's "Northeast Project" frames Balhae as a Tang regional regime ruled by the Mohe. Korean scholarship counters that Balhae's ruling class were Goguryeo refugees and that Balhae's continuity with Goryeo places it within Korean history.

**Ethnic composition and identity:** While the ruling elite were Goguryeo refugees, much of the populace was Mohe. Some scholars therefore caution against assigning Balhae a single "ethnic" identity. Japanese historians such as Shinoda Jisaku have emphasized its Mohe character.

**Consciousness of Goguryeo succession:** In a state letter sent to Japan, King Mun referred to himself as "King of Goryeo (Goguryeo)." This is an important primary source showing that Balhae's rulers publicly framed themselves as successors to Goguryeo.

**Why the rapid fall?** Many historians attribute Balhae's collapse within weeks of the Khitan attack to a fragile centralized structure and weak cohesion between the Goguryeo-descended elite and the Mohe majority.

**Continuity with Goryeo:** Goryeo's founder, King Taejo Wang Geon, embraced Balhae refugees, declaring "Balhae was originally one household with us," and welcomed tens of thousands — including Crown Prince Dae Gwang-hyeon. This is critical evidence that Goryeo recognized Balhae as part of the same people.
