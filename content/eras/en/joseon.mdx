## The Kingdom of Joseon

In 1392, the general Yi Seong-gye founded a new dynasty. Its name was **Joseon**!

Joseon adopted **Confucianism** as the foundation of state and society. Confucianism prized respect for elders, loyalty to the state, and dedication to learning.

The most famous Joseon king is **King Sejong the Great**. Pained that ordinary people could not read, he created **Hangeul (Hunminjeongeum)** in 1443. Thanks to him, we can write in Hangeul today!

## Founding and Governance of Joseon

Joseon (1392–1897) was the Confucian dynasty founded by Yi Seong-gye after he seized power in late Goryeo through the Wihwa-do Retreat (1388). The capital was moved from Gaegyeong to Hanyang (modern Seoul).

**Governance:** Below the king, the State Council and the Six Ministries formed a bureaucratic order. The civil-service exam (*gwageo*) recruited capable officials, and following Confucian ideology civilian officials were favored over military officers (rule by civil officials, *munchijuui*).

**Achievements of King Sejong:**
- Creation of *Hunminjeongeum* (Hangeul, 1443)
- Scientific instruments such as the rain gauge and the *Angbu Ilgu* (sundial)
- Northward expansion through the Four Forts and Six Garrisons

**The Imjin War (1592–1598):** Toyotomi Hideyoshi's invasion devastated the country, but Admiral Yi Sun-sin's naval victories and the campaigns of "righteous armies" eventually drove the Japanese forces back.

## Political Structure and Historical Assessment

**Sarim politics and factionalism:** From mid-Joseon onward, the rivalry between the Hungu and Sarim camps and the later split between the Easterners and Westerners drove factional politics, which became the core dynamic of Joseon political life.

**Significance of the Wihwa-do Retreat:** Yi Seong-gye's retreat at Wihwa-do (1388) was not merely a military event but a turning point in regime change, fused with the rise of late-Goryeo neo-Confucian gentry-scholars.

**International context of the Imjin War:** The Imjin War was an international conflict among Joseon, Ming, and Japan. Ming's intervention served not only to rescue Joseon but also to safeguard its own security, and the war reshaped the East Asian international order.

**Late-Joseon Silhak:** In the 18th and 19th centuries, Silhak ("practical learning") scholars — Yi Ik, Jeong Yak-yong, Park Ji-won — criticized the abstractions of Neo-Confucianism and called for pragmatic reform.
