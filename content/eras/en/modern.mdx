## How Today's Korea Was Made

When the war ended in 1953, the Republic of Korea stood in ruins. But our people did not give up.

From the 1960s, everyone worked hard — building factories, paving expressways, exporting ships and cars. This rapid economic growth is called the **Miracle on the Han River**. The world was amazed!

While the economy grew, citizens' freedoms were sometimes suppressed. In the **April 19 Revolution** of 1960, students rose against dictatorship. In the **May 18 Gwangju Democratization Movement** of 1980, citizens stood against military rule. Many gave their lives for democracy.

In 1987, the people won the right to elect their president directly. Democracy had arrived!

Today, Korea creates culture loved around the world — **K-pop, K-dramas, K-food**. A truly proud country! 🎵

## Development and Democratization in Modern Korea

Modern Korea (1953–present) traveled a dynamic road from the ruins of war to a top-ten global economy and a robust democracy.

**Miracle on the Han:** From the 1960s through the 1980s, an export-led, state-driven growth strategy delivered annual growth of more than 10% on average. POSCO (1968), the Gyeongbu Expressway (1970), and the heavy and chemical industries program built an industrial base, and conglomerates such as Samsung, Hyundai, and LG rose to global scale.

**The democratization journey:**
- **April 19 Revolution (1960):** Students and citizens rose against the rigged March 15 election; Syngman Rhee resigned.
- **May 16 military coup (1961):** Park Chung-hee seized power, beginning 18 years of authoritarian rule.
- **May 18 Gwangju Democratization Movement (1980):** Citizens of Gwangju resisted Chun Doo-hwan's "New Military" coup; many died in the army's violent suppression.
- **June Democratic Uprising (1987):** Nationwide protests forced direct presidential elections (the June 29 Declaration).

**After democratization:** Korea overcame the 1997 IMF financial crisis, co-hosted the 2002 FIFA World Cup with Japan, and deepened democracy through the candlelight revolution and presidential impeachment of 2016–17.

**Hallyu and K-Culture:** Since the 2000s, Korean dramas and films (Parasite, Minari), music (BTS, BLACKPINK), and food have spread worldwide as a symbol of "soft power."

## Historical Context and Scholarly Perspectives

**The developmental state and the Miracle on the Han:** Economic growth under Park Chung-hee is studied as a representative case of Chalmers Johnson's "developmental state" model. State control of markets and finance and concentrated cultivation of strategic industries became a touchstone for East Asian development models. Yet the imbalance of a chaebol-centered economy, the suppression of labor, and environmental damage were the underside of growth.

**Reassessing May 18:** The Gwangju Movement is the spiritual origin of Korea's democratization in the 1980s. UNESCO Memory of the World inscription in 2011 brought international recognition, and recent discussions place the "Gwangju spirit" within the solidarity of East Asian democratization movements. Responsibility for Chun Doo-hwan's coup and suppression — and the question of U.S. acquiescence — remain subjects of historical dispute.

**Democratic-transition perspectives:** South Korea's 1987 democratization is classified in comparative politics as a "pacted transition" (Juan Linz, Alfred Stepan). It was a negotiated transformation between regime and opposition rather than a revolution or collapse, though scholars note that the mass pressure of the June Uprising was decisive — pointing to limits of pure elite-pact theory.

**Structural analysis of Hallyu:** Hallyu is more than a cultural phenomenon. It is a composite of state cultural-industry policy (the 1998 Framework Act on Cultural Industry Promotion), digital platforms (YouTube, Spotify), and a post–Cold War, globalization-era search for an Asian cultural identity. K-pop's "idol system," shaped in part by visa rules and music-industry differences, is now studied as a distinctive industrial model.

**Candlelight revolution and deepening democracy:** The 2016–17 impeachment of President Park Geun-hye unfolded through nonviolent candlelight rallies that drew over ten million participants. As an example of citizens holding power to account through constitutional procedures, scholars abroad have looked to it as a new model of "deliberative democracy."
