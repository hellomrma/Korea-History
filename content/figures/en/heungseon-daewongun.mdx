## Who was Heungseon Daewongun?

Heungseon Daewongun was the father of King Gojong, the 26th king of Joseon. He was born in 1820.

When Gojong took the throne as a boy, his father served as regent and led the government. The Daewongun rebuilt Gyeongbokgung Palace, which had been destroyed in the Imjin War. He also drastically reduced the regional power of the *seowon* academies.

When Western ships appeared on Joseon's coasts, he pursued an isolationist policy of refusing trade. The aim was to prevent foreign incursion, but it is also judged to have missed the chance to adapt to changing international currents.

## Life and Achievements

Heungseon Daewongun Yi Ha-eung (1820–1898) was the father of King Gojong, regent from 1863 to 1873, and the architect of strong reformist politics.

**Breaking *sedo* politics, restoring royal power:** Royal authority had been hollowed out by *sedo* clan politics — the Andong Kim and Pungyang Jo clans. The Daewongun replaced royals and high officials with his own confidants and recruited talent on merit, restoring the throne's authority.

**Abolition of the *seowon*:** Of more than 1,000 *seowon* academies enjoying tax and corvée exemptions and undermining state finance, he forcibly closed about 600, sparing only 47 — replenishing revenue and curbing the *yangban*.

**Reconstruction of Gyeongbokgung:** He rebuilt Gyeongbokgung, burned in the Imjin War, to restore royal dignity. But to fund construction he minted the *Dangbaekjeon* coin, triggering inflation and losing popular support.

**Isolationism:** After repulsing the French (1866, the *Byeonginyangyo*) and the Americans (1871, the *Sinmiyangyo*), he erected stelae across the country reaffirming refusal of relations and trade with the West.

**Ouster:** In 1873 he was driven from the regency by the rising power of his daughter-in-law, Empress Myeongseong.

## Historical Assessment

The Daewongun embodies both the reformist and isolationist contradictions of late Joseon — a symbol of the limits of a regime in transition to the modern world.

**Character of the reforms:** Abolishing the *seowon* and breaking *sedo* clan politics can be read as oriented toward modern bureaucratization and royal centralization, but they were a restoration of feudal monarchy rather than a transformation of social structure. The general scholarly reading is that he aimed at a "strong dynasty," not a "modern state."

**The two faces of isolationism:** Repulsing the French and Americans was a military success, but it led the regime to underestimate the West's actual capacity, and is seen as a factor that lost the chance for opening. The *Seungjeongwon Ilgi* records both the immediate political effect of erecting the anti-Western stelae and the fact that some officials internally voiced the need for trade.

**Political economy of Gyeongbokgung:** The *Dangbaekjeon* coinage led to a collapse of currency value that hit ordinary lives directly, while forced "voluntary" contributions (*wonnapjeon*) and corvée mobilizations stoked resistance. The funding model exposes the structural limit of the dynasty: reform whose costs ultimately landed as exploitation of the people.

**Conflict with Empress Myeongseong:** His ouster in 1873 was not a mere domestic spat but a clash of policy lines — pro-Qing (sadae) tributary diplomacy versus internal self-strengthening. His attempted returns to power during the Imo Mutiny (1882) and the Gapsin Coup (1884) drew him further into the great-power chess game.
