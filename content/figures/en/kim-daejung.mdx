## Who was President Kim Dae-jung?

Kim Dae-jung lived an extraordinarily hard life, yet he never gave up.

Before becoming president he was kidnapped, imprisoned, and even sentenced to death. But Kim said, "I will never give up to the end" and fought for democracy.

After he became president, he extended a hand to North Korea. Saying, "Better to meet and understand than to fight," he pursued the **Sunshine Policy** and held the historic inter-Korean summit of 2000. For this work he was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize!

## Life and Achievements

Kim Dae-jung (1924–2009), born in Sinan in South Jeolla, was the 15th president of the Republic of Korea (1998–2003). The phrase "beyond death and beyond the darkness of his age" captures his dramatic life.

**Democratization struggle and persecution:** After narrowly losing the 1971 presidential election to Park Chung-hee, he was the most dangerous opposition leader under the Yushin regime and faced constant suppression. In 1973 he was kidnapped from Tokyo by KCIA agents and barely escaped death thanks to international pressure. In 1980 the New Military sentenced him to death for sedition; international opinion and U.S. pressure led to commutation.

**Presidency and achievements:** He took office at the end of 1997 amid the IMF financial crisis and overcame it early through restructuring and the gold-collecting campaign. He pursued the Sunshine Policy of engagement and held a historic summit with Kim Jong-il in June 2000, winning the Nobel Peace Prize the same year. Cultivation of IT laid the foundation for Korea as an internet powerhouse.

## Historical Assessment

**Complexity of a democracy icon:** Kim Dae-jung is internationally celebrated as a symbol of Korea's democratization. Domestically, however, assessments of his time in office are mixed. The tax investigation of media companies, allegations of abuse of power by his sons, and the late-term decline in approval ratings rubbed against an image built on moral authority.

**The Sunshine Policy debate:** The Sunshine Policy is one of the most contested topics in modern Korean foreign and security policy. Supporters credit it with real achievements: easing inter-Korean tensions, family reunions, and the Kaesong Industrial Complex. Critics argue that aid was diverted to nuclear development and that the 2000 summit was secured through cash transfers (the so-called "North Korean remittance scandal"), questioning the policy's sincerity and effectiveness. The debate has become a benchmark for North Korea policy at every change of government.

**Regionalism and historical significance:** Kim's political life cannot be understood apart from Korean regionalism — Honam vs Yeongnam. He represented the political voice of Honam, traditionally marginalized, and his election is read as a historic alignment of region, class, and ideology. The Nobel Peace Prize signaled international recognition of Korean democracy, while inviting some critique of Western-centric criteria for the prize.
