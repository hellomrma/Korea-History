## Who was King Onjo?

King Jumong, who founded Goguryeo, had several sons. Among them was Onjo. With his elder brother Biryu, Onjo went south to found a new kingdom. Biryu chose to settle by the sea, but Onjo settled at Wiryeseong, where rivers and mountains met. Biryu's land did not prosper, but Onjo's grew strong — and that kingdom was Baekje! As its first king, Onjo built solid foundations for the new state.

## Life and Achievements

King Onjo (c. 60 BCE – 28 CE) was the founder of Baekje, founding the kingdom at Wiryeseong (presumed to be in the southern part of modern Seoul) in 18 BCE. According to the *Samguk Sagi*, Onjo was a son of Goguryeo's founder Jumong; with his elder brother Biryu, he moved south, settling at Michuhol (the Incheon area) and Wiryeseong respectively. Michuhol was wet and saline and hard to live in, while Wiryeseong was fertile and prosperous. After Biryu's death, his people came over to Onjo and the kingdom grew larger.

During his reign, Onjo absorbed and subjugated Mahan polities along the Han River basin, expanding Baekje's domain. He repulsed frequent Mohe incursions and reorganized the military. He set up state altars and a founder's shrine to anchor royal ritual, and a shrine to King Dongmyeong to stress the bloodline tie to Goguryeo, raising the standing of the royal house.

Onjo's founding, anchored in the geographic advantage of the Han River basin, became the starting point of a Baekje civilization rich in agriculture and trade and laid the basis for Baekje culture in the Three Kingdoms era.

## Historical Assessment

Several scholarly questions concern the historical reality of King Onjo and the founding of Baekje. While the *Samguk Sagi* records him as a son of Jumong, the same work also records a variant view that he was the son of Utae — showing that the lineage of the founding ancestor itself is not single.

The location of the founding capital is also debated. The mainstream archaeological view places Wiryeseong in the Han basin around the Pungnaptoseong and Mongchontoseong sites in Seoul, but the Chungcheong-area hypothesis is not entirely excluded. Early Baekje pottery and structures from the 1st century BCE through the early 1st century CE excavated at Pungnaptoseong support the possibility of an Onjo-era capital.

Views also differ on the character of the founding group. The leading reading is that it combined a southward Buyeo–Goguryeo migrant group with indigenous Mahan polities of the Han basin. The Onjo group, with advanced iron-age culture and equestrian military power, gradually integrated the small Mahan states. Some analyses identify conflict and accommodation with Mokji-guk in Mahan as the core dynamic of early Baekje state formation.

The records of Onjo's reign (18 BCE – 28 CE) appear to have been compiled in retrospect; the consensus view is that the actual establishment of the Baekje state took shape in earnest under King Goi in the 3rd century. Onjo is therefore evaluated dually — as both a historical figure and a symbol that condenses the founding myth of the Baekje state.
