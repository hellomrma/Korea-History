## Who was Empress Myeongseong?

Empress Myeongseong was a queen of Joseon and an empress of the Korean Empire. She was born in 1851.

She was deeply involved in foreign policy. As Japanese influence grew, she pursued strengthening ties with other powers such as Russia.

In 1895, Japanese soldiers and *rōnin* entered Gyeongbokgung Palace and assassinated her — the **Eulmi Incident**. Foreign diplomats reported the killing internationally and condemned Japan's responsibility. Her story is still told today through musicals and dramas.

## Life and Achievements

Empress Myeongseong (1851–1895) was the queen of King Gojong and an active player on the turbulent political stage of late Joseon.

**Promotion of the Min clan:** When her father-in-law, Heungseon Daewongun, sought to check her politically, she placed her Min relatives in key posts and built her power base — a power conflict that continued with the Daewongun.

**Foreign policy:** After the Imo Mutiny (1882), she leaned on Qing as Qing's influence grew; following the Gapsin Coup (1884) and rising Japanese pressure, she shifted to closer ties with Russia. This was an "use-foreign-to-check-foreign" diplomacy aimed at containing Japan.

**The Eulmi Incident (1895):** Before dawn on October 8, 1895, on the orders of the Japanese minister Miura Gorō, Japanese *rōnin* and trained-corps soldiers stormed Gyeongbokgung, assassinated the queen, and burned her body. The killing shocked the world; foreign diplomats witnessed and recorded the event.

**Posthumous honors:** In 1897, with the founding of the Korean Empire, she was elevated as Empress Myeongseong and interred at Hongneung.

## Historical Assessment

Empress Myeongseong is among the most actively reassessed figures in Korean history since the late 20th century, with interpretations stretched between "sacrificial victim" and "political agent."

**The "victim" narrative and its limits:** The dramatic, tragic killing strengthened a narrative casting her as a victim of Japanese imperialism — the popular image fixed by the musical *Empress Myeongseong* (premiered 1995) is representative. The narrative draws criticism for portraying her only as a passive victim, simplifying her complex political agency in life.

**Reassessment as political agent:** Since the 1990s, advances in feminist historiography and modern history have brought to the fore the view that she opened the maximum political space possible for a woman within the political structure of late Joseon. Her pro-Russian diplomacy, this analysis holds, was not mere foreign dependence but a strategic choice aimed at balance between Japan and Qing.

**International-law significance of the Eulmi Incident:** The assassination of a queen in the palace of a capital where foreign legations were stationed, directly led by a foreign minister, was unprecedented under modern international law. Despite witness records by Russian, U.S., and British diplomats, the Japanese government acquitted those involved on grounds of "insufficient evidence" — a case studied for the limits of imperial-era international justice.

**Critique of Min-clan politics:** Alongside positive reassessment, scholars also note that Min-clan corruption and exploitation deepened the late-Joseon political crisis. Balancing victimhood and agency, achievements and failings, in the assessment of historical figures remains a current task of scholarship.
