## Who was Ahn Jung-geun?

Ahn Jung-geun was born in Hwanghae Province in 1879. From a young age he trained in archery and marksmanship.

In 1905, Japan forced through the Eulsa Treaty and seized the Korean Empire's diplomatic rights. Ahn rose against this and threw himself into the independence movement.

On October 26, 1909, at Harbin railway station in China, Ahn shot **Itō Hirobumi**, Japan's first Resident-General of Korea. This is known as the **Harbin action**.

He shouted "Korea Hurrah!" on the spot and was arrested. At trial he insisted, "As a Lieutenant-General of the Korean Independence Army, I engaged in combat."

Ahn began writing **On East Asian Peace** — a vision of Korea, China, and Japan forming a peace community together. He could not finish it. In March 1910, at age 32, he was executed. ✊

## Life and the Harbin Action

Ahn Jung-geun (1879–1910) waged armed independence struggle against Japanese aggression and assassinated Itō Hirobumi — an independence activist and thinker.

**Early life:** Born in Haeju, Hwanghae Province, into a Catholic family. His father, An Tae-hun, also supported righteous-army activities. After the 1905 Eulsa Treaty he went to Shanghai to chart a course for the independence movement.

**Righteous-army activity:** Returning home, he joined righteous-army organizations at home and abroad. In 1908 he served as a Lieutenant-General with the Maritime Province righteous-army unit, crossing the Tumen River to engage Japanese forces.

**The Harbin action (Oct. 26, 1909):** With eleven comrades, having learned of Itō's planned visit to Harbin, Ahn shot Itō at the station. He shouted "Korea Hurrah!" and surrendered on the spot. Itō died at the scene.

**Trial and martyrdom:** He was tried at the Kwantung Government-General court in Lüshun. Ahn insisted his act was combat by a righteous army and stood his ground in court. Sentenced to death on February 14, 1910, he was executed at Lüshun Prison on March 26.

**On East Asian Peace:** A work he wrote until shortly before his death, unfinished. It set out a vision of Korea, China, and Japan forming a peace community in East Asia.

## Thought and Historical Assessment

**Place of *On East Asian Peace*:** Only the preface and the *Jeongam* (precedents) sections remain of Ahn's unfinished work. The core proposal was to make Lüshun a jointly administered area, to establish a joint peace conference among Korea, China, and Japan, and to issue a common bank and currency. As an early-20th-century pioneering vision of multilateral peace in Asia, it draws notice for resemblance to today's European Union model.

**International-law debate:** At trial Ahn argued that he had acted as a Lieutenant-General of the Independence Army in combat and should be treated as a prisoner of war under international law; Japan classified the act as personal crime. The debate connects to the legal status of "colonial resistance" under the international law of the time and remains open to reinterpretation through modern international humanitarian law.

**Differing views in Korea, China, and Japan:** In Korea, Ahn is venerated as a national hero; the Order of Merit for National Foundation, Grand Order of the Republic, was conferred posthumously in 2010. In China, Ahn is viewed favorably — a memorial hall opened at Harbin Station in 2014. In Japan, the figure of Ahn sits between "terrorist" and "historic righteous man"; some Yasukuni-related materials, viewing the matter from the standpoint of Itō's commemoration, depict Ahn negatively.

**Unrecovered remains:** Ahn was buried near Lüshun Prison, but the location of his remains has not yet been identified. The Korean government has worked with China to find them, without success — an unfinished task in the substantive recovery of historical memory.
