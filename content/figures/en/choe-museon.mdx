## Who was Choe Museon?

In Goryeo times, Japanese pirates often raided Korean coasts by ship. Choe Museon studied the manufacture of gunpowder and succeeded in producing both gunpowder and cannons. In 1380, at Jinpo, his cannon-bearing warships destroyed some 500 pirate vessels — the famous **Battle of Jinpo**. The battle was the starting point for Korea's full-scale use of gunpowder weapons.

## Life and Achievements

Choe Museon (1325–1395) was a late-Goryeo inventor and soldier who left his name in history through the autonomous development of gunpowder technology and a naval victory using it.

In the 14th century, Goryeo suffered severely from Japanese pirate raids — pirates went beyond mere coastal raiders, penetrating deep inland to seize tax convoys and kill people, threatening the country itself. Convinced that gunpowder weapons were the answer, Choe Museon, after long study, succeeded in learning the refining of *yeomcho* (saltpeter) — the key ingredient — from a Yuan merchant named Li Yuan.

In 1377 he persuaded the Goryeo government to establish the *Hwatong Dogam* (Bureau of Firearms) and began producing a range of cannons and firearms. In 1380, when some 500 Japanese ships gathered at Jinpo (modern Gunsan in North Jeolla), Choe Museon led cannon-equipped warships out and burned most of the enemy fleet — the Battle of Jinpo, the first naval engagement using ship-mounted cannon in Korean history.

He left the *Hwayak Suryeonbeop* recording his manufacturing technology and weapon development; the technology was transmitted to his son Choe Haesan and became the basis for early-Joseon firearms development.

## Historical Assessment

Choe Museon (1325–1395) is read in Korean science and technology history as a man who achieved a military breakthrough through autonomous research. The core question concerns the path of his acquisition of gunpowder technology and his originality.

The *Goryeosa* records that Choe sought out Li Yuan, who was said to know Chinese gunpowder technology, and obtained the saltpeter-refining method through long persuasion and bribery. This is technology transfer rather than pure indigenous development. But the work of taking that refining technique and developing it into an actual weapons system — designing and institutionalizing varied firearms — is the product of his creativity and sustained effort.

The founding of the *Hwatong Dogam* in 1377 carries institutional significance — beyond a simple workshop, it was the first state-level establishment of a gunpowder-weapons system. Until then, Goryeo had to face the *waegu* without firearms, a critical limit.

The Battle of Jinpo (1380) is read as a tactical achievement and a technological turning point. Cannon-armed naval warfare diverged from earlier hand-to-hand and fire-attack-centered sea warfare, and directly influenced subsequent military technical development from late Goryeo into early Joseon. The *Hwayak Suryeonbeop* itself does not survive, but its content is presumed to be partly reflected in early-Joseon works such as the *Chongtong Deungrok*.

Some researchers note Choe's start as a merchant-technician rather than an official soldier — a sign that the late-Goryeo aristocratic order had limits in admitting technical innovation through formal channels. The long delay before his work received official recognition and the *Hwatong Dogam* was set up is read in the same light.
