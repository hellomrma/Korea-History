## Who was Yeon Gaesomun?

Yeon Gaesomun was the most powerful general of Goguryeo. In 642 he replaced the king and made himself supreme ruler — a daring man. They say he wore as many as five swords at his waist; can you picture how formidable he must have looked? When Emperor Taizong of Tang led an army into Goguryeo himself, Yeon Gaesomun's forces blocked them at Ansiseong and drove the Tang army back. Through commanding charisma and courage, he defended Goguryeo.

## Life and Achievements

Yeon Gaesomun (?–666) was Goguryeo's foremost military leader. In 642, through a coup, he seized power. From a great aristocratic family, he sought to inherit the office of *Magniji* but met resistance from rival aristocrats. Under the pretext of a military review, he killed some 180 nobles, assassinated King Yeongnyu, and installed King Bojang as a puppet, taking the title of *Daemangniji* — supreme military office — for himself.

Yeon Gaesomun took a hard anti-Tang line, promoting Daoism and curbing Buddhist and Confucian influence. In 645 Tang Emperor Taizong personally led 300,000 troops in invading Goguryeo, but at Ansiseong, with stubborn resistance led by Yang Manchun, the defenders held for months and the Tang withdrew. The Battle of Ansiseong is recorded as a symbolic victory that repulsed Tang's invasion.

Yet his concentration of dictatorial power proved a weakness after his death. When he died in 666, his three sons — Namsaeng, Namgeon, and Namsan — fought for power; the eldest, Namsaeng, surrendered to Tang and guided their advance from inside, decisively hastening Goguryeo's fall in 668.

## Historical Assessment

Yeon Gaesomun is one of the most polarized figures in Korean history. Positive readings cast him as a symbol of hard resistance to outside power; negative readings indict him for plunging the state into disorder by violent coup and ultimately bringing about Goguryeo's collapse.

On sources, the record on Yeon Gaesomun depends on the *Old Book of Tang*, *New Book of Tang*, and *Zizhi Tongjian* — Chinese works hostile in stance — and the Silla-centered *Samguk Sagi*. These tend to depict him as a wicked usurper, so source criticism is necessary. The *Samguk Sagi* compiler Kim Bu-sik strongly censured the 642 coup, and his view greatly influenced later Joseon historiography.

Militarily and diplomatically, his accomplishments deserve note. The 645 repulse of Taizong's personal expedition at Ansiseong is an important event in East Asian military history. Tang was the foremost military power of the day — having pacified the Eastern Türks, Tuyuhun, and Gaochang in succession — yet on the Goguryeo front it withdrew within months, and Taizong, planning a re-invasion in 649, died first. This testifies to Goguryeo's resilience under Yeon Gaesomun.

But the post-Yeon power vacuum exposes the basic limits of his rule. A dictatorship grounded in personal charisma rather than institutional, transmissible authority could not build a sustainable state. Namsaeng's defection to Tang was not mere betrayal but the realization of cleavages built into the very power structure Yeon Gaesomun had created. In this sense he is read as a man who symbolized Goguryeo's last resistance and who at the same time embodied the historical limits of that resistance.
