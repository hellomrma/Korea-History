## Who was King Uija?

Long ago, in the kingdom of Baekje, there reigned King Uija. Early in his reign, his filial piety and brotherly affection were so famed that people called him *Haedong Jeungja* — "the Zengzi of the East" (Zengzi being the wise sage of ancient China).

Later in his reign, however, wars with Silla grew frequent and conflicts among his ministers worsened. In 660, an allied force of Silla and Tang attacked Baekje and the kingdom fell. King Uija was taken to Tang and died there. He was the last king of Baekje, which had lasted nearly 700 years.

## Life and Achievements

King Uija (615–660) was the 31st and last king of Baekje, reigning from 641 to 660. Early on, his reputation for filial piety and brotherly love earned him the title *Haedong Jeungja* — likening him to the ancient Chinese sage Zengzi.

He showed military ability as well, winning several victories against Silla. In 642 he captured the strategic Daeyaseong (modern Hapcheon, South Gyeongsang) and seized over 40 fortresses — a great success. Among the dead was Kim Chunchu's daughter Gotaeso, with her husband Pumseok, an event that became the direct cause of the later Silla–Tang alliance.

But late in his reign, governance began to falter. He is recorded as ignoring the counsel of loyal ministers and indulging in pleasure. In 660, the Silla–Tang allied invasion captured Sabiseong; Uija surrendered and was taken to Luoyang in Tang, where he died of illness later that year — closing 700 years of Baekje history.

## Historical Assessment

The historical image of King Uija was long dominated by negative descriptions; modern critical historiography has substantially revised that view.

The *Samguk Sagi* records that late in Uija's reign "the king played wantonly with palace women and drank without ceasing." Reflecting a Silla-centered, victor's historiography, the passage requires source criticism. The legend that "three thousand palace women" leaped from Nakhwaam ("Falling Flower Cliff") is also seen as a later exaggeration — the spot was originally called Tasaam ("Cliff of Falling Death"), legendarily expanded. Given the actual scale of the Baekje palace, the figure of three thousand is far from historical reality.

Early-reign military success was real. Through the 642 Battle of Daeyaseong and other victories, Uija pushed Silla onto the defensive and brought Baekje to its military peak. The aggressive diplomacy and military policy of this period, however, paradoxically pushed Silla to plead more desperately with Tang for relief.

Internal causes of Baekje's collapse included the fragmentation of aristocratic power, weakening of royal authority, succession conflict among Uija's sons, and the elite's failure to respond to crisis. Records that loyal officials such as Seong Chung and Heung Su submitted military proposals which were not adopted point to problems in late-reign decision-making. Uija is reassessed not simply as a tyrant but as a tragic leader who could not adapt to international change or contain internal fissures.
