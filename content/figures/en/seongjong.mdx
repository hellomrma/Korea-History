## Who was Seongjong?

Seongjong was the ninth king of Joseon, ruling at the time when Joseon's institutions matured. He took the throne at thirteen and grew into office under the regency of Queen Dowager Jeonghui, devoting himself to learning.

In Seongjong's reign the basic Joseon law code, the *Gyeongguk Daejeon*, was completed. He also founded the Hongmungwan as the institution responsible for scholarship and brought *sarim* scholars into government. Joseon's learning and institutions matured in his time.

## Life and Achievements

Seongjong (1457–1494) was the ninth king of Joseon, reigning for 25 years from 1469 to 1494. A grandson of Sejo, he ascended at 13; in his early years, his grandmother Queen Dowager Jeonghui (Sejo's queen) governed as regent.

The greatest achievement of his reign was the completion and promulgation of the *Gyeongguk Daejeon* in 1485. Begun under Sejo and revised across several reigns, the code became the foundational law of Joseon for 500 years. Composed of six divisions — Personnel, Taxation, Rites, Military, Punishments, and Public Works — it announced Joseon's completion as a Confucian state ruled by law.

He founded the Hongmungwan as the king's advisory and scholarly research body. Inheriting the functions of the Hall of Worthies abolished under Sejo, the Hongmungwan became a base for the *sarim* (literati) scholars. He kept political stability by balancing appointments between the entrenched Hungu faction and the rising *sarim*.

He also promoted scholarship and culture, sponsoring projects such as the *Dongguk Yeoji Seungnam* (geography), *Dongmunseon* (literary anthology), and *Akhak Gwebeom* (treatise on music). Yet the late-reign decision to put Lady Yun (the deposed queen and mother of Yeonsangun) to death sowed the seeds of the later *Gapja Sahwa* (Literati Purge of 1504).

## Historical Assessment

Seongjong (1457–1494) is regarded as the monarch who realized Joseon's golden age, opening a new chapter in Joseon politics on the twin axes of completed law codes and the recruitment of the *sarim*.

**The meaning of the *Gyeongguk Daejeon*:** Promulgated in 1485, it was more than the completion of a code — it meant Joseon's completion as an institutional state grounded in Confucian principles of governance. That a project begun under Sejo was finished under Seongjong symbolizes the transition from the "extraordinary" rule of usurpation and purge to "normal" rule by written law. The *Gyeongguk Daejeon* continued to anchor Joseon law through the Imjin and Byeongja wars.

**The Hungu–Sarim balance and its limits:** Seongjong's recruitment of *sarim* scholars was a political move to check the monopoly of the Hungu faction. Placing Yeongnam *sarim* such as Kim Jong-jik in the *Samsa* (Office of the Censor-General, Office of Special Counselors, Office of Royal Decrees) strengthened the censorial function against Hungu. But this was structurally pregnant with the conflicts that exploded in the *Muo Sahwa* (1498) and *Gapja Sahwa* (1504) under Yeonsangun. As the balance leaned heavily on Seongjong's personal authority, fragility in the line of succession was already latent.

**The deposition of Lady Yun and later tragedy:** The late-reign decision to put Lady Yun to death in 1482 — counted as the greatest political fault of his reign — illustrates how internal palace conflict could couple with state affairs. Seongjong's instruction not to inform the heir (Yeonsangun) of his mother's fate produced the great political revenge of the *Gapja Sahwa* once the truth came out under Yeonsangun. Seongjong's legacy thus combines cultural flowering with the contradictions that prepared the explosions of the next reign.

Historiography evaluates Seongjong highly as the king of law and culture while emphasizing that the structural contradictions of his reign were not unconnected to the misrule of Yeonsangun.
