## Who was Gwanghaegun?

Gwanghaegun was the 15th king of Joseon. While his father Seonjo was in flight during the Imjin War, Gwanghaegun stayed near the front, leading a divided court (*bunjo*) and gathering troops.

As king he pursued reconstruction of a war-ravaged country. He introduced the new tax system known as the **Daedongbeop** to make tribute burdens more equitable.

But he had his half-brother Grand Prince Yeongchang put to death and confined his stepmother, Queen Inmok, within the palace, fearing them as threats to the throne. In 1623 the Westerners (Seoin) staged the **Injo Restoration**, deposing him.

Gwanghaegun is judged in two directions: pragmatic foreign and reconstruction policies, and harsh treatment of family and rivals.

## Life and Achievements

Gwanghaegun (1575–1641) was the 15th king of Joseon, reigning from 1608 to 1623. A *seoja* son of Seonjo who had led a divided court during the Imjin War as crown prince, he carried out postwar reconstruction and a neutral foreign policy after his accession, but his deposition of his stepmother and execution of his half-brother brought on the Injo Restoration.

**Imjin War and the divided court:** Soon after the war broke out in 1592, Seonjo invested Gwanghaegun as crown prince, dispatched him with a *bunjo* (provisional court), and fled to Uiju. Gwanghaegun toured Hamgyeong, Gangwon, and Hwanghae in person, encouraging righteous-army leaders and gathering provisions.

**Reconstruction and reform:**
- **Daedongbeop:** Pilot implementation in Gyeonggi unified tribute payments — moving from in-kind goods to a single rice payment. This was a landmark measure reducing the abuses of *bangnap* (forced grain sales) and easing peasant burdens.
- **Postwar recovery:** Rebuilding palaces lost in the war, restoring the army, reissuing books — broad recovery projects.

**Neutral diplomacy between Ming and Later Jin:** As Ming declined and the Jurchen Later Jin grew stronger, Gwanghaegun sought a pragmatic foreign policy that did not lean to either side. He sent troops in response to Ming's request for support, but instructed General Kang Hong-rip to avoid actual combat — a double strategy.

**Deposition of stepmother and execution of half-brother — the Injo Restoration:** He exiled Grand Prince Yeongchang (Seonjo's primary-line son) to Ganghwa Island, where the prince died, and confined Queen Inmok in the Seogung. The Westerners revolted; the 1623 Injo Restoration deposed Gwanghaegun, who was exiled to Jeju and died there in 1641.

## Historical Assessment

Gwanghaegun is among the most contested monarchs in Joseon history. The opposed labels of "tyrant" and "pragmatic king" reach beyond perspectives to a fundamental question of which historical values to prioritize.

**Reassessment of neutral diplomacy:** Since the late 20th century, his pragmatic policy toward Later Jin has been actively reappraised. Given that Injo's pro-Ming, anti-Jin policy after the restoration brought the disasters of the Jeongmyo (1627) and Byeongja (1636) wars, the argument that Gwanghaegun's choice was the realistic one has gained ground. The counter-argument cautions that the *uiri* (righteousness) logic of the time was not mere sentiment but tied to Joseon's whole understanding of international order.

**Significance of Daedongbeop:** The Gyeonggi pilot of the Daedongbeop is read as the first step in restructuring Joseon's fiscal system. Expanded nationally under Hyojong and Sukjong, it became the basis for late-Joseon commercial development. Whether the reformist will was Gwanghaegun's own or whether the king assented to the lead of officials such as Yi Won-ik is debated.

**Deposition narrative and the "tyrant" label:** After the restoration, the Westerner-led court branded Gwanghaegun an unfilial reprobate. The *Gwanghaegun Ilgi* was compiled by the restoration faction and is markedly disparaging. While historians note this bias, the actual deposition of Queen Inmok and the killing of Grand Prince Yeongchang are hard to deny. There are also voices warning against an over-positive rehabilitation that becomes a form of historical revisionism.

**The record of a deposed king:** That Gwanghaegun is called only by *gun* and lacks a temple name reflects Joseon's protocol for deposed kings. With Yeonsangun, his case is studied as an institutional-history example of the limits of royal power and the character of bureaucratic power.
