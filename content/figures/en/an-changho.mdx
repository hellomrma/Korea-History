## Who was An Chang-ho?

An Chang-ho — known by the pen name **Dosan** — was an independence activist. He believed that **forming people of upright character** was the path to recovering the country, more than fighting with weapons.

He always taught, "Do not lie. Do not be lazy." He founded schools and gathered young people to help them study hard and grow.

The Japanese police arrested him many times and interrogated him harshly. After he was released on bail in 1938 due to illness, he died shortly after.

## Life and Achievements

An Chang-ho (1878–1938), born in Gangseo, South Pyongan, is better known by his pen name **Dosan**. An independence activist and educator, he is sometimes called "the nation's teacher" — he made enlightenment of the people and the cultivation of character his life's work.

**Founding the Sinminhoe (1907):** As the Korean Empire trembled after Itō Hirobumi was felled, An Chang-ho organized the secret society Sinminhoe with Syngman Rhee, Yi Tong-hwi, and others. Sinminhoe sought to cultivate national strength and to build an independent state.

**Founding Heungsadan (1913):** In the United States, gathering Korean compatriots, he founded Heungsadan. Its motto, *Musil Yeokhaeng* ("act in truth and earnest practice"), guides the organization, which still exists today, in cultivating talent.

**Provisional Government:** After the March 1st Movement he served the Provisional Government in Shanghai, including as acting Premier. He argued that cultivating national capacity must precede armed struggle, which generated line conflicts with other factions. Arrested by the Japanese police soon after Yun Bong-gil's 1932 act, he died in 1938 from the long aftereffects of imprisonment and torture.

## Historical Assessment

**National regeneration and capacity-building:** An Chang-ho's central thought was "national regeneration." He attributed Joseon's collapse not only to external aggression but to internal moral failings — falsehood and indolence. The idea resembles Yi Gwang-su's "Theory of National Regeneration," but An emphasized self-renewal and solidarity rather than denigration. His thought greatly influenced Yi Gwang-su, yet while Yi later turned to collaboration, An held to independence to the end.

**Line debates:** Within the Provisional Government, An stood against Syngman Rhee's diplomatic-independence line and Yi Tong-hwi's armed-struggle line, consistently advocating capacity-building and preparationism. He was respected as a realist asking, "Can independence come without strength?" — and criticized for "blurring the will to independence by focusing on capacity-building."

**Reassessment today:** An Chang-ho is highly esteemed in Korean civil society as an emblem of nonviolent, education-centered independence work. Some scholars also raise critical perspectives on how the capacity-building line could be repurposed as logic accommodating colonial rule, so his thought remains a subject for layered reading.
