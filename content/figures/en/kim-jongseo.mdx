## Who was Kim Jongseo?

Kim Jongseo was a key official under King Sejong. Originally a civil official, he was sent to the northern frontier and expanded Joseon's territory, fixing its border up to today's Tumen River area. His upright, fearless temper earned him the nickname **Daeho** ("the Great Tiger").

When Prince Suyang carried out the Gyeyu Coup, Kim Jongseo, who tried to resist, was killed at his own home. He was one of the loyal officials supporting the boy king Danjong.

## Life and Achievements

Kim Jongseo (1383–1453) was an early-Joseon civil official and general who played a decisive role in fixing Joseon's northern frontier.

After passing the *munkwa* exam and serving in various offices, he was appointed Provincial Military Commander of Hamgyeong by Sejong. The Hamgyeong area south of the Tumen was an unstable zone of Jurchen activity. Kim Jongseo subjugated the Jurchens there and set up six garrisons (*Yukjin*) — Jongseong, Onseong, Hoeryeong, Gyeongwon, Gyeongheung, Buryeong. With Choe Yun-deok's establishment of the Four Forts (*Sagun*) on the northwestern frontier in the same period, Joseon's territory was fixed at the line of the Yalu and Tumen rivers.

After Danjong's accession he served as Left and then Chief State Councillor, supporting the young king. In 1453, Sejong's second son, Prince Suyang, raised the Gyeyu Coup; Kim Jongseo, who tried to block it, was killed at his home by Suyang's men. He was 70.

His daring, upright temper earned him the nickname Daeho — "the Great Tiger" — and he is remembered as a symbol of the loyal subject who defended Danjong to the end.

## Historical Assessment

Kim Jongseo (1383–1453) left distinguished tracks in three fields — military, administrative, and political — and was elevated as a symbol of loyalty through the Gyeyu Coup.

**Significance of the Six Garrisons:** His establishment of the Six Garrisons fixed today's northern border of the peninsula and carries great historical weight. Until then northern Hamgyeong was effectively the Jurchen sphere of life and lay beyond Joseon's administrative reach. He went beyond simple military pacification, combining the establishment of garrisons with migration policy to make territorialization real. The *Veritable Records of Sejong* repeatedly note his strategic judgment and adaptability.

**A civil official in a military role:** That a *munkwa* graduate could succeed as a military commander reflects Sejong's bold use of talent. The civil-military split in early Joseon was not yet rigid, and Sejong prioritized field experience and administrative capacity. Kim Jongseo's case illustrates how early-Joseon civil officials could integrate military and administrative roles.

**Politics of loyalty after the Gyeyu Coup:** The 1453 coup that removed Kim Jongseo was Suyang's preparatory move toward the 1455 usurpation. As Chief State Councillor and the center of Danjong's circle, Kim Jongseo was target number one. Suyang's records (*Veritable Records of Sejo*) describe him as having reduced Danjong to a figurehead and behaving arbitrarily with Hwangbo In; this can be read largely as post-hoc justification of the coup.

Kim Jongseo was rehabilitated under Sukjong and given the posthumous title *Chungik* ("Loyal-Wing"). Modern historiography reilluminates him beyond mere loyal-subject icon — as the practical executor of Sejong's northern policy and a central figure in the early formation of the Joseon state.
