## Who was Seo Hui?

Seo Hui was a diplomat who resolved a war by negotiation. During the Goryeo period, the Khitan invaded with an army of 800,000.

Seo Hui went out to meet the enemy commander Xiao Sunning in person. He argued, "Goryeo is the successor to Goguryeo. If we are to communicate with the Khitan, the Jurchens blocking the road between us must first be cleared." With that, without a military clash, Goryeo gained the broad territory of the Six Garrisons east of the Yalu (Gangdong Yukju). It is famous as a case of securing territory through diplomacy.

## Life and Achievements

Seo Hui (942–998) was a Goryeo civil official and diplomat. During the first Khitan invasion in 993, his diplomacy at the negotiation table secured the Six Garrisons east of the Yalu.

In 993, a great Khitan (Liao) army under Xiao Sunning invaded Goryeo. The court was so alarmed that some advisers urged ceding territory north of Seogyeong (Pyongyang) for peace. Seo Hui went directly to the enemy camp and negotiated with Xiao.

His logic was rigorous. First, Goryeo, as successor to Goguryeo — a fact reflected in the very name — held a historical claim to the northern lands of former Goguryeo. Second, Goryeo could not enter direct relations with the Khitan because the Jurchens blocked the road between them; if Goryeo cleared out the Jurchens, it would tribute to Liao. Xiao could not refute the argument, and the Khitan army withdrew without a fight. As a result, Goryeo gained the Six Garrisons east of the Yalu (Heunghwa, Yongju, Tongju, Cheolju, Gwiju, Gwakju) — among the most brilliant achievements in Korean diplomatic history.

## Historical Assessment

Seo Hui's 993 negotiation is repeatedly cited as a classic case of negotiation strategy in diplomatic history; multiple analyses examine the keys to its success.

Analysis must begin with the Khitan's invasion goal. Scholars hold that Liao's main aim was less actual conquest of Goryeo than severing Goryeo's solidarity with Song and pulling Goryeo into Liao's diplomatic order. In short, the Khitan were probably open to negotiation from the outset, and Seo Hui's diplomacy was a precise reading of that structural condition. That Xiao Sunning withdrew with 800,000 troops while granting the Six Garrisons in exchange shows that the Khitan leadership's strategic aim was diplomatic gain, not territorial conquest.

Seo's core "Goryeo as successor of Goguryeo" claim was not mere rhetoric but a historical and legal argument that operated within the East Asian diplomatic logic of the day. It was structurally hard for the Khitan to refute, since Liao itself stood within a cultural sphere in which legitimacy could be argued through historical succession to past states.

The strategic value of the Six Garrisons proved enormous in the long run. During the Khitan's second and third invasions in 1010 and 1018, Goryeo defended effectively from these strongpoints — especially Gwiju — and the geographic foundation that allowed General Gang Gamchan to crush the Khitan at the Battle of Gwiju (1019) was the one Seo Hui had laid. As a rare case of diplomatic success becoming military success, it marks his foresight. His negotiation is read as a structural diplomatic victory — beyond personal eloquence, it identified the adversary's strategic goal and crafted a meeting point of mutual interest.
