## Who was Hong Gyeong-nae?

Hong Gyeong-nae lived in Pyeongan Province in Joseon. He was born in 1780.

In his time, people from Pyeongan Province faced regional discrimination — even passing the state exam they could rarely reach high office. Corrupt officials added to the people's burden.

In 1811, Hong Gyeong-nae gathered farmers, mine workers, and local *yangban* in Pyeongan and rose up — the **Hong Gyeong-nae Rebellion**. It was put down after about four months and Hong Gyeong-nae fell in battle. The event is recorded as a starting point of late-Joseon peasant uprisings.

## Life and Achievements

Hong Gyeong-nae (1780–1812) was a fallen *yangban* of Pyeongan Province in late Joseon and the leader of a large-scale rebellion in 1811.

**Background:** Under late-Joseon *sedo* clan politics, men from Pyeongan faced severe regional discrimination — barred almost entirely from central office. Combined with the extortion of corrupt officials, deepening inequality, and famine, sentiment in Pyeongan became dire.

**The rebellion (1811–1812):** In December 1811, Hong Gyeong-nae, with Wu Gun-chik, Yi Hui-jeo, and others, raised troops under the banner of "smashing northwestern discrimination and punishing corrupt officials." The rebels rapidly seized more than ten counties in northern Pyeongan.

**The siege of Jeongjuseong and the end:** Forced back by government counter-attacks, the rebels withdrew to Jeongjuseong and held out for some five months. In April 1812, the government dug a tunnel under the wall and exploded gunpowder, breaching it; Hong Gyeong-nae fell in the breach.

**Significance:** The rebellion is read as a representative popular uprising against the contradictions of late-Joseon *sedo* politics and regional discrimination.

## Historical Assessment

The Hong Gyeong-nae Rebellion is read as the eruption of structural contradictions of 19th-century Joseon — more than mere rebellion, a forerunner of modern transformation movements.

**Class character of the uprising:** Whether to read Hong Gyeong-nae as a "fallen *yangban*'s rebellion" or as a broad popular coalition is debated. Participants included fallen *yangban*, small and medium merchants, mine laborers, and peasants — pointing to a composite social movement rather than the rebellion of a single class. Some researchers, noting that Hong's proclamation strategically mobilized regional sentiment, raise questions about the purity of the uprising.

**Source analysis of the proclamation:** The proclamation issued at the time sharply criticized "a court that has abandoned the people of the Western Region" and demanded reform of the existing order rather than the founding of a new dynasty. This suggests that the uprising aimed at internal reform rather than overturning the dynasty — and grounds research that traces an intellectual link to the later Donghak Peasant Movement.

**Meaning of the Jeongjuseong siege:** The five-month resistance is read not as mere military defense but as a political act in which the populace continuously articulated their demands. Records that the rebels maintained an internal administrative order within the fortress show that they were not a mere mob.

**Influence on later history:** The Hong Gyeong-nae Rebellion was the prelude to late-Joseon peasant uprisings. In the line of popular resistance through the Imsul Peasant Uprising (1862) and the Donghak Peasant Movement (1894), Hong Gyeong-nae is remembered as an archetypal figure of resistance. North Korea officially remembers him as a hero of anti-feudal struggle, while South Korean scholarship discusses both his significance and the regionalist limits.
