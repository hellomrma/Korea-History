## Who was Son Byeong-hui?

Son Byeong-hui, born in 1861, was an independence fighter. He was the leader of the religion known as Donghak; later he renamed it **Cheondogyo**.

On March 1, 1919, vast crowds poured into the streets shouting, "Long live Korean independence!" — the March 1st Movement. Son Byeong-hui was one of the 33 national representatives who signed the Declaration of Independence.

He gave himself up to the Japanese police. After three years of hard imprisonment, his health broken, he died. He sacrificed himself for the country's restoration.

## Life and Achievements

Son Byeong-hui (1861–1922) was the third leader of Donghak and the foremost figure of the March 1st Movement — a man who fused religious and national movements.

**Entry into Donghak:** He joined Donghak in 1882 and became the chief disciple of the second leader, Choe Si-hyeong. During the Donghak Peasant Movement of 1894, as leader of the Northern Order, he allied with Jeon Bongjun's Southern Order and took part in the Battle of Gongju.

**Founding Cheondogyo:** Under suppression, he went into exile in Japan in 1901, where he encountered modern civilization. After returning home, in 1905 he reorganized Donghak as **Cheondogyo**, transforming it into a modern religious organization. He pioneered publishing and education to enlighten the people.

**Leading the March 1st Movement:** He led the 1919 Declaration of Independence, signing as the lead of the 33 national representatives. Instead of the public rally at Tapgol Park, he held the declaration ceremony at the Taehwagwan and turned himself in to the Japanese police, minimizing collision with the public.

**Imprisonment and martyrdom:** Released after three years, his health was already badly broken; he died in 1922 at the age of 61.

## Historical Assessment

Son Byeong-hui is read as the most successful practitioner of religious nationalism in modern Korean history, but his strategy in the March 1st Movement invites complex interpretation.

**Strategic meaning and critique of the Taehwagwan declaration:** Son's choice — to read the declaration at the Taehwagwan instead of the rally at Tapgol Park, then to turn himself in — is explained as honoring the principle of nonviolent peaceful protest and a leadership concern not to send the public to take the brunt of suppression. Some research, however, reads it critically as a separation between leadership and the public, or as a limit of upper-tier guidance: the fierce demonstrations after the declaration were led not by the leaders but by the people themselves.

**Modernizing transformation of Donghak/Cheondogyo:** Son's reorganization was not a name change but a reconstruction of a traditional popular religion into a vehicle of modern social movement. Cheondogyo ran the Boseong Academy (a precursor of Korea University) and the Boseongsa printing house, contributing to the formation of national capital and the spread of modern education — a distinctively Korean fusion of religion and national movement.

**Debate over the representativeness of the 33:** That the 33 national representatives were drawn only from Christian, Cheondogyo, and Buddhist leaders is criticized for excluding socialist and anarchist independence activists. Some among the 33 later turned to collaboration, complicating historical assessment. Son himself is emphasized as having held to integrity to the end.

**Legacy of religious nationalism:** Son embodied a "Cheondogyo nationalism" that gathered the nation around a religious community rather than class or ideology. As a distinctive line standing in contrast to the later ideological split (nationalism vs socialism) within the national movement, it is a topic where modern Korean religious history and national-movement history intersect.
