## Who was Taejong Yi Bang-won?

Taejong Yi Bang-won was a son of Taejo Yi Seong-gye, the founder of Joseon. He helped his father found the kingdom but had to fight his own brothers to take the throne — the "Strife of the Princes," a sad chapter.

Once king, he set himself to ordering Joseon's institutions. He had everyone wear a *hopae* (identity tag) and introduced systems to strengthen royal authority. Later he handed the throne to his son Sejong, but kept watch over military affairs to support his son's rule. He was a king who exercised strong royal power.

## Life and Achievements

Taejong Yi Bang-won (1367–1422) was the third king of Joseon, reigning from 1400 to 1418. The fifth son of Taejo Yi Seong-gye, he was the man whose actual military strength enabled the founding of Joseon.

In 1398, he launched the First Strife of the Princes, removing Jeong Dojeon and Crown Prince Yi Bang-seok; in 1400, he overcame his brother Yi Bang-gan in the Second Strife and seized power. He took the throne after his brother Jeongjong abdicated to him.

His greatest achievement is the establishment of strong royal power. He implemented the **Six-Ministries Direct-Report system** (*Yukjo Jikgyeje*), bypassing the State Council so that the Six Ministries reported directly to the king. He weakened officials and concentrated authority in the throne. The *Hopae* (identity tag) law tracked the status and residence of the populace, helping with taxation and military service.

He thoroughly checked in-law and merit-official factions. He eliminated his own brothers-in-law, the Min brothers Mu-gu and Mu-jil, and abolished the private retinues of merit officials, concentrating military authority in the king. After abdicating to his third son Chungnyeong (Sejong) in 1418, he continued to hold military authority until his death, supporting Sejong's rule.

## Historical Assessment

Taejong Yi Bang-won (1367–1422) is read as the man who in practice completed the Joseon royal state — and as a cold ruler who killed brothers and purged in-laws.

**Power realignment after the founding:** Although the military core of the founding, Yi Bang-won was excluded from the crown-prince designation; he settled the conflict with Jeong Dojeon and Yi Bang-seok by force. The dominant scholarly reading treats this not as mere ambition but as a clash of state lines — prime-minister-centered governance vs royal-power-centered governance. With his victory, Joseon was set on the path of a royal state rather than a "gentry republic" centered on the prime minister.

**Structural meaning of institutional reform:** The *Yukjo Jikgyeje* concentrated decision-making in the king, in contrast to the State Council Decision-making system (*Uijeongbu Seosaje*). That Sejong reverted to the State-Council system after Taejong shows the continuing tension between the two regimes through early Joseon. The *Hopae* law, securing fiscal and military bases through population registration, can be read as a forerunner of modern resident control.

**Coldness and the politics of abdication:** Taejong's purge of in-laws was concentrated before Sejong's accession. Removing the core of the Yeoheung Min in-law group is widely read as preparing the ground so that Sejong could rule without in-law interference — suggesting that Taejong's harshness was not mere power-keeping but "structural adjustment" for the next generation.

Modern historians reassess Taejong as the unseen architect who made Sejong's flowering possible. Studies by Yi Tae-jin, Han Chung-hui and others stress that his institutional ordering provided the material and institutional foundation for the cultural and scientific advances of Sejong's age. Yet the moral problems of his violent seizure of the throne and his purges remain a continuing subject of debate, separable from his achievements.
