## Who was Kim Gu?

Kim Gu was an independence activist born in 1876. He devoted his life to Korean independence during the colonial period.

He served for many years in the **Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea** in Shanghai, eventually becoming its president. After liberation in 1945 he returned home — only to face the division of the peninsula into north and south.

Kim Gu argued for a single, unified Korean government. In 1948 he attended inter-Korean talks in Pyongyang. On June 26, 1949, he was shot and killed by An Du-hui.

## Life and Achievements

Kim Gu (1876–1949) served as president of the Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea and, after liberation, advocated a unified national government.

**Independence-movement years:** Born in Haeju, Hwanghae Province, he joined the Donghak Peasant Movement and was imprisoned for killing a Japanese agent. After the March 1st Movement of 1919, he joined the Provisional Government in Shanghai, serving as Director of Police and as Premier before becoming President.

**The Korean Patriotic Corps:** Founded in 1931, the corps led the actions of Yi Bong-chang against the Japanese emperor (Jan. 1932) and Yun Bong-gil at Hongkou Park in Shanghai (Apr. 1932). These acts revitalized a flagging independence movement and won crucial support from the Nationalist Chinese government.

**After liberation:** Returning to Korea in 1945, Kim Gu opposed Allied trusteeship and pushed for a unified government. Opposing the South-only election of 1948, he traveled to Pyongyang with Kim Kyu-sik to attempt inter-Korean negotiations, but returned without results. He was assassinated in June 1949 by Lieutenant An Du-hui.

## Reassessment

**The Rhee line vs the Kim Gu line:** The central post-liberation political conflict was between Syngman Rhee's case for a separate southern government and Kim Gu's case for a unified provisional government. Rhee proceeded on the premise that Soviet cooperation could not be expected in a Cold War; Kim Gu, fearing that division would become permanent, argued for inter-Korean negotiation. Rhee's line prevailed; the Korean War and the entrenchment of division that followed have given Kim Gu's warning ever heavier weight.

**Assessing the 1948 negotiations:** The April 1948 Pyongyang political conference is still judged variably — somewhere between "failed idealism" and "the last attempt to prevent division." Critics note that the Soviet–Kim Il-sung side was already preparing to found the DPRK and used the negotiations; defenders see seeking a peaceful solution, however slim the chances, as a national leader's responsibility. The judgment is tied to the question of who bears blame for division and remains politically charged.

**The Provisional Government's legitimacy:** The legitimacy Kim Gu defended for life is enshrined in the preamble of the current Korean constitution. Yet the Provisional Government was not recognized as a formal government by the Allies before liberation, and after liberation Kim was permitted to return only as a private individual — exposing the practical limits of the legitimacy claim. Some scholars distinguish symbolic and spiritual significance from legal and diplomatic status.

**Debate over the assassination:** Although the trial recorded An Du-hui as the lone perpetrator, doubts have persisted. An's own pre-death testimony in 1996 and subsequently disclosed materials suggested possible involvement by elements within the military or political forces; clear documentary proof has not yet appeared. The case remains one of the unresolved political assassinations of modern Korean history.
