## Who was Queen Seondeok?

Silla had Korea's first reigning queen — Queen Seondeok. Crowned in 632, she built **Cheomseongdae**, a stone observatory for watching the stars and judging farming seasons. She also raised the great nine-story wooden pagoda at Hwangnyongsa Temple and founded Bunhwangsa Temple. Through her diplomacy with Tang and her cultural projects, she laid foundations for Silla's later rise.

## Life and Achievements

Queen Seondeok (594–647) was the 27th ruler of Silla and Korea's first reigning queen. A daughter of King Jinpyeong, she ascended in 632 and reigned for 15 years until 647. She came to the throne under Silla's *seonggol* (Sacred Bone) rule, by which a daughter could succeed when there was no son.

She advanced many Buddhist and cultural projects. She founded Bunhwangsa Temple around 632–633, and in 645 the nine-story wooden pagoda at Hwangnyongsa was completed — its meaning was to pray for the submission of nine surrounding states that threatened Silla. Cheomseongdae is regarded as the oldest extant astronomical observatory in East Asia.

In diplomacy she strengthened ties with Tang, and figures like Kim Chunchu and Kim Yushin rose to prominence during her reign. Late in her rule, however, the Sangdaedeung (chief minister) Bidam led a revolt (647) on the pretext that "a woman cannot rule the state." Seondeok died before the revolt was put down. She is remembered as the queen who laid the groundwork for Silla's eventual unification of the Three Kingdoms.

## Historical Assessment

As a female ruler, Queen Seondeok's record has been shaped by gendered framings since the very stage of historical compilation. The *Samguk Sagi* treats her reign relatively briefly, while highlighting "the three things she foresaw" (*Jigi Samsa*) — anecdotes that present the queen as preternaturally perceptive. This may reflect contemporary or later discourse compensating for a female ruler's legitimacy through narratives of supernatural insight.

The character of Cheomseongdae remains contested. The conventional view treats it as a functional astronomical observatory; some have countered that it is an altar or a Buddhist symbolic structure of Mount Sumeru. The currently strong view leaves both observational and symbolic functions open. Research on the date (between 632 and 647) and on the precise function continues.

The nine-story wooden pagoda at Hwangnyongsa was built at the proposal of the monk Jajang, realizing a revelation he received from the Mañjuśrī Bodhisattva on his return from Tang. The story that each story symbolizes one of nine surrounding states links to the Silla court's deliberate use of Buddhist ideology as a tool of national integration.

Bidam's revolt (647) is a key variable in evaluating the reign. The slogan that "a queen does not govern well" was a surface pretext; the dominant view treats it as the eruption of a structural conflict between the True-Bone aristocracy and royal authority. The alliance of Kim Yushin and Kim Chunchu was decisive in suppressing the revolt — the starting point of a political realignment that led to King Muyeol's accession and the wars of unification. Seondeok is thus a sovereign who traversed the historical pivot toward Unified Silla, and her weight in that age deserves reassessment.
