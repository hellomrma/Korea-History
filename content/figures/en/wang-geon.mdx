## Who was Wang Geon?

Wang Geon lived about 1,100 years ago and founded the kingdom of **Goryeo**. He was born in 877 and died in 943.

At that time, the peninsula was split among Silla, Later Baekje, and Later Goguryeo, fighting one another — the **Later Three Kingdoms**. Wang Geon wisely allied with one power after another and unified them.

He named the new state **Goryeo** to claim the legacy of Goguryeo, and he treasured Pyongyang in the dream of expanding northward. The English name "Korea" itself comes from "Goryeo"!

## Life and Achievements

Wang Geon (877–943) was Goryeo's founding king, Taejo. He unified the Later Three Kingdoms and laid the foundation for 500 years of Goryeo rule.

**Unification (936):** Beginning as a general under Gungye, Wang Geon was raised to the throne by his retainers in 918, who renamed the state "Goryeo." When Gyeon Hwon of Later Baekje was ousted by his own son, Wang Geon seized the chance and completed unification in 936.

**Conciliating regional strongmen:** He bound regional lords (*hojok*) to him through marriage — taking 29 queens — and granted the royal Wang surname to those who served well (*saseong*).

**Ten Injunctions:** Before his death, Wang Geon left ten instructions for his successors — emphasizing reverence for Buddhism, the importance of the Western Capital (Pyongyang), and recruitment of talent without discrimination.

**Northern policy:** Claiming to be the successor of Goguryeo, he welcomed Balhae refugees and made Pyongyang the Western Capital, aiming to recover former Goguryeo lands.

## Reassessment

**Inclusive unification:** Unlike Silla's unification of 676, which relied on Tang military power, Wang Geon's unification proceeded through marriage, granting the royal surname, and accepting voluntary submission (*gwibu*) — accommodation rather than conquest. The choice traded short-term royal authority for the stability of the new dynasty by minimizing conflict with regional bases.

**Limits of a coalition state:** Early Goryeo was less a personal monarchy than a coalition of regional strongmen. The 29-queen policy was a tool of unification but also left structural instability through the rise of in-law factions. The fourth king, Gwangjong, pursued sweeping royal-power reforms (the slave-status review law, the civil-service exam) precisely to overcome those coalition limits.

**Authenticity of the Ten Injunctions:** The eighth article, instructing rulers not to employ people from south of the Charyeong Range and from outside the Gongju (Geum) River, has long fueled debate over whether parts or all of the Injunctions were later additions or glosses. The possibility of revision during the compilation of the *Goryeosa* and the absence of the original make authentication hard.

**Goguryeo succession and Balhae refugees:** Naming the state Goryeo and welcoming Balhae refugees were more than gestures of inclusion — they were claims of historical legitimacy as successor to Goguryeo. This later supplied Goryeo's diplomatic stance with Khitan and Jurchen — "recovering Goguryeo lands" — and is connected to Korean historiography's ongoing question of which "Three Kingdoms" line is the legitimate succession.
