## Who was Yeo Un-hyeong?

Yeo Un-hyeong wished for Korea to gain independence from Japan and remain one country, undivided.

On August 15, 1945, Japan surrendered at last. That very day, Yeo Un-hyeong quickly formed the **Committee for the Preparation of Korean Independence** and began preparing the new state.

He told the warring left and right, "Let us join hands together!" In 1947, however, he was shot dead by an extremist. His dream of a unified country has not yet come true.

## Life and Achievements

Yeo Un-hyeong (1886–1947) was an independence activist and politician from Yangpyeong in Gyeonggi Province. A flexible and charismatic figure, he walked a centrist nationalist path that did not entirely belong to either left or right.

**Independence years:** He worked in 1919 to plead Korean independence at the Paris Peace Conference, and in Tokyo the same year he publicly addressed Japanese government and press figures on the cause of Korean independence to great effect. He maintained ties with the Shanghai Provisional Government and, at home, organized the secret Geon-guk Dongmaeng (League for the Founding of the Country, 1944) in preparation for liberation.

**The Committee for the Preparation of Korean Independence:** On the day of liberation, August 15, 1945, he received a transfer of public-order duties from the Japanese Government-General and formed the committee. With 145 branches across the country, the organization expanded rapidly; in September the Korean People's Republic was proclaimed, but the U.S. military government refused recognition and the project was thwarted.

**Left–Right coalition and assassination:** As division loomed under U.S. and Soviet occupations, Yeo led a left–right coalition movement with Kim Kyu-sik to prevent partition. On July 19, 1947, he was assassinated by a far-right youth at the age of 61.

## Historical Assessment

**Tragedy of the centrist line:** In the rapidly emerging Cold War of the liberation space, Yeo pursued an autonomous line that did not fully integrate into either the left or the right. It was a pragmatic search for unification, but in the polarized politics of the time it was also a vulnerable position attacked from both sides. His assassination is read as a symbolic moment in modern Korean history when the possibility of centrist politics was cut off by violence.

**Debate over the Committee:** The character of the Committee for the Preparation of Korean Independence remains disputed. Some scholars view it as a successful case of self-organized Korean political organization; others read it critically as having been led by the left to create a fait accompli of the People's Republic. How leading Yeo himself was — or how far he was used by the left — is also a point of debate.

**Reassessment and present significance:** During the Cold War era of entrenched division, Yeo was not properly evaluated for ideological reasons. After the 1990s, with the end of the Cold War, he began to be reilluminated as a politician of centrism and integration; the Order of Merit for National Foundation, Grand Order with Presidential Sash, was conferred posthumously in 2005. His life remains an important reference for any historical imagination of overcoming division.
