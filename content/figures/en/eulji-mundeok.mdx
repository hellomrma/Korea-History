## Who was Eulji Mundeok?

Long, long ago, the king of Sui in China invaded Goguryeo with an army of more than a million. General Eulji Mundeok deliberately fell back step by step, drawing the enemy deep into Goguryeo. When the exhausted enemy tried to cross the Salsu (today's Cheongcheon River), he attacked all at once. The Sui army was crushed — the **Battle of Salsu**. Eulji Mundeok is the general who commanded that victory.

## Life and Achievements

Eulji Mundeok (dates unknown) was a Goguryeo general who repulsed Sui Yangdi's massive invasion in 612. Most of his life is unknown; only the Battle of Salsu is recorded — in the *Samguk Sagi* and elsewhere.

In 612, Sui Yangdi invaded Goguryeo with a force of some 1.13 million. Eulji Mundeok led the Goguryeo army in a "scorched earth" strategy — emptying fields to cut off enemy supply — while gradually drawing the Sui army close to Pyongyang. He bought time with a feigned offer of surrender to the Sui general Yu Zhongwen and waged psychological war with a Chinese-style poem (the *Yeo Sujang U Jungmun Si*) mocking his counterpart.

When some 300,000 Sui troops tried to cross the Salsu (Cheongcheon River) on their retreat, he had dammed the upper river; he broke the dam and launched an all-out attack. According to the records, only some 2,700 Sui soldiers made it back to Liaodong. The defeat contributed to Sui's decline, and Eulji Mundeok is remembered as a symbol of Goguryeo's defensive war.

## Historical Assessment

Despite the very limited sources, Eulji Mundeok has long been remembered in Korean history as a symbol of resistance to outside aggression. The core source is the Eulji Mundeok entry in the *Yeoljeon* (biographies) of the *Samguk Sagi*; the Chinese *Sui Shu* and *Zizhi Tongjian* also record the 612 war.

The *Yeo Sujang U Jungmun Si* is among the earliest extant Korean poems in Chinese verse: "Your divine plans pierce the heavens, your subtle calculations exhaust geography. Your victories' merit is already high; if you know enough, please return." A diplomatic taunt and psychological feint inducing retreat, the poem's literary polish and wit suggest Eulji Mundeok as a man of arms and letters both.

The military significance of Salsu is large. Sui Yangdi's three Goguryeo expeditions in 612, 613, and 614 all failed; the repeated defeats and domestic revolts decisively contributed to Sui's collapse. Eulji Mundeok's victory thus did more than save Goguryeo — it shaped East Asian international order.

Of his origin, official career, or fate after the battle, however, there is no record at all, and historians are cautious. Whether his surname "Eulji" was a native Goguryeo aristocratic name or derived from an office title is also unclear. Despite the source gap, Eulji Mundeok was reilluminated in modern nationalist historiography as the very type of hero against foreign invasion — a case of the gap between actual historical figure and historical icon.
