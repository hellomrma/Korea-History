## Who was King Jangsu?

Goguryeo had a king who reigned an exceptionally long time — King Jangsu, whose very name means "long life." He lived to 97 and ruled for 79 years. His father was the famous King Gwanggaeto. Jangsu moved the capital from Gungnaeseong in the north to Pyongyang to govern a wider land. He led armies south, took the Han River basin, and made Goguryeo the strongest power on the peninsula. Under King Jangsu, Goguryeo reached its largest territory in history.

## Life and Achievements

King Jangsu (394–491) was the 20th king of Goguryeo, reigning 79 years from 413 to 491 — the longest reign in Korean history. Succeeding his father, King Gwanggaeto, he raised the Gwanggaeto Stele (414) near Gungnaeseong shortly after his accession to commemorate his father's conquests and to strengthen royal authority.

His most important political decision was moving the capital from Gungnaeseong to Pyongyang in 427. More than a relocation, it was a declaration of the southern policy that shifted Goguryeo's strategic center toward the peninsula. In 475, King Jangsu personally led 30,000 troops, attacked the Baekje capital Hanseong (modern Seoul), killed King Gaero, and seized the entire Han River basin. Baekje was forced to move its capital to Ungjin (Gongju) and to ally with Silla (the Na-Je Alliance) against Goguryeo.

In King Jangsu's reign Goguryeo held its largest historical territory — Manchuria in the north and the lands south of the Han in the south. Maintaining diplomatic ties simultaneously with both the Northern and Southern Dynasties of China, his balanced diplomacy raised Goguryeo's international standing.

## Historical Assessment

King Jangsu is read as a sovereign who both inherited Gwanggaeto's conquests and consolidated them diplomatically and institutionally. His reign coincided with Goguryeo's progression beyond mere conquest state into a mature ancient state.

The historical significance of the move to Pyongyang (427) is multi-layered. On one hand it was a pragmatic move to bring the agricultural Daedong River basin under direct rule, strengthening the economic base. On the other hand, it can be read as the Goguryeo monarchy absorbing the symbolic weight of Pyongyang — a political and cultural center of the northern peninsula since the Lelang Commandery. Internally, it also aimed to dismantle the entrenched aristocratic structure concentrated in Gungnaeseong and to strengthen royal power.

The 475 attack on Hanseong and the killing of King Gaero fundamentally reshuffled the balance among the Three Kingdoms. The *Samguk Sagi* records that Jangsu sent the monk Dorim as a spy to Baekje to win Gaero's confidence and exhaust Baekje's strength — striking evidence of Goguryeo's intelligence and psychological-warfare capacity.

Goguryeo's international diplomacy under Jangsu also stands out. Sending envoys to both Northern Wei and the Southern Dynasties (Liu Song, Qi, Liang) — so-called "two-faced diplomacy" — was a high strategy that exploited China's fragmentation, showing that Goguryeo was an autonomous actor in the East Asian order. Historians see Jangsu's reign as the apex of Goguryeo's strength and as the era when the state capacity that later enabled the wars with Sui and Tang was accumulated.
