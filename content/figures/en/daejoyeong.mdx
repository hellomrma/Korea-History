## Who was Dae Joyeong?

Dae Joyeong founded a new kingdom. Long ago, when Silla and Tang had together brought down Goguryeo, the people of Goguryeo grieved deeply for their lost country.

Among them was a general named Dae Joyeong. He led Goguryeo refugees and Mohe people, defeated a Tang army, and in 698 founded a new kingdom — **Balhae** — in the area of present-day Jilin, China. He was the leader who built a new home where the people of fallen Goguryeo could gather again.

## Life and Achievements

Dae Joyeong (?–719) founded Balhae in 698 — a military leader of Goguryeo refugee origin. After the fall of Goguryeo in 668, many Goguryeo refugees were forcibly relocated under Tang rule to Yingzhou (modern Chaoyang in Liaoning Province). His father, Geol-geol Jungsang, is also said to have lived there.

When the Khitan revolted in Liaoxi in 696, Dae Joyeong seized the chaos as an opportunity, leading Goguryeo refugees and Mohe people eastward. The Tang general Li Kaigu pursued, but Dae Joyeong inflicted a major defeat on the Tang army at Tianmen Pass. On that victory he founded the state of Jin at Mount Dongmo (Dunhua, Jilin) in 698 and made himself king; the realm was later renamed Balhae.

He maintained tense relations with Tang in the early years while building strength through diplomacy with the Türks and Japan. In 713 he was invested by Tang Emperor Xuanzong as "King of Balhae Commandery," reaching reconciliation with Tang and consolidating a stable state. After his death in 719 he was posthumously honored as King Go (Gowang); under King Seon in the early 9th century, Balhae prospered to the point of being praised as the "Prosperous Land East of the Sea."

## Historical Assessment

The history of Dae Joyeong and Balhae remains a sensitive subject of sharp historical-attribution debate between Korea and China today.

Korean scholarship treats Balhae as a state that succeeded Goguryeo. Cited bases include Dae Joyeong's Goguryeo-refugee origin, the predominantly Goguryeo background of the ruling class, and Japanese diplomatic records in which the king of Balhae explicitly identifies himself as a descendant of the king of Goryeo (Goguryeo). The *Jiu Tangshu* records Dae Joyeong as "of Goguryeo stock." Chinese scholarship, in line with the "Northeast Project," treats Balhae as a Tang regional regime and emphasizes a Mohe-centered theory.

From a source-critical standpoint, attempts to fix a single ethnic identity for Dae Joyeong project modern nationalist categories onto antiquity. Balhae was a coalition of Goguryeo refugees, Mohe people, and some Han Chinese — a complex society where Goguryeo cultural inheritance among elites coexisted with Mohe culture among the populace. The historical significance of Tianmen Pass goes beyond a single military victory: it blocked Tang's Northeast Asian expansion and opened space for a new independent power.

A fundamental difficulty in Balhae studies is the absolute scarcity of sources. Almost no Balhae records survive; researchers must synthesize Chinese, Japanese, and Sillan sources, each written in differing political contexts. Records that Balhae refugees joined Goryeo after 926 are taken as important evidence of continuity between Balhae and Korean history.
