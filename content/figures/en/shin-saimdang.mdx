## Who was Shin Saimdang?

Shin Saimdang was a Joseon-era artist born about 500 years ago, in 1504.

She was famous for her **painting and calligraphy**. Her best-known work is *Chochungdo* — paintings of grass and insects so lifelike that, by tradition, a chicken once tried to peck the painted insects.

She was also the mother of the great scholar **Yulgok Yi Yi**. She raised her children with care.

Korea's **50,000-won banknote** today carries her portrait. She is one of the best-known woman artists of the Joseon dynasty.

## Life and Achievements

Shin Saimdang (1504–1551) was a mid-Joseon artist and scholar — a woman artist who won fame in her own time for painting and calligraphy.

**Life:** Born in Gangneung, she learned scholarship and art from her father. At 19 she married Yi Won-su; they had four sons and three daughters. The third son was Yulgok Yi Yi, the great Neo-Confucian. She died in Hanseong at 48.

**Artistic work:**
- ***Chochungdo* (paintings of grass and insects):** Detailed depictions of plants and insects, highly regarded for their realism and composition.
- ***Podo do* (grapes):** Her ink paintings of grapes were ranked at the highest level of her time.
- **Calligraphy:** Skilled in both *haeseo* (regular) and *choseo* (cursive) scripts; she was acclaimed as a master female calligrapher.

**Reception:** Yulgok Yi Yi composed a poem of praise for his mother's *Chochungdo*, and late-Joseon literati continued to extol her paintings.

## Historical Assessment

**Formation of the "wise mother and good wife" image:** Today's image of Shin Saimdang as the very type of "wise mother and good wife" is a construction shaped as Confucian ideology hardened in late Joseon. The shift toward foregrounding her role as "Yulgok's mother" rather than her artistic accomplishment took place mainly from the 18th and 19th centuries — a reflection of the Neo-Confucian view of women that reduced female value to childrearing. Her standing as an independent artist was relatively obscured.

**Possibilities and constraints of female art in Joseon:** The conditions that allowed her artistic activity included the education she received from her grandfather and father and the relative autonomy of long stays at her natal family in Gangneung. Yet women's work had very limited public evaluation channels of the time; the very survival of her works owes much to the fame of her son Yulgok and to records by later literati — exposing the structural limits of women artists in Joseon.

**Debate over the 50,000-won banknote selection:** When she was chosen as the portrait for the 50,000-won note in 2009, scholars and women's groups offered two opposing readings. One side embraced the symbolism of a woman appearing on the highest-denomination note. The other criticized the move as a state endorsement of the "mother" image rather than the independent artist — an issue tied to how to represent and commemorate women in history.

**Authenticity and artistic standing:** A significant share of works attributed to her face authenticity disputes. Only a few dozen are firmly attributed; even some *Chochungdo* screens at the National Museum of Korea may be later imitations. Even so, surviving works are judged of a level not inferior to male contemporaries in 16th-century Joseon painting and are recognized for the originality of their realism and refined composition.
