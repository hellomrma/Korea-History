## Yi Sun-sin, Defender of the Sea

Admiral Yi Sun-sin was born in 1545. From a young age he held the dream of protecting his country.

In 1592, Japan invaded Korea — the **Imjin War**. On land the war went badly, but at sea things were different. Yi Sun-sin's Joseon navy defeated one Japanese fleet after another!

Yi designed the famous **Turtle Ship** — a warship clad with iron plates so that arrows and fire could not hurt it. It looked like a giant turtle, hence the name.

His most famous battle is the **Battle of Myeongnyang**, where with only 13 ships he defeated a Japanese fleet of more than 300.

In 1598, during the Battle of Noryang, the admiral was struck by an enemy bullet. As he died, he is said to have said, "The battle is at its height — do not let them know I have died." ⚓

## Life and the Imjin War

Yi Sun-sin (1545–1598) was a mid-Joseon admiral whose naval campaign during the Imjin War broke Japan's command of the sea.

**Early career:** He passed the military exam in 1576 and held minor offices, then was promoted from Magistrate of Jeongeup to Naval Commander of the Left Jeolla Province in 1591 — an unusually bold appointment on Ryu Seong-ryong's recommendation. He immediately began refitting the fleet and constructing turtle ships.

**Naval engagements during the war:**
- **Okpo (May 1592):** First naval victory after the war's outbreak.
- **Hansan-do (July 1592):** "Crane-wing" formation routed most of the Japanese fleet — the turning point in command of the sea.
- **Myeongnyang (Sept. 1597):** After Won Gyun's defeat at Chilcheollyang, Yi engaged 133 enemy ships with 12 (some accounts 13) — and won.
- **Noryang (Nov. 1598):** Pursuing the withdrawing Japanese, he was killed in action.

**The Turtle Ship:** Built by adding a covered top to the existing *panokseon* warship, it was suited to gunnery and to ramming attacks. Whether it counts as the world's first "ironclad" is debated, but its originality in 16th-century Asian naval warfare is clear.

**Historical significance:** Yi's naval victories cut Japanese supply lines, prolonging the war and forcing the eventual Japanese withdrawal.

## Reassessment and the *Nanjung Ilgi*

**Source value of the *Nanjung Ilgi*:** Yi's wartime journal, the *Nanjung Ilgi*, is a primary source vividly recording a wartime commander's psychology, military strategy, and the society of the day. It was inscribed on UNESCO's Memory of the World in 2013. Alongside strategic judgments, the journal records his longing for his mother, anxiety for his subordinates, and lonely interior life — invaluable for understanding Yi the human being.

**Strategic re-analysis of Myeongnyang:** The 1597 victory was not bravery alone but precision terrain tactics — exploiting the narrow strait and rapidly changing currents of Uldolmok (Myeongnyang Strait). Use of the strait's roughly 11.5-knot tidal currents is highly regarded even by modern naval tacticians.

**The making of a hero:** During his lifetime Yi was twice dismissed and demoted to common-soldier service — a hapless commander as well as a glorious one. The "savior of the nation" image was actively elevated by nationalist intellectuals such as Sin Chae-ho and Choe Nam-seon during the colonial era and was redeployed as state ideology under Park Chung-hee. Awareness of this constructive history is important for a balanced assessment.

**Place in East Asian naval history:** The Imjin War involved Joseon, Ming, and Japan, and Yi's tactics are studied as exceptional cases — not only in East Asia but in Western naval history. The Japanese historian Kitajima Manji called Yi "the greatest naval admiral in Eastern history."
