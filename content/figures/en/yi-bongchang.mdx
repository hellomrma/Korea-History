## Who was Yi Bong-chang?

Yi Bong-chang was an independence fighter active in the Japanese colonial period.

After working as a laborer in Japan, he went to Shanghai for the independence cause and joined Kim Gu's Korean Patriotic Corps.

On January 8, 1932, at Sakuradamon in Tokyo, Yi threw a grenade at the carriage carrying the Japanese emperor. The emperor was not hit; Yi was arrested on the spot and sentenced to death. The act is called the **Yi Bong-chang attentat**. He was 32 when he was executed.

## Life and Achievements

Yi Bong-chang (1900–1932), born in Yongsan in Seoul, was an independence fighter. From boyhood he worked in Japanese-run businesses and became fluent in Japanese; he also lived in Osaka. The discrimination and resentment he experienced in Japanese society awakened his national consciousness, and he went to Shanghai to seek out Kim Gu of the Provisional Government.

**Joining the Korean Patriotic Corps:** Yi joined the corps Kim Gu organized in 1931, swore an oath to "kill Emperor Hirohito," and was dispatched to Tokyo.

**The Tokyo attentat (January 8, 1932):** Yi threw two grenades at the procession carrying Emperor Hirohito back from a military review. The grenades fell on a different carriage and the emperor was not killed, but the action — at the heart of the Japanese mainland — proclaimed to the world the Korean determination for independence. Yi was arrested on the scene and executed in October of the same year, shouting "Long live Korean independence!"

**Repercussions:** Yi's act, followed by Yun Bong-gil's in April of the same year, became the catalyst that brought Chinese Nationalist support to the Provisional Government.

## Historical Assessment

**Strategic intent of the Korean Patriotic Corps:** Kim Gu's organization of the Korean Patriotic Corps and the planning of the Yi Bong-chang and Yun Bong-gil actions was no simple terrorism but a calculated political strategy. In the early 1930s the Provisional Government's very existence was threatened by financial exhaustion and international indifference. Targeted action would draw international press attention and, in particular, secure support from the Nationalist Chinese government, which was hostile to Japan.

**The paradoxical success of a "failed" action:** The Tokyo attentat failed to assassinate. But Shanghai's Chinese newspapers reported under the headline "Unfortunately did not hit," voicing popular support and triggering a diplomatic dispute as Japan protested to China. Some analyses also note that the incident was among Japan's pretexts for invading Shanghai, complicating the international-political reach of the act.

**The identity question:** Yi was at one time accused of pro-Japanese leanings because he had used a Japanese name and lived as if he were Japanese. The choices were for survival, and the national consciousness he showed by giving his life answers the suspicion. His life is studied as an important case for understanding the complex identities of Koreans under colonial rule.
