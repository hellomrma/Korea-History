## Who was Wiman?

Long ago, in the Chinese state of Yan, there lived a man called Wiman. He led many followers across distant seas to the land of Gojoseon. At first, King Jun of Gojoseon welcomed Wiman warmly. But Wiman grew stronger and stronger and at last drove King Jun out and made himself king. The kingdom Wiman founded is called **Wiman Joseon**. Wiman Joseon traded actively with Han China and grew into a strong state.

## Life and Achievements

Wiman, of Yan origin, fled the chaos of his time around 195 BCE and crossed into Gojoseon with several thousand followers. King Jun of Gojoseon trusted him, putting him in charge of defending the western frontier; but Wiman built up his strength and in 194 BCE drove King Jun out, founding Wiman Joseon with Wanggeomseong (presumed to be in the modern Pyongyang area) as its capital.

Wiman Joseon held a domain spanning the northern peninsula and parts of Manchuria and built diplomatic and trade ties with Han China, prospering through middleman trade. Under Wiman's grandson King Ugeo, Wiman Joseon blocked surrounding polities from trading directly with Han, raising tensions. In 108 BCE the Han army invaded and ended the kingdom, and Han then established the Four Commanderies in the region.

As an ancient state grown on iron-age culture and built up through military and economic strength, Wiman Joseon holds an important place in Korean ancient history.

## Historical Assessment

Wiman is among the contested figures in Korean ancient history. The question of his origin is at the core. The "Treatise on Joseon" in Sima Qian's *Shiji* records Wiman as a man of Yan, noting that on his migration he wore a Korean topknot and Joseon-style dress. On this evidence, some scholars see Wiman as an indigenous resident of Gojoseon or as a Yan-line refugee already Koreanized; others read him as a purely Han-Chinese migrant and treat Wiman Joseon as a regime of foreign power.

The character of the state is also debated. Wiman Joseon actively absorbed iron-age culture to expand militarily and economically, formed an "outer-vassal" (*woesin*) relationship with Han, and built a middleman-trade structure that blocked surrounding polities from approaching Han. Some scholars argue that Wiman Joseon, with these bureaucratic features, was an early state rather than a mere tribal confederation. Others, citing the betrayal of the *Igyesang* Cham among the elite under King Ugeo — which hastened the 108 BCE fall — argue that centralization was incomplete.

The historical break after the establishment of the Four Commanderies is also noted. Wiman Joseon's fall brought direct Han rule to the northern peninsula, becoming the background of the later formation of the Lelang cultural sphere and Goguryeo's external resistance during the Three Kingdoms formation. Whether to recognize Wiman Joseon as an orthodox state of Korean history or to view it as a foreign dynasty remains debated, intertwined with the nationalist perspective of ancient-history awareness.
