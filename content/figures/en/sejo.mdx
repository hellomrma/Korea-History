## Who was Sejo?

Sejo was the seventh king of Joseon. Originally he was not in line for the throne — his nephew, King Danjong, was on the throne. Sejo seized it by force; this is called "usurpation."

As king, Sejo did much to organize the laws and institutions. He began the *Gyeongguk Daejeon* law code and led politics directly. But he removed the boy king and brutally punished the officials who tried to restore him. So he is remembered both as a king who organized institutions and as one who took the throne from his nephew.

## Life and Achievements

Sejo (1417–1468) was the seventh king of Joseon — birth name Yi Yu, the second son of Sejong, also known as Prince Suyang. He reigned from 1455 to 1468.

In 1453 he carried out the *Gyeyu Coup*, removing Chief State Councillor Kim Jongseo, Hwangbo In, and other key supporters of King Danjong. In 1455 he pressured Danjong into abdicating to him. In 1456, the "Six Loyal Subjects" including Seong Sammun and Bak Paeng-nyeon attempted to restore Danjong; the plot was uncovered and they were executed. Danjong was exiled to Yeongwol in Gangwon and given poison in 1457.

Sejo's major achievements include the start of the *Gyeongguk Daejeon* compilation, the *Jikjeonbeop* (direct land allocation), strengthening of the Six-Ministries Direct-Report system, and reform of local administration. The *Jikjeonbeop* allotted land only to serving officials — reducing the economic base of merit officials and strengthening state finance. He abolished the Hall of Worthies (*Jiphyeonjeon*) because its scholars had been at the heart of the restoration plot.

Through forceful royal authority he completed the institutional ordering of early Joseon, but assessments of him diverge because of the ethical problems of usurpation and Danjong's death.

## Historical Assessment

Sejo (1417–1468) is among the most contested monarchs in Korean history. His institutional achievements and his moral failings stand in sharp contrast, and scholarship has not converged on a single judgment.

**Character of the usurpation:** The Gyeyu Coup and the deposition of Danjong cannot be reduced to mere ambition. The senior ministers around Danjong — Hwangbo In, Kim Jongseo — were leading the state on behalf of a young king, and Sejo's coup carries the structural meaning of royal-authority-versus-bureaucratic-power. Suyang justified it as protecting the royal house and stabilizing the state, but this is hard to defend against the charge of post-hoc rationalization.

**Pluses and minuses of institutional reform:** Begun under Sejo, the *Gyeongguk Daejeon* was completed under Seongjong and became the backbone of Joseon law. The *Jikjeonbeop* was a policy with internal contradictions — meant to control the side-effects of Sejo-era massive land grants to merit officials. Restoring the Six-Ministries Direct-Report system was a return to Taejong's strong-royal-power model, a counter-reaction to the strengthening of bureaucratic power under Sejong's State-Council Decision-making system. The abolition of the Hall of Worthies brought a short-term loss of a scholarly institution, but its functions later passed to the Hongmungwan (under Seongjong), so the change can be read as restructuring rather than rupture.

**Politics of the Six and Six Loyalists discourses:** The veneration of the "Six Loyal Subjects who died" (*Sayuksin*) and the "Six who survived" (*Saengyuksin*) was formed through the loyalty discourse of Joseon *sarim* (literati) after Sejo's reign. The rehabilitation of the *Sayuksin* under Sukjong and the renovation of Janglung (Danjong's tomb) under Jeongjo connected to political needs of strengthening royal authority. The discourse strengthened a binary contrasting Sejo as tyrant and Danjong as tragic sage king — a simplification of historical fact.

Modern historiography evaluates Sejo as the monarch who completed the early-Joseon royal state, while noting that the violence and purges of that process paradoxically strengthened the *uiri* (righteousness) doctrine of Joseon *sarim* politics.
