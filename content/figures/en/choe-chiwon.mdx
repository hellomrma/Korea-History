## Who was Choe Chiwon?

Choe Chiwon devoted himself to learning. At the age of 12 he sailed alone to Tang China to study — beginning his studies in a foreign land at that young age.

In 874 he passed the *Bingongkwa*, Tang's civil-service exam for foreigners. With outstanding literary skill in classical Chinese, he became known as a fine writer even in Tang. Returning to Silla, he submitted reform proposals to the king, but the aristocracy blocked them; he withdrew to Mount Gaya and lived in seclusion. He is regarded as a foundational figure in Korean classical Chinese literature.

## Life and Achievements

Choe Chiwon (857–?) was a late-Unified-Silla scholar and writer revered as the founding ancestor of Korean classical Chinese literature. Born in Gyeongju, he set off at 12 to study in Tang and passed the *Bingongkwa* in 874 — Tang's exam for foreigners, whose mere passage proved outstanding scholarly attainment.

While in Tang he served on the staff of Gao Pian, military commissioner of Huainan, and during Huang Chao's rebellion in 880 he composed the *To Hwangso Gyeongmun*, a denunciation of Huang Chao. The piece was so forceful that, by tradition, Huang Chao fell from his couch on reading it. His Tang-era writings are gathered in the *Gyewon Pilgyeong*, the oldest extant private literary collection by a Korean.

Returning to Silla in 885, in 894 he submitted *Simu 10-jo* — Ten Items on Current Affairs — to Queen Jinseong to right disordered governance. Resistance from True-Bone aristocrats blocked the reforms; he served thereafter in regional posts and in old age withdrew to the area around Haeinsa Temple on Mount Gaya. The time and place of his death are unknown; legend says he became an immortal and vanished.

## Historical Assessment

Choe Chiwon stands at one apex of the 9th-century East Asian intellectual network — a literary figure but also a structural turning point in Korean ancient intellectual history.

The 20-volume *Gyewon Pilgyeong* contains diverse genres written during his Tang stay (874–885): memorials (*pyo*), petitions (*jang*), notices (*gye*), proclamations (*gyeok*), and poems. It is also a primary source on how Sillans functioned within the Tang bureaucracy. The *To Hwangso Gyeongmun* served less to actual military or diplomatic effect than to consolidate Choe's literary reputation, becoming a model of East Asian *gyeokmun* literature.

The contents of his *Simu 10-jo* are not preserved, but it is presumed to have been a centralizing administrative reform proposal informed by Tang bureaucratic experience. Its rejection was not a personal failure: it reflects the structural rigidity of Bone-Rank Silla, which could not accept the universal bureaucratic principles brought from outside. The dominant interpretation is that Choe's status as a Sixth-Head-Rank man directly contributed to the failure of his reforms.

Intellectually, his theory of harmonizing Confucianism, Buddhism, and Daoism is striking. In the *Nallang Biseo*, he held that Silla's native *Pungnyu* tradition already contained the core of all three teachings, inheriting an integrative current of Korean thought akin to Wonhyo's Hwajaeng. Later Confucian scholars hailed him as the founder of "Eastern" Confucianism, while Buddhists stressed his ties to the Hwaeom school — a complex profile reflecting his synthetic stance. Living through the chaotic late Silla–early Goryeo transition, Choe embodied the anguish of the intellectual; his withdrawal and wandering were not flight but a response to the fundamental clash of system and ideal.
