## Who was Gojong?

Gojong was the 26th king of Joseon, born in 1852 and died in 1919.

He took the throne very young. Hoping to make the country stronger, he changed Joseon's name to **Daehan Jeguk (Korean Empire)** and proclaimed himself emperor. He worked to modernize schools and the army.

But surrounding great powers kept trying to take Korea. Japan forced him to seal a treaty and at last seized the country itself. When Gojong died, vast crowds of mourners filled the streets — and that grief became the seed of the March 1st Movement.

## Life and Achievements

Gojong (1852–1919) was the 26th king of Joseon and the first emperor of the Korean Empire — a sovereign who led during the turbulent transition into the modern era.

**Accession and early rule:** Crowned at 12 in 1863, he was at first under the regency of his father, Heungseon Daewongun. He began personal rule in 1873 and turned his attention to modernization.

**Founding the Korean Empire and the Gwangmu Reform:** In 1897 he renamed the realm "Daehan Jeguk," ascended as emperor, and proclaimed independence. The Gwangmu Reform pursued land surveys, a modern military, the promotion of industry and commerce, and new schools.

**The Eulsa Treaty and resistance:** In 1905 Japan forced through the Eulsa Treaty, stripping Korea of its diplomatic rights. Gojong refused to submit and in 1907 sent Yi Jun, Yi Sang-seol, and Yi Wi-jong as envoys to the Hague Peace Conference to plead the treaty's nullity.

**Forced abdication and the loss of the country:** Using the Hague mission as a pretext, Japan forced him to abdicate in 1907. The 1910 annexation ended the country; Gojong was reduced to "Yi Tae-wang" and confined within Deoksugung.

**Spark of the March 1st Movement:** When Gojong died suddenly in January 1919, rumors of poisoning ignited public anger, leading to the March 1st Movement that year.

## Historical Assessment

Gojong is one of the most contested monarchs in modern Korean history, divided sharply between assessments as an incompetent failure and as a structural victim.

**"Incompetent monarch" vs "agent of autonomous diplomacy":** Dominant colonial-era discourse painted Gojong as indecisive and dependent on outside powers. Research since the 1990s, however, foregrounds his "balance-of-power" diplomacy that played Qing, Russia, Japan, and the United States off one another and his active resistance, including the Hague mission. Studies by Kim Tae-ung and Seo Yeong-hui argue that the Gwangmu Reform was not mere traditionalism but a top-down modernization.

**Character of the Gwangmu Reform:** Research has swung between "industrial-promotion-led modernization" and "self-strengthening against imperialism." The land survey was an attempt at modern land administration but was not completed, and the palace-centered fiscal structure has been criticized for limiting private capital.

**Legal validity of the Eulsa Treaty:** The fact that Gojong did not sign the Eulsa Treaty is supported by sources. This remains a Korea–Japan issue today and is the core basis for arguments that the treaty is null under international law.

**Doubts about his death:** Suspicions that the Japanese poisoned Gojong arose at the time of his sudden death and persist today; the cause is not definitively established. This forms an important context for understanding the historical force behind the March 1st Movement.
