## Who was Bak Hyeokgeose?

The elders of the six villages in the Gyeongju area found a great egg fallen from the sky. The egg shone with such light that it lit up the whole land. The child who emerged from it was extraordinarily handsome and wondrous. The villagers believed Heaven had sent them a king. The child was named Bak Hyeokgeose, and in 57 BCE he became the first king of Silla (then called Saro-guk). King Bak Hyeokgeose loved his people and built up the kingdom.

## Life and Achievements

Bak Hyeokgeose (c. 69 BCE – 4 CE) was the founder of Silla, founding the state of Saro-guk in 57 BCE. According to the *Samguk Sagi* and the *Samguk Yusa*, the chiefs of the six villages of the Gyeongju area, gathered to choose a virtuous leader, found a great shining egg near Mount Namsan (the Najeong well). The child born from it had a brilliantly shining body, hence the name "Hyeokgeose" ("brightness ruling the world"); the surname *Bak* came from the egg's resemblance to a gourd (*bak*).

Bak Hyeokgeose used the title *Geoseogan*, interpreted as a Jinhan word meaning "king" or "noble." His queen, Aryeong, was also a mythic figure said to have been born from a chicken-dragon by the Aryeong well — both founders thus carry sanctity linked to the heavens.

During his reign he encouraged farming and sericulture, stabilizing daily life, and built diplomatic ties with neighboring polities, consolidating Saro-guk's foundations. His founding was the starting point of a Silla civilization centered on the Gyeongju basin that would later achieve the unification of the Three Kingdoms.

## Historical Assessment

The Bak Hyeokgeose myth is an important text for understanding the historical context of Silla's early state formation. The Silla annals of the *Samguk Sagi* and the *Giii* of the *Samguk Yusa* differ in detail; both were compiled after the 12th century, so the source reliability for the founding period has limits.

The egg-birth structure is read within the wide distribution of East Asian founding myths. The hero born from an egg shares elements with the Goguryeo Jumong and Gaya Suro myths, and the solar attributes (glowing egg, brightened world) can be read as combined with heavenly-descent mythology — suggesting that early ruling groups sought legitimacy through divine genealogy.

The narrative of the six village chiefs choosing a king by consensus is important for understanding early Silla politics. It implies that royal authority was not established by the force of a single group but formed through alliance and consensus among multiple powerful groups. In fact, early Silla shows the unusual political structure of three surnames — Bak, Seok, Kim — alternating on the throne; the prevailing scholarly view is that this reflects a confederate kingship.

Archaeologically, traces of a powerful group centered on wooden-coffin tomb culture from around the 1st century BCE in the Gyeongju area provide a material basis for the formation of Saro-guk. Bak Hyeokgeose is a founding figure at the crossroads of historical reality and mythic representation — a symbol of the prototype of Silla state identity.
