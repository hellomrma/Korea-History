## Who was King Gongmin?

King Gongmin was a king of Goryeo. When he took the throne, Goryeo was under Yuan (Mongol) influence. Gongmin pursued anti-Yuan policies to throw off that interference. He recovered the Ssanggseong Commandery by force, regaining territory in the northeast. After the death of his beloved Queen Noguk, however, governance grew difficult. He was assassinated by close attendants in 1374.

## Life and Achievements

King Gongmin (1330–1374) was the 31st king of Goryeo, reigning from 1351 to 1374. His reign divides broadly into a reform phase and a phase of misrule.

As Yuan power weakened and anti-Yuan revolts intensified across China in the mid-14th century, Gongmin pursued bold anti-Yuan reform. In 1356 he retook the Ssanggseong Commandery by force, recovering the lands north of Cheollyeong that the Yuan had ruled for almost a century. He stopped using Yuan reign titles and abolished the offices the Yuan had set up, restoring Goryeo's autonomy. Through the *Jeonmin Byeonjeong Dogam* (Office for the Reform of Slaves and Land), he attempted social reform — returning land and slaves illegally seized by powerful clans to their original owners.

But in 1365, when his beloved Yuan-born queen, Princess Noguk, died in childbirth, Gongmin sank into grief and grew distant from government. He raised the monk Sin Don to continue reform; after Sin Don's fall, politics became still more disordered. Amid Red Turban and *waegu* incursions, Gongmin was assassinated by close eunuchs in 1374.

## Historical Assessment

King Gongmin (1330–1374) carries a double image in Korean history — reform monarch and failed king. He is highly regarded for swiftly seizing the international shift of the Yuan–Ming transition and putting an anti-Yuan, autonomous policy into practice. The recovery of the Ssanggseong Commandery (1356), as both military and diplomatic success, brought concrete restoration of northeastern territory; the cooperation of Yi Ja-chun, Yi Seong-gye's father, in this campaign is also notable as part of the chain leading to Joseon's founding.

His reforms confronted Goryeo's internal contradictions head-on — especially the powerful clans' engrossment of land. The *Jeonmin Byeonjeong Dogam* and the appointment of Sin Don aimed to strengthen royal power against the entrenched clans and to lighten the burden on the lower orders. By opening space for new gentry-scholars to grow, Gongmin's reforms are also read as a historical link to the rise of late-Goryeo Neo-Confucian political forces.

But his later rule loosened sharply after Princess Noguk's death. The *Goryeosa* and *Goryeosa Jeoryo* repeatedly note that he immersed himself in Buddhist painting and abandoned governance. After Sin Don's fall in 1371, the political vacuum and the disorders associated with the *Jajewi* (Royal Youth Guard) — including records of sexual deviance — were used by Joseon-era historians to read his reign negatively as a whole. Some scholars caution that, since these records intersect with the Joseon-founding logic of negating Goryeo, a critical distance is needed in source interpretation.

The assassination itself symbolizes the political turmoil and the limits of royal power in late Goryeo, and it marks the turning point toward the rule of strongmen like Yi In-im and the eventual founding of Joseon.
