## Who was Jeong Dojeon?

Long ago, there was the kingdom of Goryeo. As late Goryeo grew unstable, the scholar Jeong Dojeon joined hands with General Yi Seong-gye to build a new state — the kingdom of Joseon.

Jeong Dojeon was the architect of that new state. He laid out Gyeongbokgung, the king's palace, and planned the city of Hanyang. He also drafted the laws and institutions. In 1398, during the First Strife of the Princes, he was killed by Yi Bang-won. His life was short, but he set Joseon's institutional frame.

## Life and Achievements

Jeong Dojeon (1342–1398) was a late-Goryeo scholar and the central designer of Joseon's founding. From Bonghwa in North Gyeongsang, he was a Neo-Confucian official deeply trained in the classics.

As corruption among entrenched aristocrats and foreign incursions shook late Goryeo, Jeong became convinced that a new state was needed. He allied with Yi Seong-gye and was a decisive force in founding Joseon in 1392.

After the founding, he drew up the new state's blueprints in person — designing the new capital Hanyang, naming and laying out Gyeongbokgung, and compiling the founding code *Joseon Gyeonggukjeon* and the administrative reform work *Gyeongje Mungam* — laying the institutional foundation.

Politically, he advocated rule centered on a strong prime minister: the king should not govern alone but together with capable officials. This put him in direct conflict with Yi Bang-won, who sought to strengthen royal power. He was killed in a surprise attack during the First Strife of the Princes in 1398 — a tragic figure who designed Joseon but did not see the kingdom completed.

## Historical Assessment

Jeong Dojeon (1342–1398) was the theoretical and practical architect of Joseon's founding — among the most active revolutionaries in Korean history. His thought and acts go beyond the rank of a simple founding minister to a deep philosophical engagement with the state.

**A Neo-Confucian theory of the state and prime-minister-centered politics:** Drawing on Zhu Xi's Neo-Confucianism, Jeong constructed his own theory of governance. He held that the king represents Heaven's will and the people's heart, justifying a "revolution of surnames" by which a virtueless dynasty could be replaced. In *Joseon Gyeonggukjeon*, he designed a structure in which the prime minister oversees state affairs while the king reigns without directly governing — an attempt to institutionalize the political participation of the gentry-scholar class.

**Breadth of institutional design:** His work goes beyond naming Gyeongbokgung and planning Hanyang. He sought to abolish private retinues so that the private military power of royals and meritorious officials would revert to the state, and reformed the army by reorganizing battle formations (*jinbeop*). The push to abolish private retinues was a decisive driver of conflict with Yi Bang-won.

**Conflict with Yi Bang-won and reassessment:** After his death in the 1398 strife, Jeong was branded a traitor and was not rehabilitated for the rest of Joseon. The *Veritable Records* substantially diminished and distorted his contributions. He was rehabilitated only in 1865 (King Gojong's second year) under the regent Heungseon Daewongun — within a context of strengthening royal authority that itself carried other political motives.

In modern historiography, Jeong is reassessed as Joseon's "planner" and an "institutional revolutionary." Studies by Han Yeong-u, Yi Ik-ju and others stress that his thought aimed not at mere power but at realizing an ideal Confucian state. Critics, however, note that his case for prime-ministerial primacy ultimately rationalized the interests of the gentry-scholar elite. The life of a man who drew Joseon's blueprint but is remembered there as a traitor stands as a historical case of how the ideals of revolution collide with the realities of power.
