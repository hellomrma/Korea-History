## Who was Hong Beomdo?

Hong Beomdo, born in 1868, was an independence fighter. He had been a hunter in the mountains before joining the righteous-army and independence-army movements.

In 1920, the unit he led engaged Japanese troops at Bongo-dong and won. As a former hunter he excelled at guerrilla tactics using mountains and forests. The same year he took part in the Battle of Cheongsanri.

But General Hong then lived a hard life in foreign lands. He was deported as far as Kazakhstan, where he worked as a theater watchman until his death. Only in 2021 did his remains return to Korea.

## Life and Achievements

Hong Beomdo (1868–1943) was an anti-Japanese independence fighter who began as a hunter and left a brilliant name in independence-army history through victories at Bongo-dong and Cheongsanri.

**Hunter turned anti-Japanese fighter:** Born in Pyongan Province, he worked as a hunter before joining the righteous-army movement in 1907 in protest at the Japanese seizure of firearms. With his marksmanship and knowledge of terrain he conducted guerrilla operations and earned the nickname "the Flying Hong Beomdo."

**Battle of Bongo-dong (June 1920):** As Commander-in-Chief of the Korean Independence Army in Jiandao (modern Yanbian, China), he lured a Japanese pursuit unit into Bongo-dong and encircled and destroyed it. Killing 157 Japanese soldiers (per the independence army's records), it counts as the first major victory in the war for independence.

**Battle of Cheongsanri (Oct. 1920):** Joining forces with Kim Jwa-jin's Bukro Gunjeongseo, he fought six days at Cheongsanri, dealing severe blows to Japanese forces.

**Soviet Union and forced removal to Kazakhstan:** After the 1921 Free City Incident, he joined the Soviet Communist Party and met with Lenin. In 1937, however, Stalin's order forcibly removing Koreans took him to Kazakhstan, where he worked as a watchman at a Korean theater until his death in 1943.

**Repatriation:** In August 2021 his remains were repatriated from Kazakhstan to the Republic of Korea.

## Historical Assessment

Hong Beomdo stood at the front line of anti-Japanese armed struggle, but the latter half of his life — Soviet Communist Party membership and forced relocation — sits within a complex Cold War context that makes him one of the most contested independence fighters in modern Korean history.

**Significance of Bongo-dong:** The battle was the independence army's first major victory against Japanese regulars, symbolically demonstrating that armed independence was possible. The numbers of casualties on each side differ greatly between Korean and Japanese records, and cross-checking continues.

**The Free City Incident and Communist Party membership:** Hong's role in the 1921 incident at Free City (modern Svobodny, Russia), where independence troops resisting Soviet Red Army disarmament were massacred, is not entirely clear. His later joining the Soviet Communist Party is read as a strategic choice in the harsh reality of independence fighters in Manchuria and the Maritime Province, where survival was scarcely possible without Soviet support. Whether to read this as ideological conversion or as pragmatic survival differs by researcher.

**The 2023 bust-removal debate:** When Hong's bust at the Korea Military Academy was relocated on the grounds of his Communist Party past, a major social debate ensued. It raised fundamental questions about how to weigh struggle credit against ideological background and whether Cold War anti-communist logic should mediate historical assessment.

**View from the Goryeo-saram diaspora:** Hong's life touches the history of the Goryeo-saram community, displaced by Soviet ethnic policy. In Kazakhstan's Goryeo-saram society he is remembered as a symbol of national identity, and the 2021 repatriation is also read in the context of Korea–Kazakhstan relations and diaspora history.
