## Who was Seonjo?

Seonjo was the 14th king of Joseon. He took the throne in 1567 while still young.

In 1592, during his reign, Japan invaded Korea — the Imjin War. As the war began, Seonjo fled all the way to Uiju, leaving Hanseong empty; the people criticized him sharply.

He raised commanders like Yi Sun-sin and Gwon Yul to fight the Japanese. After seven years of war the Japanese withdrew, but the country and its people had suffered enormous damage.

His reign saw the rise of *sarim* politics in earnest, but views of him are divided over his flight during the Imjin War and the postwar handling of the country.

## Life and Achievements

Seonjo (1552–1608) was the 14th king of Joseon, reigning some 41 years from 1567 to 1608. With Myeongjong dying without an heir, Seonjo took the throne from a collateral royal line.

**The rise of *sarim* politics and factional split:** At the start of his reign, Seonjo brought *sarim* scholars in en masse and pushed out the Hungu faction. In 1575, however, a dispute over the appointment of the *Ijo Jeollang* split the *sarim* into Easterners (*Dongin*) and Westerners (*Seoin*), opening the era of Joseon factional politics. The Easterners later divided again into Southerners (*Namin*) and Northerners (*Bugin*).

**The Imjin War (1592) and Seonjo's response:** When Japan invaded in 1592, Seonjo left Hanseong, fleeing all the way to Pyongyang and Uiju, leaving officials and people behind. This caused popular alienation, but he also raised Yi Sun-sin and Gwon Yul to office and requested Ming reinforcements. Hanseong was retaken in 1593; the Jeongyu Reinvasion broke out in 1597; the war ended in 1598.

**Conflict with Yi Sun-sin:** Seonjo dismissed Yi Sun-sin twice and reduced him to common-soldier service. He had a tendency to check distinguished retainers and is judged to have been miserly in recognizing Yi Sun-sin's merit even after his death.

**Succession trouble:** Crown Prince Gwanghae was of secondary-line birth (*seoja*), and Seonjo favored Grand Prince Yeongchang (a primary-line son). The conflict greatly affected Gwanghae's later politics.

## Historical Assessment

Assessments of Seonjo divide sharply over his reign as a whole and over his Imjin War response.

**Positive view — completion of *sarim* politics:** Seonjo brought back into the center the *sarim* who had been hammered by literati purges under Jungjong and Myeongjong, completing the mid-Joseon Neo-Confucian political order. The age of Yi Hwang, Yi Yi, and Ryu Seong-ryong belongs here. That Yi Yi's *Maneon Bongsa* memorial proposing 100,000 trained soldiers was rejected by Seonjo is often cited as a symbolic failure of Imjin War preparation.

**Historical context of his Uiju flight:** Some hold that Seonjo's flight to Uiju cannot be reduced to cowardice — it can be read as a strategic move to request reinforcements from Ming. The *Veritable Records of Seonjo* and the later *Revised Veritable Records* show divergent views from the time itself, and the very compilation of the revised work was a political product of the Seoin redaction of Bugin-era records.

**Repression of Yi Sun-sin:** Seonjo dismissed Yi Sun-sin in 1597 after Yi refused to follow an order to ignore Japanese naval negotiation overtures. The result was Won Gyun's catastrophic defeat at Chilcheollyang, and Yi Sun-sin had to be reinstated to rebuild the navy. The case continues to be analyzed for what it reveals about Seonjo's judgment and his suspicion of distinguished officials.

**The structuring of factional politics:** The factional politics anchored in Seonjo's reign became the continuing pattern of conflict in Joseon politics. Whether to read it as political pluralism grounded in public opinion or as fragmentation around factional interest remains debated. The dominant view is that Seonjo tried to maintain royal authority by exploiting balance among factions but in the end could integrate none.
