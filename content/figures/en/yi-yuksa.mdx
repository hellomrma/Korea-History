## Who was Yi Yuk-sa?

Yi Yuk-sa was a poet and independence fighter of the Japanese colonial period.

The pen name "Yuk-sa" comes from his prisoner number, **264**. Through repeated imprisonments he carried on both anti-Japanese work and his poetry.

His poem *Gwangya* ("The Wilderness") contains the line: "After a thousand more ages, a superhuman shall come on a white horse and sing aloud across this wilderness." The poem is read as envisioning a future beyond colonial reality. Yi Yuk-sa died in a Japanese consular prison in Beijing in 1944, at the age of 40.

## Life and Achievements

Yi Yuk-sa (1904–1944), born in Andong in North Gyeongsang, was a 14th-generation descendant of Toegye Yi Hwang; his given name was Yi Won-rok. The Confucian *seonbi* (scholar) ethic was foundational to him. After being implicated in the 1927 bombing of the Daegu branch of the Joseon Bank and imprisoned, he took his pen name **Yuk-sa** (陸史) from his prisoner number, 264.

**Independence work and imprisonment:** Yi joined the Uiyeoldan (Heroic Corps) and was arrested 17 times in his life. He moved between Korea and China to lay the groundwork for armed independence and even enrolled in the Joseon Revolutionary Military and Political Cadre School.

**Poetic world:** In *Gwangya*, *Cheongpodo* ("Green Grapes"), *Jeolchong* ("Climax"), and *Kkot* ("Flower"), he expressed steely will and patient hope for liberation amid harsh trials. In *Jeolchong*, "Whipped by the lash of the bitter season / Swept at last into the north" gives the colonial reality the form of an extreme cold.

**Martyrdom:** Arrested by the Japanese police in Beijing in 1943, he died in a Beijing prison in January 1944, at age 40. Most of his poems were collected and published after his death.

## Historical Assessment

**A poet who acted:** Yi Yuk-sa is often paired with Yun Dong-ju as a "resistance poet," but their modes of resistance differ fundamentally. Where Yun resisted through interior reflection and shame, Yi struggled through direct action — Heroic Corps activity and military training. His poems sublimate this tough activist temperament: "Where shall I bow my knees? / There is not a step's space to set my foot" in *Jeolchong* is an emblem of uncompromising resistance.

**Confucian worldview and resistance:** Some research argues that the moral commitment of Toegye-school *uiri* (righteousness) thought underlies his ethic. The *seonbi* spirit that does not bow to injustice fused with modern nationalism to shape his independence work and poetic world — a case study in how Korean intellectuals of the colonial era reinterpreted and practiced traditional thought.

**Issues of textual recovery:** Yi's poems were not systematically organized in his lifetime, and many works are presumed lost. Among surviving poems, variants exist between published versions and manuscripts, so philological study to establish authoritative texts continues. Some details of his Heroic Corps activity remain unresolved and are still under investigation.
