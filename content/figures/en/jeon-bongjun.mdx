## Who was Jeon Bongjun?

Jeon Bongjun, born in 1855, was nicknamed **"General Nokdu"** ("General Mung Bean") — for being short, like a mung bean.

In his time, some officials were imposing heavy taxes and forced labor on peasants. In 1894, Jeon Bongjun gathered farmers and rose up — the **Donghak Peasant Movement**.

His peasant army was defeated by government and Japanese troops, and Jeon was captured and executed in 1895. The Donghak Peasant Movement is recorded as a representative case of late-Joseon peasant resistance.

## Life and Achievements

Jeon Bongjun (1855–1895) led the late-Joseon Donghak Peasant Movement and is remembered as "General Nokdu."

**The uprising begins:** In Gobu County, North Jeolla, the magistrate Jo Byeong-gap forced peasants to build the Mansegbo reservoir and extorted heavy water taxes. In January 1894, Jeon Bongjun led some 1,000 peasants to seize the Gobu government office, opening the rebellion.

**First uprising and the Jeonju Treaty:** The rebellion spread across Jeolla; the peasants defeated government forces at Hwangtohyeon and Hwangnyongchon and captured Jeonju. They concluded the Jeonju Treaty with the court and set up *Jipgangso* offices to carry out reform autonomously.

**Second uprising and the Battle of Ugeumchi:** When Japan intervened in domestic affairs amid the Sino-Japanese War, Jeon raised a second uprising under an anti-foreign banner in September of the same year. He suffered a decisive defeat at the Battle of Ugeumchi (Gongju, Chungcheong) at the hands of joint Japanese-government forces armed with modern weapons.

**Arrest and execution:** Jeon was betrayed and captured while fleeing, and was executed in March 1895.

**Significance:** Combining anti-feudal and anti-foreign aims, the Donghak movement influenced the Gabo Reform and is regarded as a fountainhead of Korea's modern national movement.

## Historical Assessment

Jeon Bongjun and the Donghak movement are among the most actively reinterpreted subjects in modern Korean history, with revolution, transformation, and religious-movement readings competing.

**Character of the movement:** Early research saw it as a simple peasant rebellion; from the 1960s and '70s it was reassessed as a "modern-oriented revolutionary movement." The autonomous administration through the *Jipgangso*, the demand to abolish status discrimination, and demands for land reform are read as a serious challenge to the feudal order. Other scholars argue that Donghak's religious messianism and restorationist worldview limit its modernity.

**Source examination of the reform articles:** The 12 reform articles proposed during the *Jipgangso* period include emancipation of slaves, abolition of *cheonin* discrimination, permission for widow remarriage, and equitable distribution of land. Some interpret them as reflected in part in the Gabo Reform of 1894; others read them as the court pre-empting peasant demands to absorb the movement's energy.

**The anti-foreign meaning and its limits:** The anti-Japanese banner of the second uprising is acclaimed as a forerunner of national movements, but whether the peasants' worldview rested on a modern national concept or on a traditional *hwa-i* (civilized vs barbarian) view remains disputed. The defeat at Ugeumchi laid bare the overwhelming technological gap between peasants armed with matchlocks and modern weapons.

**Source value of the trial record:** *Jeon Bongjun Gongcho* — the record of his interrogation after capture — is a primary source conveying his thought and motives directly. He consistently argued for repulsing foreign powers and for popular self-government, and the record is the basis for evaluating Jeon as a thinker rather than a mere rebel leader.
