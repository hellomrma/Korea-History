## Who was Choe Chungheon?

In Goryeo, beyond the king there were powerful men who actually moved the country — Choe Chungheon was such a military strongman. He removed his rivals among the military and rose to the top, holding power so great that he could change kings at will. He governed directly and passed his power to his son. Though never a king, he held more power than the king.

## Life and Achievements

Choe Chungheon (1149–1219) was a mid-Goryeo military officer and the foremost figure of the Military Regime period. After the military coup of 1170, Goryeo entered a period in which the military dominated politics; through the era, Yi Ui-bang, Jeong Jung-bu, Gyeong Dae-seung, and Yi Ui-min held power in turn.

In 1196, Choe Chungheon removed Yi Ui-min and seized power. He sustained his rule through more than mere military force — building institutional bases. He strengthened the *Dobang* private guard and established the *Gyojeong Dogam* to control personnel, administration, and the military as a whole. The *Gyojeong Dogam* in effect served as Choe Chungheon's personal organ of governance, displacing royal authority.

He exercised such absolute power that he deposed or installed four kings in his time — Huijong, Gangjong, and Gojong among them. Early on he submitted *Bongsa Sipjo* (Ten Articles of Memorial) to King Myeongjong proposing political reform, but his actual rule grew increasingly arbitrary. Power was inherited by his son Choe U, and through Choe Hang and Choe Ui formed the Choe military regime spanning four generations and some 60 years.

## Historical Assessment

Assessment of Choe Chungheon (1149–1219) does not split simply for and against. Traditional historiography has cast him negatively as a tyrannical strongman who usurped royal authority and exploited the people. He did receive the title Marquis of Jingang and personally appointed high officials, handling state affairs without royal sanction in unprecedented ways.

Some researchers, however, note that his rule consolidated the chaos within the military regime and brought a certain stability to Goryeo society. His proposals in *Bongsa Sipjo* — curbing temple land engrossment, prohibiting plunder by local officials — reveal an awareness of social contradictions and are sometimes credited as such. Critics counter that since these were not realized as substantive reform, they were merely rhetorical justification.

The institutionalization of the *Gyojeong Dogam* and *Dobang* is a rare case of the development of private governing organs in Korean history, sometimes compared with the Japanese bakufu system. The military regime maintained a dual structure in which the throne formally continued while real power lay elsewhere — a distinctively Goryeo political form, distinct from a "revolution of surnames" that abolishes the king altogether.

His era also saw lower-class and slave liberation movements such as Manjeok's revolt (1198) — read as an expression of the contradictions produced by the regime's exploitation and its social mobility. A full assessment of Choe Chungheon must read his personal arbitrariness alongside the structural problems of contemporary Goryeo society.
