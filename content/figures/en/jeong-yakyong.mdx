## Who was Jeong Yak-yong?

Jeong Yak-yong was a late-Joseon scholar, born in 1762.

When Hwaseong Fortress was being built at Suwon, he designed the **Geojunggi**, a hoist for lifting heavy stones. Thanks to it, the great fortress could be raised much more efficiently.

He was later swept up in a political case and sent into exile in a distant region. There he spent 18 years writing — leaving more than 500 volumes on how to improve people's lives.

## Life and Achievements

Jeong Yak-yong (1762–1836) was the foremost Silhak ("practical learning") scholar of late Joseon. His pen name was Dasan.

**Trust under King Jeongjo:** From a young age Jeong enjoyed Jeongjo's deep favor. During the construction of Hwaseong Fortress, his pulley-based **Geojunggi** dramatically reduced construction time and cost; he also worked on the design of the boat-bridge (*judagyo*).

**The Sinyu Persecution and exile:** When Sunjo took the throne in 1801, Catholics were persecuted (the *Sinyu Persecution*). Jeong was exiled to Gangjin in South Jeolla. His brother Jeong Yak-jeon was sent to Heuksan Island; another brother, Jeong Yak-jong, was executed. His exile lasted 18 years.

**Vast writings:** Even in exile he devoted himself to scholarship — completing more than 500 volumes, including *Mongmin Simseo* on the duties of local magistrates, *Gyeongse Yupyo* on national institutional reform, and *Heumheum Sinseo* on the just handling of criminal cases.

**Synthesis of Silhak:** Stressing land reform, technical innovation, and the welfare of the people, his thought is regarded as the apex of Joseon Silhak.

## Historical Assessment

Jeong Yak-yong is the synthesizer of late-Joseon Silhak, and his thought and writings are widely seen as a forerunner of modern reformism.

**Complexity of intellectual background:** His scholarship interweaves the reinterpretation of Confucian classics, the technical pragmatism of the Northern Learning school, and the influence of Western Learning (Catholicism). Baptized as "John," he formally renounced Catholicism after the Sinyu Persecution. Whether he truly abandoned the faith or chose strategic survival is a continuing debate.

**Source value of *Mongmin Simseo*:** *Mongmin Simseo* is not a mere administrative manual but a vivid record of local-government corruption and the suffering of the common people of the age. Concretely depicting magistrate avarice, clerk graft, and tax extortion, it is essential to the study of late-Joseon social history.

**The "Yeojeon" land system:** Jeong proposed *Yeojeon* — village-unit cooperative cultivation with distribution by labor input. This radical conception sought to root out Joseon's landlord system; though unrealized at the time, it influenced later debates over modern land reform.

**Reassessment and present significance:** During the colonial period, Choe Nam-seon and Jeong In-bo and others rediscovered Jeong as a wellspring of national modern thought, raising his standing. Some scholars, however, criticize the resulting elevation as turning him into an over-nationalist hero. The intellectual achievement that flowered amid extreme exile also draws notice as a self-overcoming narrative for East Asian intellectuals.
