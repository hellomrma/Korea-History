## Who was Injo?

Injo was the 16th king of Joseon. In 1623, his retainers deposed Gwanghaegun and raised Injo as the new king — the **Injo Restoration**.

When Injo took the throne, the predecessor of the Qing — Later Jin — was rising to the north. Injo and his court trusted only Ming and slighted Later Jin. The result was two invasions: the **Jeongmyo War (1627)** and the **Byeongja War (1636)**.

In the Byeongja War, Injo took refuge in **Namhansanseong** and held out for 45 days, but in the end he had to surrender. Before the Qing emperor he had to perform the humiliating ritual of striking his forehead to the ground nine times — known as **the Humiliation at Samjeondo**.

Injo's reign is remembered as a time of grave suffering for the people through two wars.

## Life and Achievements

Injo (1595–1649) was the 16th king of Joseon, enthroned in 1623 by the Injo Restoration. His reign saw two Manchu wars and Joseon's most humiliating royal surrender.

**The Injo Restoration (1623):** The Westerners (Seoin) raised the restoration on the pretext of Gwanghaegun's deposition of his stepmother and execution of his half-brother. Injo, on his accession, adopted a pro-Ming, anti-Jin policy and entirely abandoned Gwanghaegun's neutral diplomacy.

**The Jeongmyo War (1627):** Later Jin invaded. Injo took refuge on Ganghwa Island, and the war ended with Joseon and Later Jin concluding a peace based on a "brotherly" relationship.

**The Byeongja War (1636) and the Humiliation at Samjeondo:**
- Later Jin renamed itself "Qing" and demanded a sovereign-vassal relationship; Joseon refused.
- When Hong Taiji of Qing personally led the invasion, Injo took refuge at Namhansanseong and resisted for 45 days.
- In January 1637, at Samjeondo (modern Songpa, Seoul), Injo performed the humiliating *sambae gugodu* — three kneelings and nine prostrations — before Hong Taiji.
- Crown Prince Sohyeon, Grand Prince Bongnim (later King Hyojong), and others were taken to Qing as hostages.

**Crown Prince Sohyeon:** After eight years as a hostage in Qing, the Crown Prince returned home and died suddenly under suspicious circumstances. The theory that Injo poisoned him circulates; Grand Prince Bongnim was then made crown prince and ascended as Hyojong.

## Historical Assessment

Injo and his era have long been criticized as a symbol of Joseon's diplomatic failure; recent scholarship offers more complex analysis that examines the international structure and internal politics of the time together.

**Why pro-Ming, anti-Jin failed:** Abandoning Gwanghaegun's pragmatism for a pro-Ming, anti-Jin line was not mere diplomatic miscalculation; it was bound up with the legitimacy of the restoration itself. Since the restoration's banner was overthrowing "Gwanghaegun, who betrayed righteousness toward Ming," the regime could not ideologically abandon the pro-Ming line. This shows the structural limit in which diplomacy was subordinate to domestic political logic.

**Significance of the Samjeondo surrender:** The Humiliation at Samjeondo (1637) was the first and last time in Joseon history that a king performed a surrender ritual to a foreign power directly. The Samjeondo Stele commemorating the event became a symbol of disgrace for later Koreans, and the surrender produced the ideological reaction known as the *Bukbeollon* (Northern Expedition theory). Historian Han Myeong-gi structurally analyzes what choices were available to Joseon amid the rapid East Asian transformation, pointing to the cognitive limits of the whole Joseon ruling class rather than Injo personally.

**The Crown Prince Sohyeon poisoning theory:** That Crown Prince Sohyeon, having encountered Western culture in Qing, died mysteriously upon his return is sometimes read as Joseon's foreclosed chance to absorb a new worldview. But there is no direct documentary evidence of poisoning, and how deep his actual interest in Western Learning ran is also debated.

**Merit officials and the weakening of royal authority:** The Westerners, ennobled in numbers as merit officials after the restoration, remained a strong check on Injo throughout his reign. Yi Gwal's revolt of 1624, born of dissatisfaction with the merit-official appointments, exposes the regime's internal instability. Injo sought to strengthen the throne but could not escape a structure subordinated to the political weight of the merit-official–bureaucratic group.
