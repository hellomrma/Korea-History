## Who was Yeonsangun?

Yeonsangun was the tenth king of Joseon. He is called *gun* ("prince") rather than a temple-name king because he was later deposed; deposed kings are not honored as kings and are addressed by the title *gun* instead of *jo* or *jong*.

His mother, Lady Yun, died when he was a child. When he later learned that she had been unjustly put to death, his grief and rage were so great that he punished many officials and neglected the country. In the end the officials joined together and deposed him — the **Jungjong Restoration**.

## Life and Achievements

Yeonsangun (1476–1506) was the tenth king of Joseon, reigning from 1494 to 1506. He was the eldest son of Seongjong, but his mother — the deposed Lady Yun — was given poison and died when he was four. He grew up unaware of these events.

Early in his reign rule was relatively normal, but two literati purges (*sahwa*) marked his name in history. In the *Muo Sahwa* of 1498, the historian Kim Il-son's inclusion of his teacher Kim Jong-jik's *Joeujemun* in the historical drafts (*sacho*) was made the pretext for executing or exiling many *sarim* scholars. In the *Gapja Sahwa* of 1504, on learning the truth of his mother's unjust death, he punished all involved — exhuming corpses for posthumous beheading without hesitation.

He then dissolved censorial bodies (Saganwon, Saheonbu) and turned the Sungkyunkwan into a place of revelry, undermining governing discipline. In 1506, Park Won-jong, Seong Hui-an, and others raised the Jungjong Restoration, deposed Yeonsangun, and exiled him to Ganghwa Island, where he died that year.

## Historical Assessment

Yeonsangun (1476–1506) is fixed in Korean history as the very type of "tyrant." Modern historiography increasingly approaches his actions not as madness alone but as a product of structural and psychological causes.

**Political character of the *Muo Sahwa*:** Triggered on the surface by the historical-draft issue, the 1498 purge was at heart a counter-strike by the Hungu faction against the *sarim* who had grown since Seongjong. Yeonsangun absorbed the political demands of the Hungu and lent royal authority to their plan rather than personally orchestrating the suppression. By this reading the *Muo Sahwa* is less "tyrant's tyranny" than "Yeonsangun's choice in factional struggle."

**The *Gapja Sahwa* and psychological motives:** Unlike *Muo*, the 1504 purge involved much stronger personal motive. The shock and rage at learning of his mother's death were the direct cause. Psycho-historical research analyzes Yeonsangun's behavior as a pattern combining maternal deprivation, abandonment trauma, and over-reliance on the absoluteness of royal authority. Studies by Jeong Du-hui, Yi Yeong-chun and others note that his "tyranny" was not mere personality disorder but linked to the structural problems of the Joseon royal house — the practice of queen deposition and concubine politics.

**Political context of deposition and bias in the record:** After the Jungjong Restoration, records of Yeonsangun were edited from the victor's perspective. Tellingly, the *Yeonsangun Ilgi* is the only "diary" — not a "veritable record" — among Joseon royal records, and was compiled by the restoration's merit officials, raising the issue of source bias. Historians note that descriptions exaggerating and justifying the case for misrule may be embedded in it.

Assessment of Yeonsangun has not departed greatly from the "tyrant" frame, but the horizon of research has widened to read the misrule as the composite outcome of personal flaws, royal-house structure, factional politics, and psychological background.
