## Who was King Yeongjo?

Yeongjo holds the longest reign in Joseon history — he ruled the country for **52 years**!

He worked very hard to reconcile feuding officials. This is called the **Tangpyeong Policy** ("Policy of Impartial Equality"). He tried to choose people who would work for the country fairly without taking sides.

He also created the **Gyunyeokbeop**, halving the cloth tax that ordinary people paid in lieu of military service. The burden on poor people grew much lighter.

But there is a sad story about King Yeongjo. He had his own son, **Crown Prince Sado**, locked inside a wooden rice chest, where he died. What happened between father and son remains a historical mystery.

## Life and Achievements

King Yeongjo (1694–1776) was the 21st king of Joseon, reigning 52 years from 1724 to 1776 — the longest reign of the dynasty. A son of Sukjong and grandfather of Jeongjo, he led a late-Joseon revival through his Tangpyeong politics and reforms for the people.

**Tangpyeong policy:** To soften the factional conflict that had deepened under Sukjong, Yeongjo made *tangpyeong* the core of governance. He balanced appointments without favoring any faction and erected a Tangpyeong stele at Sungkyunkwan to inscribe his intent. Although factionalism was not completely resolved, political stability was substantially maintained for a long time.

**The Gyunyeokbeop (1750):** Cloth tax in lieu of military service was reduced from two bolts to one, lightening the people's tax burden. The lost revenue was offset by salt-and-fish taxes, ship taxes, and the *gyeoljak* land surcharge — a major late-Joseon reform of fiscal and military structures.

**Cultural promotion and codification:** He had the *Sok Daejeon* compiled to cover changes since the *Gyeongguk Daejeon*, sponsored book publication, and laid foundations for the late-Joseon literary revival.

**The Imo Tragedy (1762):** Yeongjo confined his son Crown Prince Sado in a rice chest; he died after eight days. Records cite the prince's mental disturbance and misbehavior as the immediate cause, but the case is analyzed as a complex of factional politics and succession conflict. The episode profoundly affected Jeongjo's later politics and historical narrative.

## Historical Assessment

Yeongjo, a representative late-Joseon monarch, is highly regarded for the reforms and *tangpyeong* politics of his long reign, while the tragic Imo affair shows the complexity of his rule.

**Character of *tangpyeong* — a tool of royal authority:** On the surface, the *tangpyeong* policy aimed to mediate factional conflict; some analyses see it as a substantive instrument by which the king controlled appointments to weaken bureaucratic power and strengthen his own. The bureaucratic group called the "Tangpyeong faction" was effectively the political base of his rule, often moving with the king's intentions. Park Gwang-yong characterizes it as "moderate-doctrine *tangpyeong*" (*Wallon*), distinguishing it from the more active "rigorous-doctrine *tangpyeong*" of Jeongjo (*Junnon*).

**Significance and limits of the Gyunyeokbeop:** The Gyunyeokbeop was a landmark fiscal reform that reduced taxpayer burden, but the *gyeoljak* surcharge imposed on landlords was often passed on to tenant farmers. Various evasions of military service continued, and assessments note that the structural problems of the military-service system were not fully resolved.

**Debate over the Imo Tragedy:** The Crown Prince Sado affair is among the most disputed cases in Joseon history. The crown prince's closeness to the Soron amid the Noron–Soron struggle, and the political amplification of his rift with Yeongjo, played a composite role. Lady Hyegyeong's *Hanjungnok* is an important source but, written to defend her natal family (the Pungsan Hong), carries a self-defensive character that requires cross-checking. Some psychiatric studies analyze the prince's behavioral disorders, and scholars note that the case should not be reduced to madness alone.

**Legitimacy of a king of secondary-line birth:** Yeongjo, the *seoja* son of a mother of *musuri* (palace water-bearer) origin (Lady Sukbin Choe), faced challenges to his status legitimacy on his accession. Yi In-jwa's revolt of 1728 had this background. Some psycho-historical analyses suggest that his lifelong, almost compulsive pursuit of *tangpyeong* and the image of a benevolent ruler was not unconnected to this anxiety about legitimacy.
