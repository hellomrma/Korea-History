## Who was Jang Bogo?

Jang Bogo led maritime activity in the age of Unified Silla. At that time pirates would seize Sillans and sell them as slaves. Jang Bogo petitioned King Heungdeok and, in 828, set up a military base at Wando called **Cheonghaejin**.

From Cheonghaejin he suppressed the pirates and led trade among Silla, Tang, and Japan. Running an East Asian maritime network, he built great power — the most prominent figure of 9th-century East Asian sea trade.

## Life and Achievements

Jang Bogo (?–846) was a maritime merchant and soldier of Unified Silla who built the greatest sea power in ancient East Asia. His origin is unclear, but he is presumed to have been a commoner from the Wando area.

In his youth he went to Tang and served as a junior officer in the Wuning Army (*Muryeonggun*), gaining military experience. Outraged by the kidnapping of Sillans by pirates and their sale as slaves in Tang, he returned home and, with King Heungdeok's permission in 828, established Cheonghaejin on Wando with 10,000 troops. From this base he swept up the pirates, took the lead in trilateral Korea–China–Japan sea trade, and built a network of overseas bases — including the Beophwawon ("Lotus Sutra Hall") on the Shandong Peninsula — that supported Sillan communities inside Tang. The Japanese monk Ennin's *Nittō Guhō Junrei Kōki* vividly records how Jang Bogo's organization operated along the Tang coasts.

Drawn into a Silla succession dispute, he helped enthrone King Sinmu in 839, but his attempt to make his daughter a royal consort was blocked by aristocratic resistance. In 846 he was assassinated by an emissary, Yeomjang, sent by the Silla court; Cheonghaejin was dissolved in 851.

## Historical Assessment

Jang Bogo is being reassessed not as a mere merchant or local warlord but as an international actor who reshaped 9th-century East Asian maritime order.

The *Samguk Sagi* biography is relatively brief, but cross-reading the Japanese source — Ennin's *Nittō Guhō Junrei Kōki* — and Tang records sharpens the picture. Ennin records that during his stay in Tang from 838 to 847, return home was impossible without the ships and provisions of Jang Bogo's organization, showing how broad the Beophwawon network across Shandong and Jiangnan really was. This suggests his organization performed near-state welfare and diplomatic functions, not merely those of a trading firm.

In economic-history terms, Jang Bogo's order symbolizes the privatization of 9th-century East Asian trade. As maritime trade shifted from state-centered tribute exchange to a private network, Jang Bogo was a core driver of the transition. His goods — Sillan silks and ceramics, Tang silks and aromatics, Japanese gold and pearls — were high-value luxuries; the capital they generated was converted into military and political power.

Behind the assassination lay the status barrier of an aristocratic Silla. The attempt to install his daughter as a royal consort was a challenge by a rising maritime stratum to the established Bone-Rank order, which the aristocracy could not tolerate. Jang Bogo's downfall is a historical case showing that 9th-century Silla society lacked the structural flexibility to accommodate the rise of new strata.
