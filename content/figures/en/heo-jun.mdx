## Who was Heo Jun?

Heo Jun was a royal physician of the Joseon dynasty who devoted his life to treating patients and compiling medical knowledge.

The book he compiled is the **Donguibogam** ("Mirror of Eastern Medicine"). It records a wide range of illnesses and treatments. In particular, it describes how to use medicinal herbs available on the Korean peninsula and lists the local Korean names of the herbs so that ordinary people could recognize them.

The Donguibogam was inscribed on UNESCO's Memory of the World list in 2009. It is regarded as a synthesis of East Asian traditional medicine.

Heo Jun was of secondary-line birth, but his medical excellence was recognized and he rose to become *eoui* — the king's personal physician.

## Life and Achievements

Heo Jun (1539–1615) was a mid-Joseon royal physician and medical scholar who compiled the *Donguibogam*, the synthesis of Korean medicine.

**Life and career:** Of *seoja* (secondary-line) birth, Heo Jun became a physician of the Royal Medical Bureau (*Naeuiwon*) despite status restrictions, on the strength of his skill. With King Seonjo's confidence, he served as *eoui* (royal physician) and accompanied the king through the Imjin War. Under Gwanghaegun he rose to *Sungrok Daebu*, an exceptionally high rank for a *seoja*.

**Compilation of the *Donguibogam*:**
- **Begun (1596):** Started under Seonjo's order; interrupted by the Imjin War.
- **Completed alone (1613):** As the scholars who had begun the project together scattered during the war, Heo Jun completed it on his own. It was printed in the 5th year of Gwanghaegun (1613).
- **Structure:** 25 volumes divided into Internal (*Naegyeongpyeon*), External (*Oehyeongpyeon*), General Diseases (*Japbyeongpyeon*), Decoctions (*Tangaekpyeon*), and Acupuncture/Moxibustion (*Chimgupyeon*).

**Significance:**
- Built on Chinese medicine but constructed an autonomous system rooted in Joseon's medical experience.
- Listed Korean folk names alongside herb names so that ordinary people could find the herbs.
- Inscribed on UNESCO's Memory of the World list in 2009 — internationally recognized.

## Historical Assessment

Heo Jun and the *Donguibogam* can be analyzed in the multi-layered contexts of medical history, intellectual history, and social history; the work is more than a medical compilation — it is a striking case of the particular character of Joseon knowledge production.

**Place in East Asian medical history:** The *Donguibogam* is a masterpiece of East Asian medicine compiled at almost the same time as China's *Bencao Gangmu* (1596). Its core innovation is to receive Chinese medical knowledge and reorganize it for Joseon's climate, landscape, and herb system. The very title "*Dongui*" (Eastern medicine) is read as proclaiming a tradition distinct from Chinese medicine. The book was exported to China and Japan, influencing East Asian medicine; it was reprinted dozens of times in Edo Japan.

**Social significance of native naming:** Listing a Korean folk name (*hyangmyeong*) alongside each herb is read not as mere convenience but as a turn toward popularizing medical knowledge. Late-Joseon Silhak scholars praised this and cited Heo Jun as a forerunner of practical learning. Some researchers caution that the actual extent to which these folk names were used among the populace deserves careful examination.

**The status question:** Heo Jun's *seoja* origin is often used to render his achievement more dramatic. But it should be read together with the social-historical context: in late Joseon, the technical ranks (medicine, interpretation, law) increasingly came to be monopolized by *seoja* and *jungin*. His success rested not only on exceptional individual talent but on the structural condition of upward mobility within the medical track.

**Re-examining the compilation process:** Records of the time confirm that Jeong Jak and other physicians initially took part under Seonjo, and that after the war disbanded the collaboration, Heo Jun completed the work alone. After the UNESCO listing, some criticize the tendency to over-nationalize the *Donguibogam* and stress the need to read it within the broader frame of shared and circulating East Asian medical knowledge.
