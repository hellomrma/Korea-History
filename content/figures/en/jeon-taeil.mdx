## Who was Jeon Tae-il?

Jeon Tae-il was a 22-year-old worker in a small garment factory in Seoul.

In those days, factory workers — especially young women — were forced to work 14 to 16 hours a day. Many of the children fell ill with lung disease from the long hours. Jeon Tae-il cried out, "We are not machines! We want to live like human beings!" — but no one listened.

On November 13, 1970, he set himself on fire shouting, "Obey the Labor Standards Act!" His act became the starting point of the Korean workers' fight for better treatment.

## Life and Achievements

Jeon Tae-il (1948–1970), born in Daegu, could not finish elementary school because of his family's poverty and began working at a sewing factory in the Pyeonghwa Market beside Cheonggyecheon in Seoul.

**Working conditions:** The Pyeonghwa Market sewing factories of the 1960s were extremely harsh. In unventilated attic-like rooms, teenage women worked 14 to 16 hours a day, and tuberculosis was common. The Labor Standards Act limiting work hours existed in law but was utterly ignored on the floor.

**Labor activism and self-immolation:** Jeon studied the Labor Standards Act on his own, filed petitions with the labor authority, and sent appeals to the president — all by lawful means, with no change. On November 13, 1970, in front of Pyeonghwa Market, he set himself on fire shouting, "We are not machines!" and "Obey the Labor Standards Act!" He was rushed to hospital and died that evening at 22.

**Mother Lee So-seon and the spread of labor activism:** Lee So-seon, who saw her son die, devoted the rest of her life to the labor movement. Jeon's act became a catalyst for solidarity between students and intellectuals and the labor movement, and is regarded as the proper starting point of the Korean labor movement.

## Historical Assessment

**Origin of the Korean labor movement:** Jeon Tae-il's self-immolation has become the symbolic origin of the Korean labor movement after the 1970s. His death first lifted the problems of the urban poor and workers onto the social agenda and turned the eyes of intellectuals and students toward the factory floor. This was the starting point of the historical current in which 1980s democratization and labor activism converged.

**A face of the developmental dictatorship:** 1970, when Jeon died, was the year Park Chung-hee's regime celebrated the completion of the Gyeongbu Expressway and the country's economic growth. The juxtaposition asks on whose labor and sacrifice the "Miracle on the Han" was built. The export-led growth model fueled by low-wage labor could be sustained only by suppressing workers' basic rights — and Jeon Tae-il bodily indicted that structural contradiction.

**Memory and inheritance:** The Jeon Tae-il Foundation and Memorial Hall continue his spirit. Cho Yeong-rae's biographical *Life of Jeon Tae-il*, drawn from Jeon's own writings, is a classic of Korean social-movement literature. On the 50th anniversary of his death in 2020, the National Assembly debated the "Jeon Tae-il Three Laws" — revisions to the Trade Union Act, the Labor Relations Adjustment Act, and the Labor Standards Act — and his name remains a living reference point in Korean labor legislation.
