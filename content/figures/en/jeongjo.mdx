## Who was King Jeongjo?

Jeongjo was the 22nd king of Joseon. After his grandfather Yeongjo had his own son — Jeongjo's father, Crown Prince Sado — confined in a rice chest until he died, Jeongjo grew up devoted to learning.

Once king, he moved his father's tomb and built a new fortress at Suwon — **Hwaseong Fortress**. Hwaseong was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 1997.

Jeongjo prized scholarship. He set up the **Gyujanggak** as a royal library and research institute, studying policy and learning together with scholars.

He also abolished some of the monopoly rights of the *sijeon* merchants in the *Sinhae Tonggong* of 1791, broadening commerce. His reign is regarded as a peak of late-Joseon scholarship and culture.

## Life and Achievements

Jeongjo (1752–1800) was the 22nd king of Joseon, reigning from 1776 to 1800. The son of the doomed Crown Prince Sado, he came to the throne against a tragic family background and, through strong royal authority and reformist policies, presided over the apex of late-Joseon literary and cultural revival.

**The Gyujanggak:** On his accession in 1776 he established the Gyujanggak as a royal library and research institute. He appointed Silhak scholars of *seoeol* (illegitimate or secondary-line) origin — Bak Je-ga, Yi Deok-mu, Yu Deuk-gong — as *Geomseogwan*, breaking through status barriers to cultivate talent.

**Founding the Jangyongyeong:** He created the Jangyongyeong as a royal guard force, putting direct command of military power in the king's hands — a substantive instrument of royal authority that supported his Tangpyeong politics.

**Hwaseong Fortress (1794–96):** He moved his father Crown Prince Sado's tomb to Suwon and built Hwaseong nearby. Using new technologies such as Jeong Yak-yong's hoist (Geojunggi), the fortress was completed in just over two years. It was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 1997.

**Sinhae Tonggong (1791):** Abolishing the *Geumnanjeongwon* monopoly of *sijeon* merchants, he opened freer commerce, energizing the economy and broadening tax revenue.

**Chogye Munsin system:** Selecting talented young officials for re-education at the Gyujanggak, he cultivated a corps of new officials loyal to the king.

## Historical Assessment

Jeongjo is highly esteemed as a representative reform monarch of late Joseon, but his death and the character of his reform invite varied interpretations.

**Deepening of Tangpyeong politics — *Junnon Tangpyeong*:** Unlike Yeongjo's moderate Tangpyeong, Jeongjo's is called *Junnon Tangpyeong*. Rather than seeking mere balance among factions, he weighed right and wrong and threw the king's weight behind the right side — a more active mode read as a project to reorganize the factions around the king on a foundation of strong royal authority. Park Gwang-yong's research argues that Jeongjo's Tangpyeong was a sophisticated political engineering that exploited fissures within the Noron.

**Silhak and the *Munche Banjeong*:** While encouraging Silhak, Jeongjo also pushed *Munche Banjeong* — regulating prose styles influenced by *paegwan* fiction and late-Ming/early-Qing writing. Apparently contradictory, the two policies show his pursuit of practical learning together with the establishment of a Zhu Xi–style literary mode. The impact of *Munche Banjeong* on the intellectual world of his time remains under discussion.

**The mystery of his sudden death:** Jeongjo's sudden death in June 1800 at age 49 is a historical puzzle. The theory that he was poisoned during treatment of a boil long held wide currency and was popularized by Yi Deok-il's writings. Many scholars criticize the poisoning theory as relying on circumstantial evidence with thin documentation. The *Veritable Records of Jeongjo* and the *Ilseongnok* document the deterioration of his health, and natural death must also be considered.

**Limits of reform and the Sunjo reversal:** When the young Sunjo took the throne after Jeongjo's death, the regency of Queen Dowager Jeongsun (Yeongjo's second consort) began, and the reformist forces Jeongjo had nurtured rapidly waned. The Sinyu Persecution of 1801 closed off the open intellectual atmosphere of the Jeongjo era — exposing the fragility of reforms that had depended on royal authority without institutional and social foundations. Historians use this to diagnose the structural limits of late-Joseon reform.

**The complex meaning of Hwaseong:** Hwaseong was not merely architecture but an instrument of political design. Some analyses argue Jeongjo aimed to develop Suwon as a new city to disperse the Hanyang-centered power structure, and to lodge an outer command of the Jangyongyeong at Hwaseong as an autonomous military base. Whether Jeongjo ultimately envisioned moving the capital to Suwon remains debated.
