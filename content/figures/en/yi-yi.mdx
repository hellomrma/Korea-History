## Who was Yi Yi?

Yi Yi is also known by his pen name **Yulgok**. His mother was the famous artist Shin Saimdang. Yi Yi excelled in scholarship from his earliest days. He took the civil-service exams nine times and placed first every time, earning the nickname **"the Nine-Time First"** (*Gudo Jangwongong*).

Foreseeing danger, he urged the kingdom to maintain an army of 100,000 — but his proposal was not adopted. Years later, the Imjin War indeed broke out. Yi Yi's portrait appears on Korea's 5,000-won banknote.

## Life and Achievements

Yi Yi (1536–1584) was a mid-Joseon Neo-Confucian scholar and statesman; his pen name was Yulgok. Born in Gangneung in Gangwon Province, his mother was Shin Saimdang, the renowned woman artist of the Joseon era.

A standout from his youth, Yi Yi placed first in all nine exams from the preliminary stage to the final, earning him the title *Gudo Jangwongong* (Nine-Time First).

With Yi Hwang, he formed one of the two great pillars of Joseon Neo-Confucianism, but with a distinct philosophy. Yi Yi held an "*iki* monism" — that *i* (principle) and *gi* (material force) cannot be separated — and stressed the role of *gi*. This intellectual difference led to the divergence between Yi Yi's Giho school (Gyeonggi–Chungcheong) and Yi Hwang's Yeongnam school.

He was also active politically. In *Dongho Mundap* he criticized the failings of contemporary politics, and in *Seonghak Jipyo* he laid out the cultivation of the sovereign and proper governance systematically. He carried out *hyangyak* (community compacts) in Haeju and Paju, building local self-governing order. He proposed maintaining 100,000 trained soldiers in preparation for Japanese invasion; the proposal was rejected, and the Imjin War broke out eight years after his death.

## Historical Assessment

Yi Yi (1536–1584) most clearly shows the combination of theoretical deepening of Joseon Neo-Confucianism and political-reform thought. With Yi Hwang he formed the twin pillars of Korean Confucianism, but with quite different orientations.

**Distinctive features of his *iki* theory.** Yi Yi's *iki* theory is summarized as "*gi* issues, *i* rides — a single path." Although *i* and *gi* are conceptually distinct, in reality they are inseparable; activity is always carried out by *gi*, and *i* operates only by riding upon *gi*. This squarely opposes Yi Hwang's *ibal* doctrine, which recognized an autonomous issuing of *i*. Yi Yi's view focuses moral practice on the concrete, real force of *gi*, and is taken to provide stronger theoretical grounds for institutional and social reform than for pure self-cultivation.

**Realist reform and statecraft.** Yi Yi's distinctive trait is the close coupling of metaphysics with concrete politics. *Dongho Mundap* (1569) and *Seonghak Jipyo* (1575) insist that moral cultivation by sovereign and gentry must run together with institutional reform. In particular, his *simu* (current-affairs) reform writings sharply diagnosed structural problems of Joseon — military disarray, abuses in tribute, weak schooling — and proposed concrete alternatives. Some scholars therefore read his thought as a forerunner of Silhak, in contrast to Yi Hwang's heart-and-mind-centered scholarship.

**Debate over the "100,000 soldiers" proposal.** The proposal has been admired and somewhat mythologized in later eras. The *Veritable Records of Seonjo* contain no clearly defined record of it, and the exact numbers and context are disputed by researchers. Still, that Yi Yi consistently argued for strengthening defense and reforming the military system is clear, and after the Imjin War his warning was reappraised as foresight.

**Giho school and faction politics.** Yi Yi's lineage, transmitted through Seong Hon and Song Si-yeol, formed the Giho school (the Seoin and later the Noron), a central force in late-Joseon politics. Its conflict with Yi Hwang's Yeongnam school (the Namin) became the structural axis of factional politics, beyond mere scholarly debate. Yi Yi himself attempted to mediate at the early split of Easterners and Westerners but did not succeed; after his death, his name was used to underwrite the legitimacy of the Seoin.
