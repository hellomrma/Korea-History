## King Gwanggaeto, Who Expanded the Land

Gwanggaeto was a king of Goguryeo. He was born in 374 and died in 412. Crowned at 18 and gone at only 39, he accomplished an extraordinary amount in his short reign.

The name "Gwanggaeto" means "one who broadly opened the land." True to his name, he greatly expanded Goguryeo — to Manchuria in the north and below the Han River in the south.

He led troops into battle himself. Records say he took 64 fortresses and 1,400 villages.

After his death, his son King Jangsu raised a great stone stele in Ji'an, Jilin (Manchuria), to honor him. Six meters tall, this is the famous **Gwanggaeto Stele**, which still stands today! 🏔️

## Conquests and Achievements

King Gwanggaeto the Great (374–412, r. 391–412) was the 19th king of Goguryeo. His full posthumous title is *Gukgangsang Gwanggaetogyeong Pyeongan Hotaewang*.

**Accession and early conquests:** Crowned at 18, he immediately attacked Baekje and seized ten fortresses north of the Han River (392). In 396, on a major personal campaign, he captured Hanseong, the Baekje capital, and forced King Asin's surrender.

**Records on the stele:** The Gwanggaeto Stele, raised in 414 by King Jangsu (height 6.39 m), records his conquests in detail. According to the inscription, he conquered 64 fortresses and 1,400 villages and, in 400, dispatched a major force to drive Wa (Japan) out of Silla.

**Reach of expansion:**
- North: Liaodong and Sushen (northern Manchuria)
- South: into the Han River basin, gaining the upper hand over Baekje and Silla
- East: subjugation of Eastern Buyeo
- Peninsula: rescue of Silla from Wa invaders (400)

**Significance:** Gwanggaeto's conquests raised Goguryeo to the status of an East Asian superpower. On that foundation, his son Jangsu carried out the move to Pyongyang (427) and the southern policy, sustaining Goguryeo's zenith.

## Source Debates around the Stele

**The Sinmyo-year passage:** The largest scholarly dispute over the stele concerns the Sinmyo-year (391) entry. Late-19th-century Japanese scholars read the line "Wa crossed the sea and destroyed Baekje, □□, and Silla, making them their subjects" as evidence for the "Mimana Nihonfu" theory of Japanese rule in the southern peninsula. Today most Korean and Japanese scholars read Goguryeo as the subject — i.e. Goguryeo defeated Wa. Parts of the inscription are abraded, so a final reading is hard to settle.

**The lime-coating controversy:** When the Japanese Army General Staff officer Sakō Kageaki took rubbings in the 1880s, the possibility was raised that lime had been applied and some characters altered (argued by the Korean-Japanese historian Yi Jin-hui). Connected to historical distortion in the imperial era, the dispute is sensitive and has not been settled.

**Goguryeo's "all-under-heaven" view:** The stele honors the king as *Hotaewang* and *Seongsang* and lays out a Goguryeo-centered order — important evidence that Goguryeo had its own conception of the world, distinct from China's investiture–tribute system.

**Korea–China differences:** China's "Northeast Project" treats Goguryeo as a Chinese regional regime. Korean scholarship, citing Goguryeo's own all-under-heaven view in the stele and the conscious succession claims of Goryeo and Joseon, predominantly treats Goguryeo as part of Korean history.
