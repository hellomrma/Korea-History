## Who was General Kim Jwa-jin?

Kim Jwa-jin was a general who led the independence army during the Japanese colonial period.

In 1920, a great battle was fought against Japanese forces at Cheongsanri in Manchuria. Although the Japanese fielded thousands, Kim Jwa-jin shrewdly used the mountains and valleys and won as many as ten engagements over six days! This is the **Battle of Cheongsanri**.

He continued independence-army work in Manchuria until January 24, 1930, when he was killed by someone within the same camp.

## Life and Achievements

Kim Jwa-jin (1889–1930), born in Hongseong in South Chungcheong, was an independence activist and soldier. As a young man he freed the family's slaves, a sign of his strong sense of justice; after the loss of national sovereignty he went to Manchuria and gave himself fully to armed independence work.

**Battle of Cheongsanri (1920):** As commander of the Bukro Gunjeongseo, Kim fought some ten engagements in the Cheongsanri area over six days, October 21–26, 1920, winning a great victory. With about 1,200 Japanese soldiers killed (per Korean records), the battle ranks together with Hong Beomdo's Bongo-dong as the greatest victories in independence-army history. Surprise tactics that exploited the terrain and high troop morale were the keys.

**Afterward:** To escape Japan's massive retaliatory operation (the Gyeongsin Incident), he moved to the Maritime Province before returning to Manchuria, where he organized the Sinminbu and rebuilt the independence-army base. Holding a nationalist line, he came into conflict with communist factions, and in January 1930 he was assassinated by a communist in Jilin Province at the age of 41.

## Historical Assessment

**Military significance of Cheongsanri:** The battle is highly regarded in military history as a case of an independence army at absolute disadvantage in regular military strength defeating regulars through guerrilla tactics that exploited mountain terrain and the mobility of its commander. The officer-training system of the Bukro Gunjeongseo and the staff corps of Yi Beom-seok and others underwrote the victory. Differences between Korean records (some 1,200 Japanese killed) and Japanese records have produced ongoing scholarly debate over the scale of casualties.

**Ideological conflict and assassination:** In 1920s Manchuria, conflict between nationalist and communist (socialist) lines deepened. Leading the Sinminbu, Kim held an anti-communist line, and that ultimately led to his assassination by a member of the communist camp. His death is a symbolic case showing how grave the ideological division within the Manchurian independence movement had become.

**Reassessment:** Long honored as a "national hero" under anti-communist regimes, Kim's image came to be somewhat schematized and mythologized. Recent research seeks a multidimensional understanding of him within the complex internal dynamics and ideological terrain of the independence movement.
