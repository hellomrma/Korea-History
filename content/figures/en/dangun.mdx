## Who was Dangun Wanggeom?

Dangun Wanggeom founded **Gojoseon**, the first nation of Korea — about 4,300 years ago, in 2333 BCE.

According to legend, **Hwanung**, the son of the sky-god Hwanin, came down to earth and married **Ungnyeo**, a bear that had become a woman. Their son was Dangun Wanggeom. He set his capital at **Asadal** and founded the kingdom.

Dangun's founding ideal was **"To broadly benefit humankind"** — *Hongik Ingan*. The phrase remains the guiding principle of Korean education today.

## Life and Achievements

Dangun Wanggeom is the founding ancestor who established Gojoseon in 2333 BCE — the first national leader of the Korean people.

**The Dangun myth:** According to the founding myth recorded in the *Samguk Yusa*, Hwanung — son of the sky-god Hwanin — descended beneath a sacred tree on Mount Taebaek and opened the *Sinsi* (City of God). He married Ungnyeo, a bear who had become a woman, and their son was Dangun Wanggeom, who set his capital at Asadal and founded Joseon.

**Hongik Ingan ideal:** "To broadly benefit humankind" — *Hongik Ingan* — was Gojoseon's founding ideal and is enshrined as the guiding ideal of education in today's Framework Act on Education.

**The Eight Article Code:** Gojoseon had a code of eight laws (*Paljo Geumbeop*); only three articles are transmitted today. They prescribed punishments for murder, injury, and theft, showing that Gojoseon already had private property and a class society.

## Historical Assessment and Reinterpretation

**The Dangun myth as historical source:** The myth first appears in writing in Iryeon's *Samguk Yusa* (c. 1281, under King Chungnyeol of Goryeo). The traditional founding date of 2333 BCE was aligned to the accession year of Emperor Yao in China — a symbolic rather than empirical anchor. The contemporary *Jewang Ungi* (Yi Seung-hyu, 1287) also mentions Dangun but differs in detail, showing that the myth did not crystallize uniformly.

**Reading the social structure within the myth:** Scholars treat the myth not as a simple tale but as a reflection of the society of its time. The descent of Hwanung's group is read as the migration of a sky-worshiping agricultural people; their union with the bear-totem group is read as alliance with — or absorption of — an indigenous hunter-gatherer population. The dominant interpretation is a mythic expression of tribal-confederation formation.

**Founding-date debate and archaeology:** Korean archaeology generally regards 2333 BCE as predating the developed Bronze Age and places the actual formation of the Gojoseon state in the 10th–7th centuries BCE or later. Some scholars suggest connections to the Liaohe (Hongshan) civilization, but no consensus has emerged.

**Dangun and national identity:** During the Japanese colonial period, Dangun was rediscovered as a spiritual center of national independence, and the religion Daejonggyo systematized Dangun belief. Today, Dangun retains symbolic status as the common ancestor of the Korean people, while where to draw the line between myth and history remains a debate among historians, archaeologists, and folklorists.
