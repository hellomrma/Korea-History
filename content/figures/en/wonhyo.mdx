## Who was Wonhyo?

Wonhyo was a Buddhist monk and thinker of Silla. There is a famous story about him. One night, parched with thirst, he drank water from a vessel he found by feel — and it tasted delicious. The next morning he saw that the vessel was a skull! Startled, he had a realization: **"Everything depends on the mind."**

He wanted to share this insight with everyone. Instead of teaching only in difficult temples, he went out to markets and villages, even singing, to bring the Buddha's teachings to ordinary people. Thanks to him, many in Silla could understand Buddhism easily.

## Life and Achievements

Wonhyo (617–686) was a leading Buddhist thinker and monk of Silla around the time of unification. His secular surname was Seol, and he is said to have been from Gyeongsan in North Gyeongsang.

On his way to study in Tang, the experience of drinking from a skull led him to grasp *Ilche Yusimjo* — "all things are made by the mind alone" — and he gave up the journey to continue independent practice and study in Silla. He left vast writings, including the *Daeseung Gisillon So* and *Geumgang Sammaegyeong Ron*. Tang Buddhists treated his commentaries as required reading — a measure of his level.

His core thought, **Hwajaeng** (harmonization), is an integrative philosophy that holds that the divisions among Buddhist schools can be reconciled in one truth. After his connection with Princess Yoseok and the birth of his son Seol Chong, he called himself *Soseong Geosa* and took up bold popular preaching. Spreading Pure Land devotion to Amitabha through dance and song, he turned Buddhism from an aristocratic religion into a religion of the people — a historic shift.

## Historical Assessment

Wonhyo holds a unique place in East Asian Buddhist intellectual history; he greatly influenced Buddhism in China and Japan as well.

His Hwajaeng thought is a meta-philosophical system that sublates (in a Hegelian sense) the rivalry between Emptiness (*kong*) and Consciousness-Only (*yusik*) thought that divided contemporary East Asian Buddhism. The logic of his *Sibmun Hwajaeng Ron* unfolds dialectically: any claim, taken to extreme attachment, is in error, and individual positions are integrated at a higher level. This is not eclecticism but a rigorous internal analysis of each school followed by exposure of its limits.

The *Geumgang Sammaegyeong Ron* deserves particular note. The sutra itself is suspected of having been composed in Silla, with Wonhyo's commentary establishing its authority and re-exporting it to China — an instructive case in the complex political economy of 7th-century East Asian Buddhist text production.

His "breaking of precepts" (*pagye*) and popular preaching are read not as deviation but as a deliberate challenge to a precept-centered aristocratic Buddhism. Linked to the work of his son Seol Chong, who systematized *idu* and laid foundations for Korean studies in classical Chinese, the Wonhyo line was a key family in 7th-century Silla intellectual history. Today Wonhyo is reassessed as a Buddhist scholar, popular thinker, and educator in one, and the philosophy of "all things are made by the mind alone" is being newly examined in dialogue with modern psychology and cognitive science.
