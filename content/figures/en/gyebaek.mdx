## Who was Gyebaek?

In 660, the allied forces of Silla and Tang invaded Baekje. Baekje had a general named Gyebaek. Silla's army numbered 50,000, but Gyebaek's force was only 5,000. He met the Silla army at Hwangsanbeol but was defeated and fell in battle. Gyebaek is remembered as the general who commanded the last battle of Baekje.

## Life and Achievements

Gyebaek (?–660) was a late-Baekje general who fell at the Battle of Hwangsanbeol against the Silla–Tang allies in 660. The dates and most of his life are unknown; only the battle itself is recorded, in the *Samguk Sagi*.

In 660, Silla joined Tang in a two-pronged invasion of Baekje: the Tang general Su Dingfang led 130,000 troops in from the west (the Geum River estuary), and Silla's Kim Yushin led 50,000 in from the east. Gyebaek led a "decided-to-die" force of 5,000 to block the Silla advance at Hwangsanbeol (today's Nonsan area in South Chungcheong).

Tradition tells a tragic anecdote that on the eve of battle Gyebaek killed his own wife and children, declaring, "Better to do it with my own hand than have them captured and made slaves." At Hwangsanbeol the Baekje force defeated the Silla army four times in succession, but the overwhelming numbers and the desperate charge of the Silla Hwarang Gwanchang inflamed the Silla soldiers; the battle was lost and Gyebaek fell. Hwangsanbeol was the last battle that sealed Baekje's fall, and Gyebaek became a figure who symbolizes Baekje's loyalty and final hour.

## Historical Assessment

Almost all that survives of Gyebaek is the entry on him in the *Yeoljeon* of the *Samguk Sagi*. Given that the work is Silla-centered, it is striking that it portrays an enemy general as a figure of loyalty and righteousness — a case of the Confucian ideology of loyalty applied across enemy lines, and a sign that Gyebaek's act was already perceived as remarkable in his own time.

The anecdote of his killing his family before battle has been disputed both for its historicity and for its ethics. One reading takes it as an extreme expression of devotion to the country; a critical contemporary reading sees it as violence against family. In the context of the source, the description may be a constructed military narrative declaring Gyebaek's resolve to expect no return.

The military significance of Hwangsanbeol is also notable. Repulsing Silla four times despite a 1:10 numerical disadvantage shows the qualitative level of Baekje's military and Gyebaek's tactical capability. What turned the tide was not strategy but the emotional resolve sparked among the soldiers by the Hwarang Gwanchang's sacrifice — analyzed as a case in which morale and psychology decided the outcome.

In modern nationalist historiography, Gyebaek has been repeatedly summoned as a model of the loyal subject. The fact that the king to whom his loyalty was directed (Uija) was already corrupt, and that his resistance ultimately could not prevent Baekje's collapse, exposes a tragic structure beneath the heroic narrative. Gyebaek can be read as a figure embodying the historical irony that arises between individual loyalty and a state's fate.
