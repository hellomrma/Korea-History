## Who was Kim Yushin?

Kim Yushin was a famous Silla general. Born into a royal house of the old kingdom of Gaya, he later became a Silla subject and fought for his country with all his strength.

From a young age he trained in swordsmanship, and tradition says he polished his skills alone in the mountains. Kim Yushin was the commanding general as Silla destroyed Baekje and Goguryeo and unified the Three Kingdoms. He lived to 79 and fought in many of Silla's wars.

## Life and Achievements

Kim Yushin (595–673) was Silla's greatest general and the leader of the wars of unification. He was descended from the royal family of Geumgwan Gaya. After Geumgwan Gaya was annexed by Silla in 532, his family was admitted into the True-Bone aristocracy, and Kim Yushin grew into the foremost soldier of his age despite that origin.

A former Hwarang, he became famous for rigorous martial training. After his first achievement at the Battle of Nangbiseong (629), he commanded battles large and small for decades. Politically he allied closely with Kim Chunchu (King Muyeol), supporting the formation of the Silla–Tang alliance. At the Battle of Hwangsanbeol (660) he broke Gyebaek's last stand and went on to capture Sabiseong, ending Baekje. In 668 he took part in the destruction of Goguryeo, completing the unification.

When Tang sought to rule the whole peninsula, the Silla–Tang War (670–676) followed. Despite his age, he supported the war and served as a moral anchor of the Silla army. After his death in 673, he was posthumously honored as King Heungmu, and his record opens the *Yeoljeon* (biographies) of the *Samguk Sagi*.

## Historical Assessment

Kim Yushin received the longest biographical treatment in Korean history; three volumes (41–43) of the *Samguk Sagi* are devoted to him. This reflects his historical weight but also calls for critical reading, as the work is shaped by Silla-centric historiography.

His ascent from the "non-mainstream" Gaya bloodline to the apex of True-Bone aristocracy reveals political acumen beyond his military gifts. His sister Munhui's marriage to Kim Chunchu symbolized a military-political alliance, and that bond became the structural basis for unification. The story of his sending his son Bangul and his nephew Gwanchang to their deaths at Hwangsanbeol (660) to rally the army shows the cold face of a commander — and may have been embellished into a hero narrative.

Debates over the meaning of unification persist. Critics charge that Silla brought in an outside power (Tang) to destroy fellow Korean states, Baekje and Goguryeo; defenders point to the successful expulsion of Tang and the integration of the people south of the Daedong River. About Kim Yushin's own role, scholars split between viewing him as a strategic agent and treating him as a military instrument of the Muyeol-line monarchy. Either way, his military and political weight in the East Asian realignment of the 7th century is undeniable.
