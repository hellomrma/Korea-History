## Who was Yun Bong-gil?

Yun Bong-gil was an independence fighter active during the Japanese colonial period.

On April 29, 1932, at a Japanese ceremony marking the emperor's birthday and a Japanese victory at Hongkou Park in Shanghai, Yun threw a bomb at the dais. The bombing killed Shirakawa Yoshinori, commander of the Japanese Shanghai Expeditionary Army, and wounded several other high-ranking figures.

Chiang Kai-shek of China's Nationalist Party is said to have remarked, "What a million Chinese troops could not do, one Korean youth has done." Yun was captured and executed in Japan in December of the same year, at the age of 24.

## Life and Achievements

Yun Bong-gil (1908–1932) was a young independence fighter from Yesan in South Chungcheong. He studied the Chinese classics from boyhood and led rural enlightenment work. With the resolve "Once a man leaves home, he does not return alive" (*Jangbu chulga saeng bul hwan*), he went to Shanghai and joined Kim Gu's Korean Patriotic Corps.

**The Hongkou Park action (April 29, 1932):** At a Japanese army event marking the emperor's birthday and the Japanese victory at Shanghai, Yun threw a bomb. The blast killed General Shirakawa, supreme commander of the Japanese Shanghai Expeditionary Army, and wounded many top Japanese figures including Lieutenant General Nomura, Lieutenant General Ueda, and Minister Shigemitsu.

**International repercussions:** Generalissimo Chiang Kai-shek of the Chinese Nationalist government praised the act, saying that what a million Chinese troops could not do, one Korean youth had accomplished. The action led the Chinese government to provide full support to the Korean Provisional Government, marking a new phase for the Korean independence movement. Yun was arrested on the spot, transferred to Japan, and martyred by firing squad in December of the same year.

## Historical Assessment

**Diplomatic and military effects:** The Hongkou Park action is read as more than mere armed resistance — it changed the geometry of international politics. With the Sino-Japanese conflict deepening after the Manchurian Incident of 1931, the action provided the decisive occasion for the Chinese Nationalist government to formally support the Korean Provisional Government. On the basis of subsequent Chinese financial and military support, the Provisional Government was later able to establish the Korean Liberation Army.

**Ethics of violence and independence struggle:** Yun's action also figures in ethical debate over "terrorism" versus "righteous deed." Under international law it was the bombing of a civilian event venue; another view reads it as resistance to an unlawful colonial situation. Beyond the binary of Korean veneration as righteous deed and the violent-act framing in some international-law writing, research now seeks complex interpretation within the context of colonial resistance.

**Memory and commemoration:** Memory of Yun Bong-gil is constructed differently in Korea and China. In Korea he is honored as a national hero; in China he is remembered as a symbol of anti-Japanese solidarity. A memorial hall to Yun stands in Hongkou Park (modern Lu Xun Park) — a symbolic site of Korea–China historical solidarity.
