## Who was Jeong Mongju?

Jeong Mongju lived in the very last days of Goryeo. At that time General Yi Seong-gye was preparing to found a new dynasty. Yi's son Yi Bang-won asked Jeong, "Come over to our side!" Jeong answered, "I will live for Goryeo and die for Goryeo." His sijo poem **Dansimga** ("Song of a Steadfast Heart") expresses this devotion. He was killed by Yi Bang-won's men on **Seonjukgyo Bridge**. To this day many remember him as a symbol of loyalty.

## Life and Achievements

Jeong Mongju (1337–1392) was a late-Goryeo civil official and Neo-Confucian scholar who left his name in history through unwavering loyalty to Goryeo.

He passed the state exam at the top in 1360 and was active in diplomacy, scholarship, and administration. He distinguished himself in particular as a diplomat, traveling as envoy to Japan to negotiate the *waegu* (pirate) problem and bringing back hundreds of abducted Koreans. He was also an important promoter of Neo-Confucianism in Goryeo.

After Yi Seong-gye seized power through the Wihwa-do Retreat (1388), Jeong stood at the center of moderate reformers who sought to keep the Goryeo dynasty while pushing internal reform. He cooperated with Yi yet opposed dynastic replacement — a difficult position. To Yi Bang-won's verse *Hayeoga* recruiting him, Jeong replied with *Dansimga*, declaring his loyalty to Goryeo.

In 1392, Jeong was killed at Seonjukgyo Bridge in Gaeseong by Jo Yeong-gyu and others sent by Yi Bang-won. After Joseon's founding he was paradoxically venerated as the very model of the loyal subject; even Taejong (Yi Bang-won) elevated him posthumously in recognition of his loyalty.

## Historical Assessment

Jeong Mongju (1337–1392) holds a firm place as Korea's icon of loyalty, but historiographically his political position invites more complex reading.

Jeong is often classified as a "moderate reformer." He agreed with Yi Seong-gye's faction on reforming Goryeo's old institutions but refused to the end the path of dynastic replacement. He thus stood opposed to the radical reformers Jeong Dojeon and Jo Jun. Some scholars stress his thinker's stature: not a mere dynastic loyalist but a Neo-Confucian who sought to defend a legal political order according to the *myeongbun* doctrine.

The *Dansimga* — "Though this body die a hundred deaths, my white bones become dust, and the soul be or be not — how could the single-hearted devotion to my lord ever fade?" — has been the subject of debate in scholarship over its actual authorship; it is currently treated as Jeong's work.

A striking paradox is that Yi Bang-won — the very man who had Jeong killed — later, as King Taejong, officially elevated Jeong as the model loyal subject. This is read as Joseon's use of Jeong's loyalty as moral capital for the new dynasty: by honoring his loyalty to Goryeo, Taejong could establish an ideological basis on which to demand equal loyalty from Joseon's officials.

The bridge's name is suggestive too. Originally Seonjigyo, it came to be called Seonjukgyo (good-bamboo bridge) after a tradition that a bamboo grew on the spot where Jeong fell — symbolic memory inscribed even in toponymy. Jeong was later enshrined in the National Confucian Shrine and reproduced through Joseon's official Confucian education as a continuing emblem of loyalty.
