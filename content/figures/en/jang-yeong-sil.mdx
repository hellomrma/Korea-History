## Who was Jang Yeong-sil?

Jang Yeong-sil was a Joseon-dynasty **scientist and engineer**, born around 1390.

He was originally of slave (*nobi*) status. The status barriers of the time made government office unthinkable for slaves — yet King Sejong recognized Jang's mechanical genius and conferred official rank on him.

Jang built astronomical and meteorological instruments — the **sundial (Angbu Ilgu), the water clock (Jagyeongnu), and the rain gauge (Cheugugi)**. Thanks to these, time and rainfall could be measured by standard, helping farmers plan their crops.

## Life and Achievements

Jang Yeong-sil (c. 1390 – 1450s) was a scientist of King Sejong's reign and a remarkable man who, despite slave origins, rose to the senior fifth rank.

**Sejong's promotion:** A government slave from Dongnae County, Jang's mechanical talents brought him to Sejong's attention. Sejong sent him to Ming China to absorb advanced techniques and then appointed him *Byeoljwa* of the Sangui Court (senior fifth rank).

**Major inventions:**
- **Jagyeongnu (1434):** A self-striking water clock that became the national standard timekeeper.
- **Angbu Ilgu (1434):** A hemispherical sundial that read both the hour and the seasons.
- **Cheugugi (1441):** A standardized rain gauge for nationwide measurement, contributing to the scientific administration of agriculture.
- **Honcheonui:** An armillary sphere used in calendrical calculations.

## Reassessment

**Beyond the status system:** Joseon's social order was Confucian, with status as the basic principle. Appointing a former government slave to the senior fifth rank was extraordinary — a product of Sejong's pragmatic view of talent. Sejong staffed major state projects (the Hall of Worthies, the Four Forts and Six Garrisons, the creation of Hangeul) by ability rather than status. But the change did not extend to the status system itself; Jang's appointment remained an exceptional case enabled only by royal favor.

**State-led science under Sejong:** The scientific advances of Sejong's age were not the work of lone inventors but a "modernization from above" in which the state set goals and concentrated resources. The rain gauge, the armillary sphere, and astronomical instruments were built for practical aims — improving agricultural productivity and reforming the calendar — and Jang functioned as the central technical talent.

**The rain gauge in world-historical context:** Built in 1441, the Cheugugi predates European rain measurement (1639) by some 200 years. With standardized vessels and units organized into a nationwide observation network, it is regarded as the world's first systematic rainfall-measurement system — a pioneering case of state meteorological administration.

**Disappearance after 1442:** After being beaten 80 strokes for the 1442 accident in which the king's palanquin (*anyeo*) was damaged, Jang vanishes from the record. The time, place, and cause of his death are unknown. Some scholars surmise execution or exile; others a natural retirement. With no surviving primary sources, certainty is impossible — a blank that itself reflects class bias in Joseon record-keeping.
