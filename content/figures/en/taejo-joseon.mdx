## Who was Taejo Yi Seong-gye?

Taejo Yi Seong-gye was a general of late Goryeo, famous as a great archer and a victor of many battles. As Goryeo society faltered, he chose to found a new dynasty. In 1392 he established **Joseon** and became its first king, Taejo. He moved the capital to Hanyang (modern Seoul) and laid the foundations of the new state. When his sons fought over the succession (the Strife of the Princes), he abdicated and retired to his hometown.

## Life and Achievements

Taejo Yi Seong-gye (1335–1408) was a late-Goryeo general and the founder of Joseon in 1392. Born in Hamgyeong Province, where he grew up between the Yuan sphere and Goryeo, he excelled in archery and tactics. His campaigns against the Jurchens, the Red Turbans, and the Japanese pirates made him Goryeo's leading general.

The decisive turning point was the **Wihwa-do Retreat** of 1388. Ordered to invade Liaodong in response to Ming's establishment of the Cheollyeong Commandery, he turned his army back at Wihwa-do and marched on the capital, Gaegyeong. Removing Choe Yeong, he seized power, allied with new Neo-Confucian gentry-scholars like Jeong Dojeon and Jo Jun, and pushed land reform (the *Gwajeonbeop*). In 1392 he deposed King Gongyang, took the throne, and renamed the realm Joseon, moving the capital to Hanyang in 1394.

Under Jeong Dojeon's direction, Gyeongbokgung was built, Confucian rule was established, and a new bureaucracy was set up — the institutional foundation of Joseon. But in 1398, the First Strife of the Princes broke out: Yi Bang-won killed Jeong Dojeon and his half-brothers. Heartbroken, Taejo abdicated in favor of his second son Jeongjong and withdrew to Hamheung. The "envoys to Hamheung" anecdote — that envoys sent by his son Taejong did not return — has come down as an emblem of the deep father–son rift.

## Historical Assessment

Assessment of Taejo Yi Seong-gye spans three dimensions: the legitimacy of the founding, the character of dynastic transition, and the complexity of personal identity.

On legitimacy, the Wihwa-do Retreat is traditionally narrated as the start of a "revolution of surnames" (*yeokseong hyeokmyeong*). Yi Seong-gye and Jeong Dojeon justified the coup by declaring that Goryeo had lost the Mandate of Heaven and that founding a Neo-Confucian state was the call of the age. Modern historiography, however, treats this justifying narrative as having been reproduced as the dynasty's regime ideology throughout Joseon, and asks us to distinguish actual historical process from after-the-fact justification.

His background is itself a question. His family rose amid complex relations with the Yuan, and Yi himself grew up in a hybrid cultural environment with the Jurchens. Some scholars note that his family's record of cooperation with Yuan affected his political standing before Wihwa-do. As post-founding narrative cast him as the founder of a Confucian state, this complex background tended to be marginalized.

Religion is also revealing. Personally, Yi was devoutly Buddhist; many records preserve his bond with the monk Muhak. The tension between Joseon's official adoption of Confucianism (and suppression of Buddhism) and the founding monarch's personal Buddhist faith is an interesting lens on early-Joseon religious policy.

After his death, his image was fixed within the framework of "sacred founding monarch" throughout Joseon. The royal-shrine rites and the institutionalized founding myth converted Yi from a historical figure into a dynastic symbol. Critically examining how faithfully that symbolic image reflects actual deeds and contexts is a task of modern historiography.
