## Who was Yun Dong-ju?

Yun Dong-ju was a poet of the Japanese colonial period.

He wrote poems of self-reflection under colonial rule. His best-known work, *Foreword* (Seosi), opens, "Until the day I die, may I look up to heaven without a single moment of shame…"

While studying in Japan in 1943, he was arrested as a "thought criminal" and imprisoned at Fukuoka. He died in prison on February 16, 1945, at the age of 28. He is one of the most widely read poets in Korea.

## Life and Achievements

Yun Dong-ju (1917–1945) was a poet born in Mingdong-chon, North Jiandao (Jilin Province, China). Born into a Christian family, he graduated from Yonhi College (today's Yonsei University) and built a poetic world of clarity and self-reflection under the suppression of colonial rule.

**Poetic world:** In *Foreword*, *The Night When I Count the Stars*, *Self-Portrait*, and *The Cross*, he expressed shame, self-reproach, resistance, and hope quietly and deeply. He is regarded as a poet of resistance who sang the suffering of the age through pure conscience and inward reflection rather than direct struggle.

**Arrest and martyrdom:** He studied at Rikkyo University in Tokyo in 1942 and then Doshisha University in Kyoto. In 1943 he was arrested with his cousin Song Mong-gyu on charges of independence activity and sentenced to two years' imprisonment. He died in Fukuoka Prison in February 1945 at the age of 28. After liberation, his posthumous collection *Sky, Wind, Stars and Poetry* was published and became a classic of modern Korean poetry.

## Historical Assessment

**Rediscovery as resistance poet:** Yun Dong-ju was scarcely known in his lifetime and was introduced to the public only through his posthumous collection, prefaced by Jeong Ji-yong, after liberation. Through the upheavals of the Korean War and the divided peninsula, his poetry was summoned as a symbol of national purity and the will to resist; especially during the democratization movements of the 1970s and 1980s, he became a spiritual support for the youth.

**Truth of his death in prison:** The cause of his death remains disputed. The Japanese authorities announced cerebral hemorrhage, but his family — beginning with his mother — and some researchers have raised the possibility of human experimentation. The historical fact that the medical school of Kyushu Imperial University carried out human experiments on prisoners during the war lends weight to such suspicions. Without decisive evidence, scholarship has not reached a definitive conclusion.

**A point of Korea–Japan cultural exchange:** Doshisha University, where Yun studied, has erected a stone memorial to his poetry on its campus and holds an annual memorial. Some Japanese citizens commemorate and study him — a rare case of cultural reflection on the complex history between the two countries. Yun is both a representative figure of modern Korean literature and a mediating presence for Korea–Japan historical reconciliation.
