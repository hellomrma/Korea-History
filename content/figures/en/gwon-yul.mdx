## Who was Gwon Yul?

Gwon Yul was a general of the Imjin War.

In 1593, Japanese forces attacked Haengju Sanseong fortress. They numbered around 23,000; Gwon Yul had only about 10,000 troops. Far outnumbered, he did not give up!

Women inside the fortress are said to have tied up their skirts and carried stones to help in the fight — the famous tale of the **haengjuchima** (the Haengju apron). Thanks to the people working together, the Japanese were soundly defeated. This is the **Battle of Haengju**.

Gwon Yul later became Supreme Commander of all Joseon land forces and worked with Admiral Yi Sun-sin to drive the Japanese out.

## Life and Achievements

Gwon Yul (1537–1599) was a mid-Joseon general who led the Battle of Haengju, one of the three great victories of the Imjin War.

**A late-blooming officer:** Gwon Yul was originally a civil official; he passed the *munkwa* exam only in 1582, at age 46, beginning his official career relatively late. Before the war he served mostly in administrative posts, but when the conflict erupted he rose rapidly as a military commander defending Jeolla Province.

**Battles of Ichi and Haengju:**
- **Ichi (July 1592):** First fame, blocking Japanese troops trying to push into Jeolla at Ichi (Wanju, North Jeolla).
- **Haengju (Feb. 1593):** With about 10,000 troops at Haengju Fortress, he repelled all nine assaults by some 23,000 Japanese under Kobayakawa Takakage. The defense made effective use of the *hwacha* (fire wagon) and *chongtong* cannons — a turning point in the war.

**As Supreme Commander:** After Haengju, appointed *Dowonsu*, Gwon commanded all Joseon land forces through the Imjin War and the Jeongyu Reinvasion, working closely with Yi Sun-sin's navy to block the Japanese inland advance.

**The *haengjuchima* legend:** The story that women carried stones in their tied-up skirts is a tradition that symbolizes popular determination, but its historicity is unconfirmed. Whether the word *haengjuchima* derives from this battle is also etymologically debated.

## Historical Assessment

Gwon Yul, the most prominent commander in the land battles of the Imjin War, is assessed primarily through the strategic and symbolic meaning of Haengju.

**Strategic significance of Haengju:** The February 1593 battle was more than a defensive engagement. The Joseon–Ming allied forces had retaken Pyongyang but had been pushed back at the Battle of Byeokje-gwan (Jan. 1593), and Ming troops were retreating. Gwon's victory at Haengju broke Japanese morale and pressed them to evacuate Hanseong — a psychological and strategic moment that directly influenced the negotiation phase and the recovery of Hanseong (April 1593).

**Mastery of firearms:** Gwon's organized use of *hwacha* and *singijeon* arrows at Haengju is striking. Considering that one reason Joseon regulars repeatedly lost in the field was inexperience with firearms, the battle taught the lesson of concentrating firepower at a defensive strongpoint.

**Limits and achievements as Supreme Commander:** As *Dowonsu*, his role draws mixed assessments. After Yi Sun-sin's dismissal, keeping Won Gyun within the chain of command but failing to prevent the disaster of Chilcheollyang (1597) is cited as a command failure. He is also credited with maintaining the land-army chain of command throughout the war and preventing the collapse of the Joseon military system.

**Etymology of *haengjuchima*:** Two derivations of *haengjuchima* coexist: from the Battle of Haengju, or from *haengju* (kitchen work). Korean linguistics tends to favor the latter, and the story may have been constructed later to preserve the memory of popular resistance.
