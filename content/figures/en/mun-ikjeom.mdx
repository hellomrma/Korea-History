## Who was Mun Ikjeom?

In the Goryeo era, ordinary people suffered terribly in winter — they had no warm cotton bedding. Mun Ikjeom, sent as an envoy to far-off Yuan China, hid cotton seeds in the cap of a writing brush and brought them home. From cotton, warm clothes and bedding could be made. With his father-in-law Jeong Cheonik, he worked hard to grow cotton — and the people of Goryeo could pass the winter warmly. A single seed changed everyone's life!

## Life and Achievements

Mun Ikjeom (1329–1398) was a late-Goryeo civil official who introduced cotton cultivation to Korea, transforming the clothing of common people. Sent as envoy to Yuan in 1363, he is said to have brought cotton seeds back hidden inside a writing-brush cap.

Most people in Goryeo wore hemp and ramie, cool in summer but offering little protection from winter cold. Cotton (*muyeong*, *myeonpo*), by contrast, retained heat well. Back home, Mun Ikjeom and his father-in-law Jeong Cheonik experimented with cotton cultivation. At first only one seed in ten survived, but they gradually succeeded and the crop spread nationwide. Jeong Cheonik later learned from a Yuan monk how to make a cotton gin and a spinning wheel, completing the manufacturing chain.

The diffusion of cotton became general from late Goryeo into early Joseon, and cotton cloth was so important throughout Joseon that it served almost as currency. Mun Ikjeom continued to be honored after the founding of Joseon and was enfeoffed as Buminhu (Marquis of Enriching the People).

## Historical Assessment

Mun Ikjeom (1329–1398) symbolizes a figure who transformed society by importing technology. Several historical points around his record, however, deserve attention.

Most contested is the smuggling of the seeds. Traditional records and folk tradition hold that Mun Ikjeom secretly brought back cotton seeds — Yuan contraband — hidden in a brush cap. The story is widely known as a symbol of devotion to the country. Some researchers note that it is hard to confirm whether Yuan strictly forbade the export of cotton seeds at the time, and raise the possibility that the smuggling tale was embellished and exaggerated in Joseon. Yuan and Goryeo were exchanging various goods, and there is no clear direct evidence that cotton was an absolute prohibited item.

There is also a perspective that re-examines his contribution from cultivation and technical completion. The view emphasizes that Jeong Cheonik's development and diffusion of weaving technology — gin and wheel — is what made actual cotton-cloth production possible, and that the heroization of Mun Ikjeom alone may simplify a collective process of technical development.

Even so, the social consequences of cotton's introduction can hardly be overstated. The spread of cotton cloth made warm clothing accessible to the masses and contributed to cotton's role in Joseon as a key commodity that doubled as tax and currency. Beyond a mere agricultural-technology transfer, the impact reaches across economic structure. A shrine to Mun Ikjeom and a memorial cotton field still stand in his hometown of Sancheong in South Gyeongsang.
