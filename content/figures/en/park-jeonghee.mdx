## Who was President Park Chung-hee?

Park Chung-hee was the president who led Korea during a time when the country was very poor.

In 1961 Park, a former soldier, became the country's leader. He built the Gyeongbu Expressway, founded a great steelworks at Pohang, and led the New Village Movement to modernize the countryside. In a short time the country became wealthy — what is now called the "Miracle on the Han."

But to hold power for a long time, Park took away many of the people's freedoms. In 1979 he was shot dead by one of his own subordinates.

## Life and Achievements

Park Chung-hee (1917–1979), born in Gumi in North Gyeongsang, was a soldier-turned-politician who served as the 5th through 9th president. A career officer, he graduated from the Manchurian Military Academy and the Japanese Military Academy and seized power in the May 16 coup of 1961.

**Economic development:** From 1962 he carried out successive Five-Year Economic Development Plans, choosing an export-led industrialization strategy. Funds secured through normalization with Japan (1965) underwrote the Gyeongbu Expressway (1970), POSCO (1973), and the Ulsan shipyards. The New Village Movement (1970) modernized the countryside. Per-capita GNP, around $80 at the start of his rule, rose to about $1,600 by his death — giving rise to the phrase "Miracle on the Han."

**The Yushin system and authoritarianism:** Promulgating the Yushin Constitution in 1972, he abolished direct presidential elections and laid an institutional foundation for indefinite rule. He used emergency decrees to suppress dissent and the Korean Central Intelligence Agency to pressure opposition forces. On October 26, 1979, he was shot by his KCIA director Kim Jae-gyu, ending 18 years of rule.

## Historical Assessment

**The "developmental dictatorship" debate:** Assessment of Park's era is among the sharpest historical debates in Korean society. Supporters cast him as a hero of industrialization; the democratization camp brands him a dictator. The analytical framework of "developmental dictatorship" is widely used, and debate continues over the causal link between authoritarian rule and high-speed growth — and whether growth could have come without dictatorship.

**Pro-Japanese record and legitimacy:** Park served as an officer in Manchukuo; some records describe him pledging loyalty in a blood oath. His pro-Japanese record is the central basis for criticism of his political legitimacy. Supporters counter that the limited choices available to Koreans of the time make a simple "pro-Japanese" labeling anachronistic.

**Pluses and minuses of the growth model:** The export-led industrialization of Park's era delivered fast results but, critics argue, deepened structural problems — a chaebol-centered economy, suppression of basic labor rights, and uneven regional development. The 1970 self-immolation of Jeon Tae-il symbolizes the shadow of growth. The lessons and cautions of the Park-style development model for late-developing economies remain a major subject of comparative political economy today.
