## Who was Ilyeon?

Ilyeon was a Buddhist monk of the Goryeo dynasty. In his time, Goryeo endured long Mongol invasions. Ilyeon gathered the old stories and legends of the peninsula and compiled them into a book — the *Samguk Yusa* ("Memorabilia of the Three Kingdoms"). It contains the Dangun myth, folk tales, and *hyangga* (vernacular songs). The book Ilyeon compiled is regarded as a key source for the study of Korean ancient history.

## Life and Achievements

Ilyeon (1206–1289) was a great late-Goryeo Buddhist monk and scholar who compiled the *Samguk Yusa*, a core source for Korean ancient history.

Ilyeon entered the monastic life at the age of nine and placed first in the monastic exam (*seunggwa*) in 1227. He cultivated and taught at various Seon (Zen) temples, building a great reputation. He continued his practice through the worst of the Mongol invasions and in 1283 was invested as *Gukjon* (National Master), recognized as the foremost Buddhist authority of his age. In his last years he stayed at Ingaksa Temple in Gunwi, North Gyeongsang, devoted to writing.

His most important achievement is the five-volume *Samguk Yusa*, completed around 1281. The work supplements material absent from Kim Bu-sik's contemporary *Samguk Sagi*. It includes the founding stories of Gojoseon — including the Dangun myth — Buddhist tales and the deeds of eminent monks, fourteen *hyangga* poems, and folk and regional legends. Where the *Samguk Sagi* followed the framework of orthodox history with Confucian rationalism, the *Samguk Yusa* drew on a Buddhist worldview to embrace folk oral tradition and legendary material.

Written when national identity was threatened by the Mongol invasions, the *Samguk Yusa* carries a historical intent of reaffirming the roots of Goryeo's national culture.

## Historical Assessment

Ilyeon (1206–1289) and his *Samguk Yusa* hold multi-layered importance in Korean ancient history, folklore, and literary history.

The source value stands out above all in complementary relation to the *Samguk Sagi*. Where that work (1145) deliberately excluded or rationalized myth and legend within the frame of orthodox Confucian history, Ilyeon preserved orally transmitted material while citing diverse sources — ancient inscriptions, temple records, regional traditions (*hyangjeon*). For ancient-history reconstruction, the *Samguk Yusa* therefore functions as an irreplaceable primary source.

The recording of the Dangun myth is itself an important point. Ilyeon's account, citing the *Wei Shu* and the *Gogi*, became the core text of later Korean discourse on the national founder. After the late Goryeo and early Joseon, the Dangun myth was reinforced as a symbol of national identity; without Ilyeon's commitment of it to writing, it is uncertain whether the myth would have come down in its present form.

Two interpretations of Ilyeon's motive coexist. One is the nationalist reading — a sense of cultural-preservation mission arising from the national crisis of the Mongol period. The other is religious-institutional — propagating Buddhism and asserting the historical authority of Buddhist temples. Much of the *Samguk Yusa* is in fact taken up with miracles of eminent monks and *yeongi* (origin) tales of temples, lending support to the second reading.

By preserving 14 *hyangga* poems, the *Samguk Yusa* is also the unique source for early Korean literature. Songs such as the *Cheoyongga* and the *Chan Gippa-rang ga* survive only through it and are essential for the study of ancient Korean and early Korean poetry.
