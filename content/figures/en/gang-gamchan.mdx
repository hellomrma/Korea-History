## Who was Gang Gamchan?

Long ago in Goryeo there was a general named Gang Gamchan. He was originally a civil official who had passed the state exam, but a great crisis came: Khitan armies, 100,000 strong, invaded. Gang Gamchan was already past 70. He led the Goryeo army and met the Khitan at a place called Gwiju, and won. This battle is called the **Battle of Gwiju**. The victory ended the Khitan invasions, and Goryeo's territory was saved.

## Life and Achievements

Gang Gamchan (948–1031) was a Goryeo civil official and general who led the victory at the Battle of Gwiju. He passed the literary exam and served principally in administrative posts. But when the Khitan (Liao) invaded with 100,000 troops under Xiao Paiya in 1018, Gang Gamchan — well past 70 — was appointed Supreme Commander.

At Heunghwajin he dammed a river and broke the dam as the enemy crossed, inflicting heavy losses. In 1019 at Gwiju (modern Guseong, North Pyongan), he encircled the retreating Khitan army and all but annihilated it — the famous **Battle of Gwiju**. Of the 100,000 Khitan, only some thousands are said to have returned alive.

The result was that the Khitan no longer threatened Goryeo, and Goryeo's territory — including the Six Garrisons east of the Yalu — was preserved intact. The battle ranks with Eulji Mundeok's Salsu and Yi Sun-sin's Hansan-do as one of the three great victories of Korean history.

## Historical Assessment

Gang Gamchan (948–1031) passed the exam under King Seongjong and grew into a civil official; his military activity is concentrated in the latter part of his life. Scholars note this case of a Confucian civil official commanding an army in a national crisis as a striking exception to the typical commander archetype.

The *Goryeosa* and the *Goryeosa Jeoryo* record Gwiju's strategic features in some detail. The water attack at Heunghwajin is praised as a creative use of terrain, and the encirclement at Gwiju is read as a precise reading of contemporary Khitan tactical doctrine. Some scholars caution that the figures for Khitan losses may have been exaggerated in later records.

Historically, Gwiju is more than a single military victory. After the first invasion of 993 and the second of 1010, Goryeo had endured diplomatic pressure and military threat; Gwiju was the moment when relations with the Khitan were reset on terms favorable to Goryeo. Direct military provocation against Goryeo ceased, and a relatively peaceful relationship took shape.

The popular deification of Gang is also worth noting. Tales — that the *Munhok* star fell from the sky at his birth, that he drove foxes from Gaegyeong — spread widely through the Joseon period. They are a precipitate of collective memory about a hero who saved the nation from invasion. Naksungdae in Gwanak District, Seoul, is held to be his birthplace; today it is a park with a monument and statue.
