## Who was Yu Gwan-sun?

Yu Gwan-sun was born in Cheonan, South Chungcheong Province, in 1902, into a family active in the independence movement.

She was 16 and studying at Ewha Haktang in Seoul when she joined the **March 1st Movement** of 1919 — the great nationwide rally where people shouted "Long live Korean independence!"

When the Government-General closed the schools, she returned home and prepared a rally in Cheonan. On April 1, 1919, thousands gathered at the **Aunae market** for a manse demonstration. Japanese gendarmes opened fire, and 19 people — including her parents — were killed on the spot.

Yu was arrested and imprisoned at **Seodaemun Prison** in Seoul. Even there she continued to lead manse cries and was tortured. On September 28, 1920, at 18, she died in prison. 🌸

## Life and Independence Activity

Yu Gwan-sun (1902–1920) joined the March 1st Movement and led the manse rally at Aunae market. She is remembered as a symbol of national resistance under colonial rule.

**Background:** Born in 1902 in Cheonan into a Christian family. Both her father, Yu Jung-gwon, and her mother, Yi So-je, were active in the independence movement. She was admitted to Ewha Haktang, where her education sharpened her national consciousness.

**Participation in the March 1st Movement:** She took part in the March 1, 1919 demonstrations in Seoul with her fellow Ewha students. After the Government-General ordered the schools closed, she returned home and began organizing her own manse rally.

**The Aunae market rally (April 1, 1919):** She traveled the surrounding villages to spread word of the rally and led a manse demonstration of thousands at the market on the lunar March 1 market day. Japanese gendarmes opened fire and 19 people, including her parents, were killed on the spot.

**Imprisonment and martyrdom:** Sentenced to five years' imprisonment by the Gongju District Court, Yu was held at Seodaemun Prison. While imprisoned she led a prison manse rally on March 1, 1920, and was severely tortured. She died in prison on September 28, 1920, at the age of 18.

## Memory and the Making of a Heroine

**Limits of contemporaneous records:** Contemporary records about Yu Gwan-sun are very limited. Court records, newspaper reports, and Ewha-related materials are the main sources, and parts of her concrete biography were filled in by later testimony. This makes historical criticism necessary about which elements of the "Yu Gwan-sun narrative" are historical fact and which were constructed afterward.

**Construction of the heroic narrative:** Yu's elevation as the "older sister of the nation" and "symbol of the March 1st Movement" took shape after liberation, especially after the founding of the Republic of Korea in 1948. The Rhee government and Ewha Haktang (precursor of Ewha Womans University) actively publicized her story, shaping and strengthening the narrative — within the context of nation-building's need for national heroes.

**Women's roles in the March 1st Movement:** Yu represents the many women independence activists who took part in the movement. Female students, *gisaeng*, and rural women participated widely in the manse rallies, yet their roles were not fully illuminated in history. Her symbolic standing also serves as a collective memory representing them.

**History of the Order of Merit award:** Yu was posthumously awarded the third-class Order of Merit for National Foundation in 1962; criticism persisted that the rank was low relative to her contributions. In 2019, on the centenary of the March 1st Movement, the award was upgraded to the first-class Grand Order. The process illustrates how assessments of merit can shift with the times and social awareness.
