## Who was Yi Hwang?

Yi Hwang was one of the most renowned scholars of Joseon. He is often called by his pen name **Toegye**. Born in Andong, Gyeongsang Province, he loved learning and teaching all his life.

The **Dosan Seowon** academy he founded still stands in Andong. Many students came there to study and debate. His teachings reached beyond Korea — they had a great influence on Edo-period scholars in Japan as well. His face appears on Korea's 1,000-won banknote (the 1,000-won note actually depicts Yi Hwang).

## Life and Achievements

Yi Hwang (1501–1570) was a mid-Joseon Neo-Confucian scholar; his pen name was Toegye. Born in Andong in North Gyeongsang, he passed the *munkwa* exam at 34 and entered office, but repeatedly declined posts to devote himself to scholarship.

Yi Hwang deepened and developed Zhu Xi's Neo-Confucianism for the Joseon context. In *iki* theory, he developed a dualism distinguishing *i* (principle) and *gi* (material force). For him *i* was moral principle and *gi* was material force, and he held that *i* could actively govern *gi* — the doctrine of *ibal* (issuance of *i*). This grew into the famous *Sadan Chiljeong* (Four Beginnings, Seven Feelings) debate with Gi Daeseung, a core question of Joseon Neo-Confucianism.

In 1561 he founded Dosan Seowon in his hometown of Andong to teach disciples. The *Seonghak Sipdo* he prepared for King Seonjo presents the core concepts of Neo-Confucianism in ten diagrams for the cultivation of the sovereign. His vast scholarly discussions are gathered in the *Toegye Jip*.

His school formed the Yeongnam school centered in Gyeongsang and deeply influenced Japan's Edo-period Confucianism.

## Historical Assessment

Yi Hwang (1501–1570) is regarded as the thinker who best shows the originality of Joseon Confucianism within East Asian Neo-Confucian history. His scholarship went beyond reception of Zhu Xi to creative reinterpretation, and played a central role in shaping subsequent Korean and Japanese Confucianism.

**Philosophical meaning of *iki* dualism and the Four–Seven debate.** Yi Hwang developed Zhu Xi's *iki* theory in the direction of stressing the activity of *i*. He argued that the Four Beginnings (the seeds of humaneness, righteousness, ritual propriety, and wisdom) issue from *i*, while the Seven Feelings (joy, anger, sorrow, fear, love, hate, desire) issue from *gi* — the doctrine of "mutual issuance of *i* and *gi*." Gi Daeseung countered that *i* and *gi* cannot be separated, so the Four Beginnings cannot be unrelated to *gi*. The eight-year debate is taken as evidence that Joseon Neo-Confucianism was not mere imitation of Zhu Xi but achieved an autonomous philosophical accomplishment.

**The Yeongnam school and its legacy.** Yi Hwang's lineage passed through Ryu Seong-ryong and Kim Seong-il into the Yeongnam school, which through late Joseon formed the cultural and political identity of the Gyeongsang region. The opposition between the Yeongnam school and the Giho school of Yulgok Yi Yi became the scholarly background of the late-Joseon faction politics of Namin and Seoin.

**Influence on Japanese Confucianism.** After the Imjin War, Yi Hwang's thought spread to Japan and decisively shaped Edo Confucianism. Pioneers such as Fujiwara Seika and Yamazaki Ansai studied his works deeply, and Yi Hwang continued to be highly regarded in Japan after the Meiji Restoration. He was called "the Confucius of Korea" by Japanese scholars — a measure of the pan–East Asian reach of his thought.

In contemporary philosophy, scholarship is active in reassessing Yi Hwang's *iki* theory as moral metaphysics. Cultural-historical assessment also notes that his ethic of office (when to serve and when to withdraw) and his educational practice centered on Dosan Seowon shaped the very type of late-Joseon *sarim* (literati) culture.
