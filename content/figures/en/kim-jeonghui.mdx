## Who was Kim Jeong-hui?

Kim Jeong-hui was a calligrapher and scholar of Joseon. He was born in 1786. His pen name was **Chusa**.

The script he wrote has such a distinctive style that it is called **Chusache**. People still study Chusa style today.

He devoted himself to *geumseokhak* — the study of inscriptions on stone and metal — and proved by careful analysis that a stone on Mount Bukhan was a "tour stele" of King Jinheung of Silla. Even when exiled to Jeju, he continued his scholarship and art, leaving behind the painting *Sehando*.

## Life and Achievements

Kim Jeong-hui (1786–1856) was a great late-Joseon scholar and artist, better known by his pen name **Chusa**.

**Creating Chusache:** After deep study of Chinese ancient inscriptions and scripts, Kim Jeong-hui completed his **Chusache** style — characterized by intense contrast between thick and thin strokes. It was not imitation but the original product of decades of research and practice.

**Epigraphy (geumseokhak):** He left outstanding work in *geumseokhak*, the study of inscriptions on metal and stone. A representative achievement is identifying, by careful textual analysis, that a stone on Mount Bukhan was the tour stele of King Jinheung of Silla.

**Jeju exile and *Sehando*:** From 1840 he was exiled to Jeju for eight years amid conflict with the Andong Kim faction. During his exile, when his disciple Yi Sang-jeok brought him rare books from Beijing, he painted **Sehando** — a pine and a hut — to express his gratitude. *Sehando* is now designated a National Treasure.

**Scholarly exchange with Qing:** He visited Beijing and built deep ties with Qing scholars such as Weng Fanggang and Ruan Yuan, absorbing the methodology of evidential scholarship (*kaozheng*).

## Historical Assessment

Kim Jeong-hui is taking his place not as an artist alone but as a central figure in East Asian intellectual exchange and late-Joseon intellectual history.

**Aesthetic of Chusache:** Chusache departed boldly from traditional calligraphic norms, taking deliberate imbalance and break-pattern as aesthetic principles. Contemporary East Asian artists, including Japan's Hirose Daikō, prized it; modern art history sometimes reads Chusache as an "expressionist" turn in calligraphy. Some researchers debate whether the breaks were intentional or partly the result of physical decline during exile.

**Epigraphy in scholarly history:** Kim Jeong-hui's epigraphy transplanted Qing evidential methodology — textual criticism and empirical verification — into Joseon. The reading of the Bukhansan stele was not a mere discovery but a Joseon application of a modern historical methodology that cross-checks texts and inscriptions.

**Layered reading of *Sehando*:** On the surface a painting of gratitude to a disciple, *Sehando* visualizes the *Analects* line "Only when the cold season comes do you know that the pine and the cypress are the last to wither" — read as an expression of the gentleman's integrity preserved through political exclusion. Famously, the painting was taken to Japan during the colonial period and was later returned through the persuasion of Son Jae-hyeong.

**Exile and creation:** Kim experienced exile in Jeju (1840–48) and Bukcheong in Hamgyeong (1851–52). With Chusache's completion overlapping his exiles, some readings hold that isolation and suffering enabled artistic deepening. With Jeong Yak-yong's exile writings, this is studied under the theme of late-Joseon "exile culture" of intellectuals.
