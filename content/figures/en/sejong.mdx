## Who was King Sejong the Great?

King Sejong was the fourth king of Joseon. He was born in 1397 and died in 1450.

The most famous of his achievements is **the creation of Hangeul**! Until then Joseon had used Chinese characters (Hanja), which were very hard to learn. Sejong thought, "It would be wonderful if all my people could read and write easily!" — and in 1443 he created **Hunminjeongeum**.

He also drew on craftsmen like Jang Yeong-sil to build sundials, water clocks, and even an instrument to measure rainfall.

## Life and Achievements

Sejong (1397–1450) was the fourth king of Joseon and the third son of King Taejong.

**Creation of Hunminjeongeum (1443):** Educated elites used Chinese characters, but ordinary people could not write at all. With scholars of the Hall of Worthies (*Jiphyeonjeon*), Sejong analyzed Korean phonology in fine detail and created the 28-letter Hunminjeongeum.

**Promotion of science and technology:** Despite Jang Yeong-sil's low-born status, Sejong appointed him to office and had him invent the Angbu sundial, the Jagyeongnu (self-striking water clock), and the rain gauge.

**Territorial expansion:** Through Choe Yun-deok and Kim Jongseo he opened the Four Forts and Six Garrisons, fixing the borders at the Yalu and Tumen Rivers.

## Sejong's Statecraft and Reassessment

**Role of the Hall of Worthies:** Sejong ran the Jiphyeonjeon not as a mere research bureau but as a core institution for state advisement and cultural development.

**Resistance to Hunminjeongeum:** Choe Mal-li and other scholars of the Jiphyeonjeon submitted memorials opposing the new alphabet. The reasons were a Sino-centric anxiety about departing from the Chinese cultural sphere and an anxiety over losing entrenched privilege.

**Limits and breakthroughs of the status system:** Jang Yeong-sil rose to the senior third rank despite his origin as an *gwan-no* (government slave) — an extraordinary breakthrough in a Confucian status society, made possible by Sejong's bold appointment.

**Critical perspectives:** Sejong's reign is generally praised, but some scholars note that he inherited the stable foundation his father Taejong secured through political violence.
