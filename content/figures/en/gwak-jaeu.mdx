## Who was Gwak Jae-u?

Gwak Jae-u was a courageous man who rose on his own to save the country during the Imjin War.

When Japan invaded in 1592, he did not wait for orders from the government. In his hometown of Uiryeong in South Gyeongsang, he poured out his own wealth to raise troops — the first **righteous army** (*uibyeong*) of Korea was born!

He always fought wearing **red robes**, so people called him the **Red Robe General** (*Hong-ui Janggun*). Around the Nakdong River he appeared and vanished, harassing the Japanese with guerrilla tactics.

After the war, he refused high office and lived quietly in nature. He showed by example that "when the country is in danger, the people themselves must rise."

## Life and Achievements

Gwak Jae-u (1552–1617) was a representative righteous-army leader who raised troops in Gyeongsang Province during the Imjin War. Famous for his red robes, he was called the **Red Robe General**.

**Raising the righteous army:** When the war began in April 1592, Gwak Jae-u organized a militia in Uiryeong (South Gyeongsang) on April 22, donating his entire fortune. It is recorded as the very first righteous-army uprising in the country, becoming a symbol of voluntary resistance as the regular army collapsed.

**Guerrilla war on the Nakdong:** Operating from the Nakdong River basin and the Jeongam crossing, he conducted highly mobile guerrilla operations. By blocking the Japanese route into Jeolla, he contributed greatly to defending the country's grain belt. His red robes raised allied morale and pressed the enemy psychologically.

**The Gyeongsang defense network:** With Jeong In-hong and other righteous-army leaders, he built a defense network across Gyeongsang. Blocking the Japanese from spilling west across the Nakdong, his troops played an important role on the war's whole front.

**Withdrawal after the war:** After the war he refused office and withdrew to the Hwawangsan area in Changnyeong, South Gyeongsang. Later generations praise this stance — valuing righteousness over power. He died in 1617 at the age of 66.

## Historical Assessment

Gwak Jae-u stands as a symbol of the Imjin War righteous-army movement, his significance going beyond military merit into the structures of Joseon society and the agency of the *sajok* (local elite).

**Character of the righteous armies — voluntarism and the *sajok* order:** Often narrated as a "spontaneous popular uprising," Gwak Jae-u's mobilization can also be read as a movement by regional *sajok* (local *yangban*) using their wealth and networks to defend the local order. The *Veritable Records of Seonjo* records that he raised soldiers with his own property, and many of those who joined were *sajok* sharing his social network. This is used to qualify readings of Imjin righteous armies purely as "popular resistance."

**Strategic value of the Nakdong defense:** Militarily, his defense of the Jeongam crossing is important because it cut the Japanese route into Jeolla. Given that securing Jeolla's grain and resources was among Japan's first objectives, the defense's success helped sustain Joseon's long-war capacity. Some scholars rate it, alongside Gwon Yul's Battle of Ichi, as a joint contribution to preserving Jeolla.

**Meaning of his withdrawal:** After the war he refused multiple summons. The choice is read as composite — disappointment with Seonjo's court, distaste for intensifying factional conflict, or commitment to the traditional Confucian principle of office and withdrawal. The withdrawal helped consolidate the image of "a righteous man who does not chase utility" and made him an exemplar of pure loyalty in later commemorative discourse.

**Memory and commemoration:** Through late Joseon and the colonial period, Gwak Jae-u was actively summoned as a symbol of national resistance. Some note that the process simplified a *sajok*-led righteous-army movement into a popular movement. A righteous-army memorial hall stands today in Uiryeong honoring him.
